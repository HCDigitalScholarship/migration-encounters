Anne:        Why you went.

Jose:        Okay, well. For starters, thank you for listening to me. The main reason why we went is, according to my mom, it was to have a better life. Have some experience on knowing different ways of living, different languages, stuff like this.

Jose:        We did, to arrive to the U.S. when I was about 10 years old and we did arrive to the U.S. illegally. I remember that up to a certain point on the road, we had to split from my sister. She was, if I remember properly, she was somewhere around about six to seven years old. She was barely starting in elementary and I remember that I had to walk with my mom.

Jose:        During the beginning of the walk, I remember that we were a considerable... We were about seven or eight in the group and barely starting. We got mugged by, I don't know how you may call it, but these people just came out of nowhere and everything that we had, we had it removed from us.

Jose:        From there, we pretty much had to keep on walking, we walked about a day and a half because I remember that we started let's say a Friday and we arrived to the U.S. somewhere around Monday morning. So, we had to walk a considerable amount of time. I do remember that.

Jose:        I remember that at the beginning when I started, I hated everything. I do remember that. I remember that for example food tasted funny. I remember that for example, the language since I couldn't understand anything at the beginning, it was really frustrating because I wanted to say something and no one would understand it and yeah, it was kind of complicated.

Jose:        For example, I do remember this one time that when I was barely catching up to the language, we were living on a house—we were renting a room in a house and these kids, they used to always bother my sister. So, I took to defend her as much as I could. And obviously there was some discrimination regarding, like, offensive language and stuff like this. But obviously since I couldn't reply in English, I would normally say in Spanish. So, they would always say that they didn't understand until I started to get the language.

Jose:        I mainly started to learn the language because of the offensive words. Not because one would want to say them, but it's so that one would know when they're being cursed at. Yeah. (chuckle) It sounds funny now but-

Anne:        A little backwards but, yeah. Seriously. Yeah.

Jose:        Yeah. At least that's why I learned it.

Anne:        Uh-huh (affirmative).

I do remember that at the beginning, for example, Hispanics, well, the worst racism that I pretty much can think of is when it comes from your own race. And I do remember that Hispanics, especially for example other Mexicans that were born there or other Mexicans that have lived there for a considerable amount of time, they were way, considerably more racist to me than any other Caucasian or African American or any other ethnicity that was in mind, which sucked. Because at the end of the day, I felt considerably alone there.

Jose:        I do remember there was this one kid. He was my first friend there. He was like, "Hey, did you just barely arrived?" I'm like, "Yeah." "Do you speak English?" You're kind of reminding me of her.

Jose:        But it was understandable and well, that was my elementary.

Jose:        And that's where the point of being discriminated by your own race comes because, for example, the other academical teams that didn't have language barriers, they would say that we were stupid because we didn't know the language or they would start discriminating because of this same fact. Letting us know that they were better than us in some dumb way.

Jose:        I do remember that, for example, sometimes coming back from middle school, I used to hate it so much that once I remember that I kicked everyone out of my room and I just closed the door and I started crying because I hated it so much, I didn't want to be there.

Jose:        And I do remember that I blamed my mom so much because she took me to a place where I didn't want to be. She forced me to do something that I didn't want to do. And I do remember my mom regretting it or telling me that she was sorry and telling me that.

Jose:        I didn't see it at the moment, but eventually I will start to see what she wanted for us to see or the reason of everything. Things that I saw for granted, that I didn't pretty much wasn't aware of.

Like for example in our case was to learn about the culture itself, the language. Have a different mind, open, and we just went along. At least the way that I see it now, it is the worst thing that you can do because you never learn anything. You just stay in some random cycle of self-destruction which I really don't... I don't know, I think back and I literally regret every single opportunity that I was offered. I literally do regret it.
