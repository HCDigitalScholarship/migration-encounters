Francisco

Interviewee

Sergio Diaz

Interviewer

Mexico City, Mexico

June 4, 2019

Sergio:        All right. This is the interview with Francisco S. Francisco, the first question I have is, why did your family leave from Mexico to the United States in the first place?

Francisco:        My family left to the States because they thought it was a better opportunity for me growing up. That's why they left to the States.

Sergio:        Was your hometown violent at all?

Francisco:        No, my hometown wasn't violent at all. It was normal, and it's always been normal.

Sergio:        That's Mexico City?

Francisco:        Yeah, that's correct.

Sergio:        How old were you when you first went to the United States?

Francisco:        I was, I would say, 8 years old when I left to the States.

Sergio:        Eight years?

Francisco:        Yes.

Sergio:        Do you remember your first day in the United States?

Francisco:        I do remember my first time in the States. I do remember, because when I got out of the plane, the heat. The heat was really, really, really hot in there. It was choking me. Yeah, and everything's different. The people are different, the culture is different, everything's different. Yeah, I do remember. It was really, really hot.

Sergio:        What's one of your first good memories of the US?

Francisco:        One of my first good memories is when I went to school. The school over there is different. A bus goes and picks you up to your house, and then after that you get to eat breakfast. You get to play around, you get to eat again, and then they take you back home. Actually, that's one of the first good memories that I have from out there.

Sergio:        That was different from Mexico?

Francisco:        Yes. Yeah, from Mexico, you've got to go to school. There's no bus, there's no cafeteria either. Yeah, it's way, way different.

Sergio:        Do you have any teachers or people that you remember from school?

Francisco:        I do remember teachers and people from school. I don't talk to them no more, but I do remember some.

Sergio:        What are some of the memories you have of them?

Francisco:        We used to have this teacher in high school. He was into things, like outdoors and hiking. We actually went to the mountains to hike with him. That's why I remember him.

Sergio:        You went to mountains to hike with the teacher?

Francisco:        Mm-hmm (affirmative), yeah.

Sergio:        What was that like?

Francisco:        It was actually nice, because the mountains, they're not far. It's probably an hour to get up there. Then once you get up there, you can see the whole city. It was really nice.

Sergio:        How did that happen, that you got to go hiking with your teacher?

Francisco:        I like to do things outside from school, and he liked to do things, so we got together and we went. It was a group. It was just me. It was just a group, and yeah, we went.

Sergio:        Did you ever work in the US?

Francisco:        Yes, I did work in the US, doing furniture.

Sergio:        Furniture? When did you start working?

Francisco:        I started working when I was 16 years old. I used to work when I was off school, on vacation. Then when I finished high school, I started working full-time.

Sergio:        During your high school experience, you went to the United States on a visa.

Francisco:        Yes.

Sergio:        Your family overstayed it?

Francisco:        Yes.

Sergio:        Did you ever think about your status while going through school? Was that ever on your mind?

Francisco:        It wasn't in my mind when I was through school. When I finished and I wanted to get a job, that's when it started getting into my mind.

Sergio:        How did it affect you?

Francisco:        Well, I couldn't get a job anywhere. If I wanted to get a job, I had to go do it illegally, so I didn't have as much opportunity as other people my age.

Sergio:        How did you end up getting the job at the furniture store?

Francisco:        My dad used to own it, so I started working for him.

Sergio:        Your dad owned a furniture store?

Francisco:        Yes.

Sergio:        What was that like? Was he ever afraid of ...

Francisco:        No. Well, we were actually living right here, without pressure, without anything. The only difference is you didn't have services like regular people. Let's say if you were sick, you had to stick with it. You couldn't go to the hospital, because you didn't have insurance, but it was just normal.

Sergio:        Did you ever feel like that was unfair?

Francisco:        It was, but we were breaking the law, so actually it wasn't. To me, I didn't care. Yeah, it wasn't unfair, because we were there illegally.

Sergio:        What's your least favorite thing about the US, or your worst memory?

Francisco:        My worst memory from the States is that I couldn't travel, probably. Yeah. Let's say that I wanted to go to Spain. I couldn't go, because if I left, I wouldn't be able to come back, so that was probably the worst thing.

Sergio:        When did you want to travel? When was it that you wanted to travel somewhere?

Francisco:        I've been traveling all over the world. Not the world, but I did travel in the States. I know a lot of cities from there, and then I got back here in Mexico. I also know a lot of states, too. I've been going around.

Sergio:        How did you end up back in Mexico?

Francisco:        Well, they sent me back. It was a voluntary departure, so that's why I'm here. That's why I'm back.

Sergio:        You said you were driving, and you had a ...

Francisco:        Yeah. Well, actually, I was at a party and I drank too much. I took off. I wasn't feeling right, so I stopped my car and fell asleep. A cop saw me, and since I had my keys in the ignition, they said I was driving, so they took me and gave me a DUI.

Sergio:        They gave you a DUI. How many days did you spend in jail?

Francisco:        None. Actually, probably a few hours, but then after I talked to the judge, immigration went and got me.

Sergio:        What judge did you talk to?

Francisco:        I talked to a city judge from _____. He was giving me my charges, and he told me that he was going to let me go, that I had to go back to the court date to face my charges, but I never got to it because immigration got me.

Sergio:        When did they get you?

Francisco:        They got me when the police released me. That's when immigration got me. They just passed us through. You get released, so they release you, but since you're still in custody, immigration goes and picks you up, because they know you're illegal.

Sergio:        It was like a handoff?

Francisco:        Yes.

Sergio:        They turned you over to ICE?

Francisco:        Mm-hmm (affirmative), that's correct.

Sergio:        Then what happened?

Francisco:        They took me to the immigration center, detention center, and then I spent there nine months fighting my case, trying to stay legal. Then at the end, I didn't win, I lost, so they sent me back.

Sergio:        Nine months in detention, that's a while.

Francisco:        Yes. I was actually trying to do it the right way.

Sergio:        What's the conditions of detention like?

Francisco:        It's actually bad. They actually treat you like a criminal. You kinda are like a criminal. It's detention and jail, they're completely different. You can…so yeah, it's normal, I guess you can say. They don't mistreat you or anything. It's just you're locked up. Your freedom has been taken away, and that's something that you don't know how to cope with. Everything to you looks bad, but I would say it's okay.

Sergio:        What kind of things were bad in your eyes?

Francisco:        Huh?

Sergio:        What was bad in your eyes?

Francisco:        Getting locked up for not being there illegal. Staying there for so long, that was the hard part.

Sergio:        Did anything happen to you that you thought was unfair, you thought was violating your rights?

Francisco:        The only thing that I think was unfair is the judge. He had his decision made before he even saw me, and then he made me wait so much time, not to do anything. I would rather have him be like, "Hey, you know why you're not going to apply for anything? You're not going to apply for anything because you don't qualify for anything. You might as well just leave." To me, that was just the hardest part, to be there trying to do it the right way, making it the right way, just so they can tell me no, wasting so much time in there. That was the hard part.

Sergio:        How old were you when you got deported?

Francisco:        I was 28.

Sergio:        28? So before that, you had a different misdemeanor that wouldn't qualify you for DACA?

Francisco:        Yes. It was another DUI, so this was my second one.

Sergio:        This was your second DUI?

Francisco:        Yeah. The other one was 10 years ago, but yeah, the judge decided the second one he'd throw. The other one was-

Sergio:        You were 18?

Francisco:        Mm-hmm (affirmative).

Sergio:        You were under-age drinking too?

Francisco:        No, no. I only pled guilty for driving under the influence. They dropped the other charges.

Sergio:        Wow. What was your experience like working in the US, and just going to work, coming home?

Francisco:        Actually, I did like it. I used to like my job because I used to be all over the place, working out of town, going to different states, going to different buildings. It was actually good. That's why I liked it.

Sergio:        What was the favorite part of the US that you visited?

Francisco:        The favorite one? I will have to say Florida. It was really nice. They've got Disney World in Orlando. They've got Disney World and then Universal Studios. It was actually nice out there. That, and then Texas, but no, it was better over there in Florida, because they've got the beach.

Sergio:        That's crazy.

Francisco:        I've been all over the place, dude. Well, [inaudible 00:11:55] New York. I've been all over the place. I got here last May, and I've been to a couple of states.

Sergio:        Was there ever a moment when you were in the States that you felt American?

Francisco:        Well, yeah, most of the time. You know you're not American, but you feel like one. The only difference is that you can’t go out of the country, and that's it, and you don't get any paid benefits.

Sergio:        I see you have a Cowboys hat on. Are you a fan?

Francisco:        Yeah. I was a fan, a Cowboys fan. Actually, I went to the stadium, which is nice, big TV.

Sergio:        Was it weird to go to the stadium and then have so many people around you?

Francisco:        No. Since I was living there, since I was a little kid, I wasn't afraid, even talking Spanish, talking Spanish with the cops or anything. There's no racial profiling like they make it seem on the news. It's not like that, or maybe I've got the luck of not having that.

Sergio:        What was your return in Mexico like when you came back?

Francisco:        It was actually nice, but it's a lot different than the States, a lot different.

Sergio:        How?

Francisco:        Just the way the people are, how they live, everything. It's just another world.

Sergio:        Was transitioning over hard for you?

Francisco:        No, actually I did like it. I liked the food. I liked everything. It was actually easy. Since I was leaving there I was always speaking Spanish, so that wasn't even an issue either. It's been okay.

Sergio:        Did you experience any discrimination as a migrant in the US?

Francisco:        Yes. Yes. It's from everybody.

Sergio:        How about here in Mexico?

Francisco:        No. Well, actually, you know what yeah, from the cops. They see you and they know that you're not from here, and then they go after you. I've been working here in T Tech since probably February. What's that, four months? Just by the way that I walk, or how I look, they have pulled me over probably three or four times, just to check my back, to see I don't have any drugs or anything. Yeah, I do see that kind of discrimination here with the cops. That's it. With people, no.

Sergio:        Have you ever been approached or recruited by bad groups here in Mexico?

Francisco:        No. I haven't been approached by bad groups.

Sergio:        Do you miss anything about the US?

Francisco:        Just my family, mostly, and friends.

Sergio:        What do they say?

Francisco:        To go back. That's what they say.

Sergio:        Do you want to?

Francisco:        Yeah. I do want to go back.

Sergio:        How would you return?

Francisco:        Illegally. I tried doing it the right way, and look what it got me. Since I was locked up for nine months, I kinda know, I kinda figured how the system works, so I'm just going to try it illegally.

Sergio:        How does the system work, in your eyes?

Francisco:        If you try to work and do it the right way, they're just going to take your time and they're going to take your money away. Now, if you just give up and do the, what's it called, the deportation, you're just there probably a week, not even two weeks. They'll say things, like you're going to stay longer there, like 10 years, five years, if you come back. When I was locked up, I saw a person go there three times, and he got deported every time. I was there fighting my case the right way, and look what happened. So yeah, I'm going to try to go illegally.

Sergio:        In what ways do you think that being in the US has shaped who you are now?

Francisco:        Well, the way that I am, and I also know English. That's an advantage here in Mexico.

Sergio:        Do you feel more Mexican or American?

Francisco:        I don't feel neither Mexican or American. I'm in the middle.

Sergio:        Why is that?

Francisco:        ‘Cause…I’m Mexican, but I was raised out there. Then the same thing, I was raised out there, but I'm not from there, so same thing.

Sergio:        Do you have anything else on your mind that you want to say?

Francisco:        No. No. I'm okay.

Sergio:        What do you think that governments can do better for you?

Francisco:        Probably do more opportunities, pay more. Mexico is a nice country and it has everything. It's just the government is too screwed up. Supposedly they say that they're going to change it, but I haven't seen a change, not even with the new president. Probably give more opportunities for people, more programs, more things like that.

Sergio:        Do you think things have gotten worse since Trump came into office?

Francisco:        Not in Mexico. I wouldn't know about the States, because I'm not living there no more, but Mexico, I think, is just the same. It just doesn't affect as much down here. Probably on the border it does, but not here.

Sergio:        Okay. Thank you, Francisco, for your time.

Francisco:        All right.

Sergio:        That covers all the questions I have for you.
