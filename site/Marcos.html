Marcos

Interviewee

Claudia Ojeda-Rexach

Haverford College

Interviewer

June 4, 2019

Mexico City, Mexico

Claudia:        First of all, I want to say thank you for coming out here and speaking with me. And I understand that sometimes talking about these stories can be a little difficult because they bring about emotions and things that you don't want to go through again or relive. But I think it's good to note how important your story is and how important it's going to be for people to hear it and to understand the realities of migration and also the human side of migration. So thank you very much.

Marcos:        No, you're welcome.

Claudia:        And I do want to say that I may be taking some notes along the way if that's okay?

Marcos:        That's all right.

Claudia:        And yeah. So my first question is why did you leave Mexico and who with?

Marcos:        By myself, and it was because what happened where I lived, it was a lot of crime... that's why. I've never been gang-related, nothing like that. Never like that.

Claudia:        And what type of crime would happen?

Marcos:        Just drugs and things like that, and a lot of violence.

Claudia:        Was it organized criminals or cartels that ran?

Marcos:        It was a little bit of everything.

Claudia:        Do you remember your first day in the United States?

Marcos:        Yes. Well, I remember when I arrived there, it was on... I depart Mexico on August the 16th and it took me... 10 days to cross the border and then I got there on August the 29th, on Arizona. That was my first day there.

Claudia:        And what did you do?

Marcos:        Nothing. I just took a shower and eat.

Claudia:        What did you eat, do you remember?

Marcos:        It was a burrito.

Claudia:        So what did you do in the United States? What was your life like?

Marcos:        Well, I was trying to work, but my brother, he decided not to. He's the one that put me in school.

Claudia:        So you went to school? Yes?

Marcos:        Yeah with—

Claudia:        And do you remember any teachers or friends?

Marcos:        Yes, I remember Mrs. _____, she was a really nice teacher. She used to speak Spanish. I went to the _____ High School in 2006 to 2008. And yeah, it was really nice. I had a really nice time there, they were really great. They was helping the Spanish people.

Claudia:        Awesome. And so tell me about your job, your work, your life.

Marcos:        I used to do a little bit of everything, what I wanted to be was... I tried to play professional soccer, but I never had the money or the [inaudible 00:03:17]. So I started school, I started playing soccer. I was good at it and then what happened is that I moved to a different county. And that was when it was over with. But then after that I started working, a little bit of everything, restaurants, waitress, carwash, and things like that.

Claudia:        What did you like about soccer?

Marcos:        Everything. Yeah, since I was a little kid.

Claudia:        What's your favorite team?

Marcos:        From Mexico is America and from Europe is Barcelona.

Claudia:        And so how long did you play soccer for, until...

Marcos:        I played soccer for, I think it was a season and a half. What happened was that I got a little injured, my leg. And then I stopped playing.

Claudia:        And was there a lot of gang affiliations and stuff in the county that you lived in?

Marcos:        Well, it's everywhere, but I was never involved to that.

Claudia:        So why... how did you end up in prison?

Marcos:        Yeah. What happened is that I was with some friends, we were in the streets.

Claudia:        What were you doing?

Marcos:        We were just hanging out, like walk out, things like that. But, there was people that were doing other stuff. So I got involved and that's the reason I got arrested.

Claudia:        What kind of stuff?

Marcos:        Just stupid stuff, like just chillin’, [inaudible], doing like... going to a, what you call? Just kid places, you know?

Claudia:        So how did they get you then? You just got picked up one day?

Marcos:        Yeah. I just got picked up. We were doing just stupid stuff and then I got caught off.

Claudia:        Like drugs, alcohol?

Marcos:        No, no, no. It was other stuff. Yeah. So yeah, they pulled me over and as a result I ended up in the county.

Claudia:        So, how did you end up back in Mexico?

Marcos:        In Mexico, that was because... For that, that's how everything started, they did a follow-up. So when I did time in the county, they dropped, supposedly, the felony. They dropped it to a misdemeanor. And then when I went, they took me to immigration so that's where they hold me. They released me, they gave me a chance to stay in the States, but they got me for no driver's license, two times, three times total. So, and then... they keep you like that for two years.

Marcos:        So I applied for DACA. I did a follow-up, but I don't know what happened to be honest. The only thing that I wanted to be was to be over with. I just want to be free, I don't want to be... People follow me or, because I used to take daily appointments, once a week to immigration. I need to go over there and sign up, they talk to me and things like that. And they got me with a little bracelet on the leg so they know where I am, what I'm doing and all that stuff. So it was kind of aggravating and frustrating at the same time because I was feeling like a little dog, like an animal. I understand that we make mistakes and that's the way we learn. But that's no way to treat people like that, you know?

Claudia:        So why did they give you the chance to stay in the States?

Marcos:        Because I went to school and they told me to get a lawyer. I get a lawyer, the lawyer didn't do nothing, at all.

Claudia:        Yeah. Wow. So you kept continuing on in that process for two years, but you still were able to apply for DACA and all that, and you were on your path to get residency?

Marcos:        I was about to get the DACA, the permission to be there, to study, to work.

Claudia:        And you said that you later applied for political asylum?

Marcos:        Yeah, I did. Because what happened was I had no choice. I didn't know what to do. So they give me the voluntary departure. But if I do that, I have no one to help me out because what happened is that I have my buddies, you know they come here with the money? So no one... Besides that, I didn't want to get involved with no one, I didn't want to affect no one. So that's the reason that I did the political asylum, but that's the reason that I got deported, for that.

Claudia:        Got it, because you didn't get approved.

Marcos:        I didn't, right.

Claudia:        I understand. How was it like coming back to Mexico?

Marcos:        It's different. The weather, the atmosphere, it's really heavy. But it's okay, I raised here, so it's no big deal. One of the good things about me is that I get adapted real quick, anywhere. Doesn't matter if it's cold, if it's hot, I get adapted easily.

Claudia:        So was the return difficult?

Marcos:        Not really because everything... my entire family is here. My mom.

Claudia:        Yeah. Tell me about that. You left, how was it leaving your family and then coming back all those years later?

Marcos:        To me, it wasn't difficult to be honest. Because I'm those type of people that when I decide something I go for it. It doesn't matter what I had to leave behind. If I want that, that's what I do. So, I'm not, like, lonely person, but the type of the situation, I just always decide for myself. It is difficult, you've left your family, your dad, it was kind of difficult because my dad passed away. My brother got killed.

Claudia:        By?

Marcos:        My brother got killed by I don't know who. My dad just died. So those are the things that's kind of difficult still because I'm never going to get the chance to see them. The last time I saw them it was 2006, it was in August. That was the last time I saw their face.

Claudia:        Okay. So you said something about maybe getting some help from an organization here, but then not...

Marcos:        It was an organization about immigration, they welcome the immigrant. They call it ‘Repatriados’ [Returnees], something like that. So they were helping, they were giving me like a 2,265 Pesos a month, for six months, they were helping me out with that. They were about to help me with 25,000 Pesos, when I open my own business, but that's the part I didn't get. That they didn't follow up.

Claudia:        Would you like to open your own business?

Marcos:        Yes, to be honest. Yes.

Claudia:        What would you like it to be?

Marcos:        I like to create things, I like to do tile, so I will open a tile business.

Claudia:        Have you been discriminated here in Mexico for being a return migrant?

Marcos:        No, never.

Claudia:        Do you consider yourself Mexican or American?

Marcos:        United States is not just America, we're all American. But for country, I feel Mexican.

Claudia:        I'm from Puerto Rico, I totally understand that. If you could have stayed in the United States, what do you think you would have done?

Marcos:        Do better, to be honest. Do better and grow up and mature because in Mexico it's hard, to get a job, to get money. Everything's really expensive. If I would've stayed there, I would do better, I would have start saving money, sending money back to Mexico, helping my family, my brothers and sisters, something that I didn't...

Claudia:        Why do you think that you didn't do better?

Marcos:        I don't know. When you're a kid, when you're young you think you have everything in your hands, but you never know that you can lose everything on a blink of an eye. One mistake can take everything away.

Claudia:        And what do you think you'd like to do now that you're back in Mexico, what are your dreams?

Marcos:        Right now I just want to focus on my family. My little girl, my little boy, my wife and if I get the chance to help my family, I will.

Claudia:        So why, I'm going to ask you, these are my last few questions. They're more reflection based, but what can the Mexican government do to support Mexican men and Mexican returnees so that they don't turn to crime when they come back?

Marcos:        To be honest, that depends on everyone, because there's choices. You decide to be whatever you want to be, so even the government do something about it, if you don't want it, if you don't like it, you won't do it. So that's depending on self, on yourself than anyone else.

Claudia:        So is there anything that the government can do?

Marcos:        I believe they do good. They're receiving the immigrants back, helping them out with money, food, shelter and things like that. That's really good. But what they have to do is just make everything better, just don't leave it halfway. Like schools, they have really big needs. The opportunities for people that trained to became a teacher or to became a soldier or to became an engineer, help them because there's a lot of opportunities. And what I think is that Mexico could be one of the biggest country in the world if we help each other. If we open our own business, if we open our own companies and don't depend on other countries.

Claudia:        So, yeah. Those were all the questions that I had, but now I want to give you the space for you to say anything, anything that you want me to put in, anything you want to add or...

Marcos:        If someone hears this, I just want to let them know that take advantage of whatever opportunity they have if they're in the States. Just focus on the future because future is never... You never know what can happen, something can change, one little mistake can take you back. You can never regret it because you had the opportunity, you had the chance. I'm not saying in my country you don't have that, but it's easier over there in the United States, it's really easier. You have everything to do someone or become someone because there's a lot of support and the government helps a lot. Here in Mexico, it's pretty much the same thing, but this is more difficult. You have to work.

Claudia:        Yeah. Are you satisfied with everything that you've told me?

Marcos:        Yeah.

Claudia:        Yeah? All right, awesome. Thank you so much.
