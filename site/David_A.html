Anne:        So, first of all, I really want to thank you very much for being here.

Anne:        I know that we're going to talk about issues that are not always easy to talk about. They can be painful, the memories.

Anne:        Yeah. Anyway, so we can start just asking you a little bit about, when was it that you first came to the US, and how did you feel about it?

David:        The first time I came to the US, I was two and a half, so I don't ... I remember a lot of things. I remember being taken over the border when I was a baby. I can remember being on the bottom of the truck. I was in ... You know the trucks, in the passenger seat, the co-pilot has this space right under?

Anne:        Right.

David:        So, I hid under there the first time, and I kind of remember that, and it feels strange. I wouldn't say it feels bad, but it feels strange, because you don't know what's going on, and you're just told to ... or you only see things, and you're traveling. Or that's what I saw, at least. I saw that I was traveling, but then again, I was just a baby, two and a half. But I remember.

Anne:        Was that your first-ever memory?

David:        Not really, no. My first memory I have? I remember my mom and my dad on a bed. That's my first memory, but I was like, maybe eight months. I remember the scenario, the room. I remember where I was, where they were laying, the TV, a few portraits. I remember that. That's my first memory.

Anne:        So, you came at two and a half. And why did your parents take you to the US?

David:        Well, they had a bit of a problem. They separated, so I didn't really know what the problem was until I was like, 24. And when I was 24, they told me what happened, but it's a very big problem. I don't talk to my parents because of that.

David:        And the reason ... Well, how do you say it? When you cheat on someone? That was the problem. My dad used to be very popular with women, so yeah. Well, he had another home for another person, another woman. And my mom found out with my auntie, so they did something ... That's how Mexican women are. They do have to do something about that, always.

David:        And they found out. They took her stuff out. My dad had bought things for that other woman, so that was the reason. And she left. She left when I was two and a half. She just decided to ... First, we were living in Querétaro, where I was born. And she came back to el Estado de México [the State of Mexico]. So, she came back. And then next day, she just left. She had some cousins in San Diego that are Americans. They were born there.

David:        And they're Mexican descent, but they were born there. So, there's a kid that's my cousin. So he's the same age as me. So, that's how they passed me over the border. That was the first time. And, well, yeah. That was the problem.

Anne:        So it was you and your mom.

David:        Yeah, just me and my mom, and her cousin, which is the one that helped her. And yeah, just me and her. Yup.

David:        Her name was Katie. She used to go to preschool with me, and then she went with me until first grade. She used to be the daughter of this lady that we used to clean the house for, so she used to go to this small school in Oakland, but it was a small elementary school, so I think that lady helped her for me to get into that school for that year.

David:        But I had to leave. I had to go to the elementary school, the public elementary school. And I remember her because we used to go everywhere. Her mom and my mom were really ... I don't know. They connected. She was American. My mom was Mexican, but they connected and they were friends.

David:        And I remember her because of that. We used to go everywhere. I remember Katie a lot. I still remember, I have a few pictures of her. And there's this picture that I still have. David:        My grandma's house is right next to an elementary school and a little preschool. So one day, when she came back, she was like, "Hey, that's my kid." And she was trying to take me from the school, but here in Mexico, that's not allowed.
