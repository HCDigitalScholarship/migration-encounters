Octavio

Interviewee

Claudia Ojeda

Interviewer

June 7th, 2019

Mexico City, Mexico

Claudia:        Okay. So this is Claudia. Thank you so much for coming and speaking with us. I just first wanted to say that I understand that talking about these experiences and telling these stories can be a little difficult sometimes, because they bring up-

Octavio:        No worries.

Claudia:        ... certain emotions and memories, right? That you don't necessarily want to relive or feel. But, I think it's good to note how important your story is, and how important it is for people to hear it, so that they can begin to understand the realities of migration and the human side of migration. So thank you so much for participating.

Octavio:        No problem.

Claudia:        So, my first question for you, is why did you decide to leave Mexico?

Octavio:        Because, like I'm telling your coworker, that my plan was going to Canada, to Vancouver. Because I met a girl, and she was about to have a high school graduation, and she invite me over. But, I didn't have papers. So I had to go through the [inaudible 00:01:10].

Claudia:        How old were you?

Octavio:        At that time, I'm 19.

Claudia:        Got it.

Octavio:        But I ended up going to Lake Tahoe where I have an uncle living there, and I didn't make it to Canada. I didn't move to Canada. My uncle told me not to leave because I was a little young. But I only spent eight months in Lake Tahoe, and I went back to Los Angeles, because I had some friends from school, and from Mexico, they were already living there. So, I got in touch with them and I moved to LA.

Claudia:        Do you remember your first day in the United States?

Octavio:        Oh well, I remember when I was crossing the border.

Claudia:        Do you remember the first thing that you did when you got into the States?

Octavio:        Well, the first thing, since we crossed the border through the beach in Tijuana. I remember just thinking, I will say the trolley, because it wasn't a train, it was a trolley, going down to downtown San Diego. I don't remember the day, and then I get to have the feeling, the atmosphere that it was totally different country. It was totally different than Mexico. Even though I'm from Mexico City. You can… I remember seeing the difference, no? The way that people live in other time. I got, not surprised, I got curious because of the difference, no?

Claudia:        How did you learn English? What did you do in the States?

Octavio:        Oh, I learned the basics in Mexico, in junior high, and that helped me a lot when I went to the States. That was a really, really good course that I had.

Claudia:        So, what did you do there? How was your life like?

Octavio:        In the States? Great (chuckle). It was very good. All the time working, and at that time when I was 19, I couldn’t go to high school. I wanted to go to high school, but I was already old. So basically, sum my time up, I was by myself all the time was working.

Claudia:        What were you working at?

Octavio:        In a lot of restaurants. I'm a graduate here in Mexico as a French pastry chef, and I worked in restaurants over there all the time.

Claudia:        What were your favorite things to make?

Octavio:        Well, I used to like to bake cakes… and basically cakes. What I did most in the restaurants, I worked most in the kitchen on a culinary side, and the service side. Because I wanted to learn.

Claudia:        What did you like about that?

Octavio:        Everything. I like cooking.

Claudia:        Why?

Octavio:        Because I like it when I make something, and people like it, enjoy it. Basically is it.

Claudia:        So how long were you in the United States for?

Octavio:        Since 1989, til 2011.

Claudia:        All right, and is that all you did during that time? Just work? Or did you have like family

Octavio:        Actually, I used to live with always with roommates, sometimes by myself. But I used to all the time work, eventually when I go out and met more people or something like that, I started doing salsa lessons. I did DJ on Latino clubs. I used to DJ Spanish rock, salsa, and electronic music.

Claudia:        How was that?

Octavio:        Great. (chuckle)

Claudia:        What's your favorite thing to play?

Octavio:        Salsa, and electronic music. What else? I did… from some time to time I did commercials, TV commercials. I came up as an extra, I came out in a movie, and a couple actual TV series.

Claudia:        Which movies and series?

Octavio:        Showtime, with Robert DeNiro and Eddie Murphy. I was about to come out on the movie, Dance With Me, with Chayanne and Jessica Williams. But the filming for the extras, it was about to last two weeks, and I didn't get permission to my work, my job. But I did most commercials.

Claudia:        So did you meet a lot of some celebrities?

Octavio:        Yeah.

Claudia:        Who did meet?

Octavio:        Oh, you can name it. I used to work in a foreign five-star restaurants.

Claudia:        Oh. How many restaurants did you work at?

Octavio:        I lost the count, but always nice restaurants, good restaurants. I've worked with really, really well-known chefs.

Claudia:        Did you have a favorite one?

Octavio:        A couple, yeah.

Claudia:        Like which one?

Octavio:        One of them is Neil Fraser. And the other one is French, it's Fredrick Ludovich.

Claudia:        So, you learned from them and stuff?

Octavio:        Yeah, one of them has his own TV show.

Claudia:        Oh, nice. So what was your favorite thing about being in the United States?

Octavio:        Their way of living, totally different than Mexico.

Claudia:        In what way?

Octavio:        Money-wise.

Claudia:        Money-wise?

Octavio:        Economy.

Claudia:        What was it like to have left your family behind in Mexico to border the States?

Octavio:        Well, it was always hard, it's hard. The things that really make me go back to Mexico was that, my family. I left all my brothers, my mother, my father.

Claudia:        Yep. Tell me about that. How did you end up back here in Mexico?

Octavio:        Well, I end up here because my mother had an accident. It was like eight years before I decided to come here. And my brothers and sisters, they have their life, they married, they have a lot of stuff to do besides taking care of my mom. So, it was time for me to take care of my mother. That's basically the main, basic thing that made me move to back to Mexico City. Also my license expire at that time. So, I know I wasn't able to have a new license.

Claudia:        So you had a license, how did you get it?

Octavio:        I get it 14 years in _____ Oregon.

Claudia:        Even by being undocumented?

Octavio:        It was time to still travel license without a security number at that time.

Claudia:        Yeah. So, did you ever get caught by the authorities in the U.S.?

Octavio:        Yeah, I got some tickets. I had to pay a ticket, I had a few warnings.

Claudia:        But you came back on your own. You weren't deported?

Octavio:        One time.

Claudia:        Oh, you were deported one time? Tell me about that.

Octavio:        Oh, I went to pay a traffic ticket, and I find out that I have already a warning. So, I came arrest. I couldn't pay with cash or nothing. Had to do a time. So I did, they gave me 90 days. I make three days, but I got caught by the immigration, in the county. So that's why they send me back to Mexico City. But as soon as they send me back, I went back to L.A.

Claudia:        How long before-

Octavio:        Before I get back? Hours.

Claudia:        Oh really? That same day you went back?

Octavio:        Mm-hmm (affirmative). Then, you know I'm always stay out of troubles, but I have a couple of tickets. That's why I get caught. But then since I got back, I pay all my tickets, all my fines, so I have to do the right thing.

Claudia:        After that time that you got deported, then, when you came back permanently, you decided to come back?

Octavio:        To Mexico City. Yeah, years later, I mean, a lot of years later.

Claudia:        That was just your decision, no police involved, no I.C.E., No, nothing?

Octavio:        Mm-hmm (affirmative).

Claudia:        Okay. How was that return?

Octavio:        Hard. So I didn't even want to come back.

Claudia:        You didn't?

Octavio:        But I mean, I had to come back, basically, because my mother. I knew maybe nine years before I decided to move in. They were closing for illegal immigrants. I said, work-wise, no? Every single time I'm working a restaurant or any place I always apply as an American citizen.

Claudia:        Were you ever discovered?

Octavio:        Oh all the time. At the restaurants and all that they used to have the letters from the social security, with this big list that people that have the real social security number. But the owners or the managers, they used to… didn't care. (chuckle) That's a good thing, no?

Claudia:        Yeah. So, what's been the hardest part about being back in Mexico?

Octavio:        It's been seven years since I got back, and I haven't got on my feet yet.

Claudia:        What have you been up to here?

Octavio:        I work in a little restaurants, I opened my own business, I close down. That's a lot of stuff I closed down because they want me to pay, let's say rent.

Claudia:        What was your business?

Octavio:        I had a pizzeria, pizza parlor.

Claudia:        How long did you have it?

Octavio:        For a year and a half. I was doing okay, but I had to close it down, because like I told you, they want me for me to pay rent, you know? I mean, I used to pay rent legally. I have my business legally, but the chicos malos wanted for me to pay them rent so I can be safe.

Claudia:        So, do you feel safe in Mexico?

Octavio:        Yes, but until that time, so I had to close down. I didn't want to pay any money as a security, no? You know what I mean, right?

Claudia:        Yeah.

Octavio:        So, I decided to close down, and then I opened the business in Los Cabos, and then I closed down because of that. Then I came to Mexico City.

Claudia:        What has it been like to see your family again?

Octavio:        It's alright. I mean, it's always great being around your family, but I still miss there, the way I used to live in LA.

Claudia:        So. what are you up to now?

Octavio:        Now, I'm working here and there, and I have maybe an opportunity to go overseas. I was working on it right now.

Claudia:        Where?

Octavio:        To Armenia.

Claudia:        Oh really? To do what?

Octavio:        A Mexican place.

Claudia:        To open-

Octavio:        To open a Mexican restaurant.

Claudia:        To open a Mexican restaurant in Armenia, wow that's crazy.

Octavio:        With a friend of mine, maybe, maybe.

Claudia:        That's awesome. That would be really cool.

Octavio:        In the meantime, you know, we're here and there.

Claudia:        Are you still working on restaurants?

Octavio:        Yeah, from time to time, and I work from my home.

Claudia:        Have you ever been discriminated against for being a returning migrant in Mexico?

Octavio:        Believe it or not, yes.

Claudia:        I believe it. Tell me a little bit more about it.

Octavio:        People look at you like you... people who don’t know how it works. They think you left Mexico because you hate Mexico. No. On the first place. So, when you come back, the way I did was like... they make you think that you are a loser, no? A failure, but it's totally the opposite, no?

Octavio:        I mean, working over there, learning stuff over there, I mean, it's different than working over here, and people feel threatened. I mean, threatened? Because you have more experience, you do stuff more easily. You got more choices, but still no, people want to put you down, because you speak English better than people that have courses, expensive certificates, no?

Claudia:        Yeah, a lot of people that we talk to sometimes say that here in Mexico, people just want to shut you down. They're very competitive, especially when you come from the States. Whereas in the States, people try to help you, and be good Samaritans or…

Octavio:        Right here, I mean, they're still good, but the people really, really sometimes discriminate, in that way. I mean, and it's hard feeling that here in your country, to be honest, I never feel discriminated in the States.

Claudia:        Did you leave any family behind in the States when you came back?

Octavio:        Well, it's funny. It's a good question because I have a daughter, she's 16. But I find out when she was 10 years old.

Claudia:        Oh, you didn't know about her?

Octavio:        I didn't know. Her mother found me on Facebook, and she told me the news when she was already 10.

Claudia:        How was that?

Octavio:        Hard. It's hard because one of the things that made me move back to Mexico City is that I accomplished a lot of stuff over there in the States, but I was by myself at the time. I'm always feel, it got to a point where I feel empty, and I was thinking, I miss my family. I miss my family. I get back to Mexico City, I hug my mom and all that. I hang with my brothers, my sisters. The love is there, right? They don't, they don't care, no? They love you anyways. But I still feel empty.

Octavio:        Then a year, a year and a half later I found out that I have a daughter. So, I ended up with the conclusion that was my emptiness, no? You know what I mean?

Claudia:        Yeah, that's what you wanted.

Octavio:        I mean, I didn't know how my daughter and she was already 10 when I find out.

Claudia:        Yeah.

Octavio:        So I put the time together when I start feeling that way, and it started. The feeling starts basically when she born, which I didn't know. I brought her.

Claudia:        So, you've never met her?

Octavio:        Through pictures, her mom sent me pictures. But she hasn't tell her, my daughter, that her real father is me, not the guy she grew up with.

Claudia:        So you've never spoken to her?

Octavio:        No. I left that to God. No? She told me because she went to these, a new religion. One of those religions that make you clear all the things of yourself. Basically that's what she told me. And I didn't pressure, nothing. I just, I only told her that I leave that to God, whatever she wanted to tell her, beyond, I'll be right here.

Claudia:        Do you still get pictures?

Octavio:        Yeah, every couple of months, sometimes every month, every three months, every six months.

Claudia:        What's her name?

Octavio:        Caroline. Carolina.

Claudia:        Carolina. Where in the States?

Octavio:        She's in _____.

Claudia:        In Oregon?

Octavio:        That's where we bake the muffin. That I didn't know.

Claudia:        So, do you consider yourself Mexican or American?

Octavio:        I consider myself both.

Claudia:        Well, there's a saying that we always hear that says from migrants who come back, that it's, "We're neither here nor from there." What do you think about that?

Octavio:        Well, it depends on how we say in Mexico is the [foreign language 00:20:41]. I mean, to me, it's like, I only had good things from the States. When I left Mexico, I was 19. I was under my mother and my father wing, so everything was fine, no? I left because I decided to go. But because of this girl that I met, no? But I feel Mexican, and from the U.S. I don't feel like I'm not from here, I'm from there. Legally or not from there, legally I'm from here. But my heart is in the States, and most because my daughter is over there. You know?

Claudia:        In what ways do you think that the United States has shaped where you are today?

Octavio:        Because I grew up in the States basically. Well, I leave my childhood here, I become a man in the States.

Claudia:        If you could have stayed in the U.S., what do you think you would have done?

Octavio:        I had the opportunity to work on my paperwork, no? Over there. But I wanted to come back because my family and my mother, so I didn't take it that way. I had the money to fix out all that. But, I decided to come back. That's what I would've done, fix all my status, and then come back. But I didn't think it that way.

Claudia:        Do you think you're going to go back someday?

Octavio:        Yeah, I have a lot of friends, families that I met through the years over there.

Claudia:        And your daughter.

Octavio:        My daughter basically, no?

Claudia:        So I know you've been back here eight-

Octavio:        Seven.

Claudia:        Seven years.

Octavio:        Seven and a half.

Claudia:        So, now that you've been back here, what do you think you'd like to do? What are your dreams and your goals?

Octavio:        Well, my dreams right now are take a certification course for English, which I never took in the States. I should've done that, too. Have my own business.

Claudia:        Your business being?

Octavio:        Restaurant.

Claudia:        What kind of restaurant?

Octavio:        I was thinking most like American, Mexican, a little bit of everything. Not too much, but always with a salsa band, live salsa band.

Claudia:        Well, definitely let me know when you open it, and I'll come back to visit it.

Octavio:        You got it.

Claudia:        So, these next few questions are more reflective-based. So, what do you think that the Mexican government can do to help returning migrants reintegrate into Mexican society?

Octavio:        They can do a lot, they didn't do nothing.

Claudia:        What's one thing you think they should do?

Octavio:        To reunite the families?

Claudia:        To help Mexican migrants-

Octavio:        Not to go over there?

Claudia:        ... reintegrate when they back?

Octavio:        Reintegrate? One thing that I think, this won't happen, if you get good salary. You know? I think that’s the… from that, it will be totally different. People don't go to States. I didn't go because of the economy, when my family didn't have that much, while I used to have what I need. Don't take me wrong, my family is not rich, or nothing, no? But I didn't have the need for me to go to States because to make money. But when I was there, I noticed the difference, right? But I know a lot of people go because of the money. Because of low salary, low opportunities, no? That's the first thing came to my mind. If Mexico have a good salary, and they really care about the employees, about the citizens in Mexico, people won't go anywhere. You know?

Claudia:        Yeah.

Octavio:        That's what I think. That's my point of view.

Claudia:        What can the United States government do to help Mexican deportees, and also the families that they leave?

Octavio:        To me, to be honest, they don’t have to do nothing. Right? But also, being there, they can also help people over there. The people that stay over there already, no? Because at some point they always get benefits from the illegals, immigrants, right?

Octavio:        Because just, just telling you this, that there's a lot of people that don't do their income tax, a lot of illegal immigrants. And then always, when you get your paycheck, you always got your taxes deducted. So, where all that money is ,no? That people got never the income tax until now, because they got afraid of doing an income tax and get caught through immigration because of that. So, where all the money is? That's why I'm point. It's a lot. Like in Mexico, like me, I'm in Mexico. I don't think there's something that the United States need to do. For me, it has nothing to do with that, no? United States doesn’t have to do anything, just because I live over there.

Claudia:        So, I don't know how much interaction you had with gangs in the United States, but I'm curious what you have your opinion of why young, Mexican men in the United States turn towards crime and gangs?

Octavio:        Because they're stupid. I mean, I have a lot of friends over there, they were Mexican Americans. They were members of, of a gang. They decided to go deeper there. But I don't know, I think it's an easy way out. I don't know.

Claudia:        So, I guess in talking about what happened with your business, and the people who were trying to get you to pay them, which I'm assuming were some sort of organized criminals.

Octavio:        Yeah, yeah they were organized criminals, yeah.

Claudia:        What can the Mexican government do to prevent returning migrants from turning to crime and gangs when they come back to Mexico? Or from falling into that?

Octavio:        The main thing, is get rid of the corruption. That's it. Being a country free of corruption, it would be a different country. Right?

Claudia:        Mm-hmm (affirmative). Okay. So those were all the questions that I had. But now I want to give you the space to add anything. Anything else that you would like to say. It can be whatever you want, something that you feel like I should know, or that you didn't tell me, or that you want to clarify, or something that you want to add on to.

Octavio:        Nah, maybe just that I miss the States.

Claudia:        What do you miss the most about it?

Octavio:        Everything. Yeah, for me, thank God, I didn't have any bad experience. I never ran into a gang. I'm just work and try to be a decent citizen. There's no, I mean, I didn't have the problems most of the guys got deported from, no? I could've done things different. I could've fix my papers right away, as soon as I can. I couldn't make it.

Claudia:        Are you satisfied with everything that you've told me?

Octavio:        Yeah, it's fine.

Claudia:        Cool, well, thank you so much.

Octavio:        No problem.

Claudia:        Yeah, thank you.
