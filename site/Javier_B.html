Javier

Interviewee

Claudia

Interviewer

June 4th, 2019

Mexico City, Mexico

Claudia:        So this is Claudia. I am interviewing Javier, whose ID is JGE.

Javier:        JG... Sorry.

Claudia:        Yeah, so thank you so much for coming out here and speaking to me and speaking with us and participating in this study. I know that speaking about these sort of experiences can sometimes be a little painful because it brings about emotions that you don't necessarily want to relive or rethink about.

Javier:        Yeah.

Claudia:        But I think it's good to note that this story is very important and it’s important for people to hear it and for you to tell it so that we can start breaking the stereotypes and start telling people about the realities of migration and the human side, so yeah, thank you very much.

Javier:        You're welcome.

Claudia:        My first question for you is... Oh, and I'm also going to be taking some notes maybe, if that's okay.

Javier:        Yeah, yeah. For sure.

Claudia:        My first question is why did you decide to leave Mexico and how did you get to the United States?

Javier:        Why did I decide?

Claudia:        Mm-hmm (affirmative).

Javier:        Because I wanted to learn English, I guess, the American dream. I wanted to make money, live well. I did to the river. I crossed the river. That's how I did it.

Claudia:        You were 17?

Javier:        I was 17 then.

Claudia:        And you did it alone?

Javier:        Well, yeah, I mean with some people there at the border, but yeah.

Claudia:        How was it to leave your family behind in Mexico? How did that feel?

 Javier:        It was hard. It was difficult at first you know. I was a teenager still. It was hard. I was at the border for a month trying to cross, and then when I get to cross, I was there another month or so, waiting to travel to Houston, actually. It took me three months to get to Houston.

Claudia:        Do you remember your first day in the United States?

Javier:        Yeah.

Claudia:        Can you tell me about it?

Javier:        It was, yeah I remember when I crossed the border. It was so pretty. I find it was like a different world. It was very, very nice, clean. It was dark because it was, I think, it was like 10:00 or 9:00. I don't know. Yeah, it was very different. It was nice.

Claudia:        What did you do your first day?

Javier:        I had a hamburger and then I just... We just went to hike.

Claudia:        How was the hamburger?

Javier:        It was awesome. It was very good.

Claudia:        Where did you get it?

Javier:        I don't know. It was near the border, because we walked like all night, and then hot in the day, and then we start walking again in the afternoon. Then, I don't know, we walk a lot. So, it was my first meal. It was awesome.

Claudia:        That's great. How did you end up learning English? What did you do when you got to the states?

Javier:        It was awful at first. I used mainly through TV, though, with subtitles and everything, watching movies and then making friends, people at work. That's how, I guess, that's how I learned English, though. Yeah, mostly with friends, and you know girlfriends.

Claudia:        Did you go to school?

Javier:        No. I didn't.

Claudia:        What happened?

Javier:        I wanted to, but I just didn’t had to support. I had to pay for my bills and everything. It was either work or study.

Claudia:        Where did you work?

Javier:        I guess I work in a lot of places, really, many, many things. My first job, I think, was cleaning, I think, for some Chinese guy. I don't know. Yeah, it was cleaning.

Claudia:        Do you remember any specific friends, or people, somebody in the US that made an impact?

Javier:        I did have friends from there though. I keep in touch with them. Yeah, I do remember a lot of friends.

Claudia:        Yeah.

Javier:        Like Mexican friends or American friends?

Claudia:        Whichever, just people who you remember.

Javier:        Cause I hang out most with white people.

Claudia:        How was that?

Javier:        Well, my ex-wife was white. It was good.

Claudia:        Did you ever come across any gangs or any of that sort of thing?

Javier:        Oh, no.

Claudia:        No? Your ex-wife, you had mentioned that you have...

Javier:        Son.

Claudia:        ... a son?

Javier:        Right, what happened?

Claudia:        Yeah, no, I mean tell me about him. Tell me about your family and that stuff in the states.

Javier:        That's hard. I haven't seen him ever since I was, since they took me. When the police got me was back in what 2010, I guess. It was 2010 when they got me, and then they put me away. I haven't seen my son ever since.

Claudia:        How old was your son then?

Javier:        He was five at that time. I was already divorced with his mom. She never took him to see me or anything. I used to call him, though, every other week, every week sometimes. She got married again with another Mexican [inaudible 00:06:11].  She deleted her Facebook. She moved, changed her cell phone. I haven't talked to my son. It's been a while, though. I don't know anything about him.

Claudia:        During your time in the states you pretty much just worked? What else did you do for fun? Or what did you like to do?

Javier:        Go out a lot.

Claudia:        Yeah?

Javier:        Yeah, I mean I used to go to the games, a lot of football. Played ball with my friends. What else? Just drink, party, work, hunting sometimes, fishing. That's pretty much all.

Claudia:        Tell me how you ended up back in Mexico.

Javier:        How? I was deported.

Claudia:        How were you deported?

Javier:        Because when I was released from prison, they even offer me to... They gave me for my ticket, though.

Claudia:        Wait. Why were you in prison?

Javier:        They took me because I was illegal.

Claudia:        How did they get you?

Javier:        ICE got me when I was in [inaudible 00:07:41]. I pay my bond. It was $1,000. I paid that, and then they said my status was illegal, so they took me. They sent me to prison. When I got out of prison, I was supposed to be released, but I didn't, though.

Claudia:        You were just minding your own business one day and then ICE just got you?

Javier:        No. I was drinking and driving. There was a stop. They were doing some type of a... I don't know. They were looking for someone. I didn't have a license. I passed the test, but they knew, so they checked my status and they sent me to jail.

Claudia:        You passed the breather test and stuff?

Javier:        I did. But they still took me, so in jail all night and then the next day, when I paid my bond, that's when they were ready, so they took me.

Claudia:        What was that return like? How has that been?

Javier:        It was awful at first, no money and my wallet and my stuff, everything was missing. That's what the marshal say, it was missing. I have no money at all, no clothes, zero contacts. I don't have any phone number. I didn't even know how I was going to make it here until I crossed the border and I met these people. They told me that they could help me with... How much was it? I think it was… like I think they say 2,000 pesos. That was the limit for my ticket. I think it was 1655 pesos. I don't know how much was it. They paid for it, though. They even got me an ID because I didn't have anything at all. And that's how I got here.

Claudia:        The people who got you the ID and everything, was it people in Mexico or were they people in the states?

Javier:        In Mexico.

Claudia:        What did you do when you come back? Did you see your family? How was that?

Javier:        Oh, yeah. The first day, as soon as I got here, I start to... Well, I didn't... It was changed, the city. I needed, for the metro, I needed to buy a ticket, so someone at the bus station, I think at the time it was two pesos, I think. I don't know how much was it. They give me five pesos, and that's how I bought my ticket. Then I knew my way for some reason. It's not that hard to be here on the metro. I just went to see my aunt and then my mom. It was nice. It was kind of sad, but it was nice.

Claudia:        In what way was it sad?

Javier:        Because I never expect to be back in Mexico the way I did, broke and no clothes and everything. I don't know. My dream just went away. I don't know. It was bad.

Claudia:        What was the hardest part of coming back?

Javier:        Coming back. I didn’t, I didn’t, I never thought I was going to be back at all. I had my life there. I don't know. It was hard, though, because I have no friends, no money, no clothes, no job. I didn't know what to do.

Claudia:        You virtually come back to a country that you don't know. Right?

Javier:        It was changed a lot, a lot, buildings a lot, malls and whatnot. It was very different. The people, the way they look at me because of my tattoos. It was bad. It was bad. Yeah.

Claudia:        In your survey you mentioned that one of the challenges that you faced was discrimination. Can you talk a little bit about that?

Javier:        When I was starting to get a job, because of my tattoos they thought I was like, I don't know, a member of a gang. I don't know what they thought. I don't even smoke, but they would say I was... I don't know. It was bad, though. Then my first job that I was able to land, I was making 700 pesos a week, I think. That's not enough, though. After I met this girl, and she told me that she was working as a bilingual and she was making good money. That's how it started. Otherwise I wouldn't even know about it, like a bilingual job here. That's how I started working at the call center ever since. That was in 2012. I think it was May. I got here September, so yeah, it was in May, I think, when I was able I got that job. It was pretty decent. Ever since, I just start working in the call center.

Claudia:        What do you like about call centers?

Javier:        The money, I guess, talk to people, meeting new people. What else? Somehow I feel like I'm still connected to people in the States, so that's what I like also.

Claudia:        Have you received any help from the government or any other organization at all?

Javier:        Here? No, I wish not at all.

Claudia:        You mentioned that you are going to start continuing your studies. What has prevented you from doing so, so far?

Javier:        Well, I have another son here now, so I have to pay child support because I'm not with the mom. That and I have to pay my bills, my rent. I have to have a stable job so I can go to school, but it's hard because you have to work nine, eight, nine, ten hours so you can make a decent money. It's hard, though.

Claudia:        What do you miss about the United States?

Javier:        What do I miss about the states? Well, mainly my son, my friends, the culture, I guess, the way that you can live there. It's different. That's pretty much all.

Claudia:        How do you think that the United States shaped who you are today?

Javier:        I'm sorry. I didn't hear that question. What was that?

Claudia:        How do you think that the United States shaped who you are today?

Javier:        I mean I guess I learned English because of there, so I gotta be thankful, all the ways I see things different because the culture is a lot different. The people I used to hang out with, it was decent a people. I don't know. I guess just a different way of seeing the life of what you do, what you can achieve. I don't know. I guess it was in a good way, though. Yeah.

Claudia:        Do you consider yourself Mexican or American?

Javier:        Well, now I guess, I guess now Mexican again.

Claudia:        Mexican again?

Javier:        Yeah. When I came back, I didn't feel Mexican at all, though.

Claudia:        Why not?

Javier:        Because I feel like I'm rejected by my own people, so I didn't... I felt like I didn't belong here, even though I was born here. It was, I don't know, it felt weird. Yeah, it was weird.

Claudia:        If you could have stayed in the United States, what do you think you would have done?

Javier:        I guess the American dream, probably have a career, I don't know, be with my son, start a new family maybe. I don't know, just be there, though.

Claudia:        You said that you got deported for life?

Javier:        For life, yeah.

Claudia:        Just because they caught you one day and you hadn't done anything wrong, basically. And of course drinking and driving is not okay, but you passed the test.

Javier:        I did pass the test with the pen and everything, but I guess officer, he was racist. He was white, but you can tell. He was very, very racist. I guess, I don't know, based on my record I guess they decided I was not a welcome person there, I guess. Like I say, I was married also. I applied for my citizenship. I was about to become a resident, not citizen, resident, but I got divorced, so...

Claudia:        You were married to a citizen? To an American citizen?

Javier:        Right, yeah. I had a court day in Georgia, but I missed that because I was in prison. The letter said it was okay, though, that I don't need to worry about it, but obviously it wasn't because they deported me anyway. So one day, when I was released from Mississippi, they picked me up again, ICE. They take me. They took me to where it was this... Where they took me, though? I think it was a Thursday, or Wednesday or Thursday. It was 9:00 or 8:00, because I hadn't even had breakfast that day. They took me to some kind of like a rathole. It was bad, though. I was there for three nights, and then on Monday they took me to the... I don't know if a prison, but it was for only people from everywhere. Where was that at? All I remember was they had good food there, though. It was good food. It was good. The only thing good about it was the food.

Claudia:        What was bad? How did they treat you?

Javier:        It was good. They didn't let me. I wanted to see a judge but they didn't let me. They just went to my cell one night and they said I was going to be deported.

Claudia:        Why didn't they let you see a judge?

Javier:        I don't know. They were laughing, though. They didn’t need to. I said, "I didn't sign anything."

Javier:        They said, "Well, you know what? You don't need to sign anything. You're going to go back to your country." They just took me.

Claudia:        Now that you're back in Mexico, what do you think you'd like to do? What are your hopes and dreams?

Javier:        Here? I guess buy a house, have a steady job so I can do well. I guess make my American dream here now. Now that I'm here, now  that I realize that you can also make money here. It's going to be hard, but it's not impossible. You can make it happen, though.

Claudia:        There's no other questions.

Javier:        That's all?

Claudia:        That I have. But I guess now I want to give you the chance to just say anything you'd like to add, anything that you feel like you didn't say or that you'd want me to know. Now's the time. Whatever you want to say.

Javier:        Whatever I want to say? I wish I can go back at least once to see my son, though. If they could just allow me to once see, that would be nice. I mean I know it's tough. That's not an option, but that would be now my only reason to go back to the states, probably see my friends, but mainly my son. Definitely not to stay, though, because I don't think I can... Well, who knows? It's not anywhere in my plans to live in the States or go back to the States, though. After the way I was treated when they deport me, no. I feel like I was somehow betrayed. Because those people from ICE, they were, it was bad. It was bad, so...

Claudia:        Are you good with everything you've told me?

Javier:        Yeah.

Claudia:        All right, well thank you so much.

Javier:        You're welcome.
