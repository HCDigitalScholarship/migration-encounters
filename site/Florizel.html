Mexico City, Mexico

Anne:        Great. Hi, Florizel.

Florizel:        Hi.

Anne:        It's so nice to have you here. I'm Anne. I'm here from Philadelphia. We are so thankful that you're here to tell us about your experiences as a returning migrant. I understand that it could be painful. I don't know your story, since I didn't listen to your survey. I'm sorry if it is. I just think it's important for people to hear the stories of people who have been to the United States and left. So, anyway, as I said, I don't know anything about your story. How old were you when you went to the United States?

Florizel:        I was actually eight months old.

Anne:        I was going to ask you what it was like when you first got there, but I bet you don't remember.

Florizel:        I don't remember that. My mom actually, I guess by her job, she got a visa, so we actually went to the United States legally.

Anne:        What did she do?

Florizel:        Her job? I actually don't know. I think she worked at a factory or something.

Anne:        You went with her and who else?

Florizel:        Just my mom. My dad, he was already over there. We actually went to California. We were in Long Beach, California.

Anne:        No siblings?

Florizel:        No. I'm actually the oldest.

Anne:        I see. I see. You're eight months old, and so you really probably grew up as an American. Is that true?

Florizel:        Yeah. I did.

Anne:        What did your father do?

Florizel:        My dad, he was actually working at a restaurant. There's this famous place in California called __________. He was actually working at ____________. That's what he was doing. My mom was just a stay-at-home mom.

Anne:        Did you get more siblings as you-

Florizel:        Yeah. I actually have one brother and two sisters.

Anne:        What was it like growing up? Were you in Long Beach the whole time?

Florizel:        First of all, since I'm the oldest, I kind of learned how to speak Spanish and English at the same time, so I was my mom's translator the whole time, since I was, that I remember, was six years old and she would tell me to translate when we would go to the bank, when she wanted to order something. I was actually her main help. My brothers and my sister were actually, they were born over there. They grew up speaking English instead of Spanish.

Anne:        She only spoke Spanish?

Florizel:        My mom, she speaks Spanish, but we'd always talk to her in English, but she would always answer back in Spanish. My dad actually knows both languages, because we always speak to him in English.

Anne:        You went to school?

Florizel:        Yeah. I actually attended, it's called _______  in Utah. We moved to Utah. Then I went to _________ and then ______ High School.

Anne:        Why did you move to Utah?

Florizel:        My dad was actually, since California's a place where a lot of the people from Mexico go, so he was actually just getting into trouble and all that stuff. My mom needed a man to help out in the house, not a child. So, we decided since we're members of the Mormon church, we have some friends that lived in Utah, and they invited us to go over. We decided to stay there.

Anne:        Your dad went with you?

Florizel:        Yeah.

Anne:        Did he become a man?

Florizel:        He became a man. He became a father type. He was actually way different, because that I remember, when we used to live in California, he used to drink a lot. I don't know if he did any drug use, but he would always come home beaten up, all bloody, his knuckles all bloody, stabbed. When we moved to Utah, all that thing, all of it stopped. It was good to see that.

Anne:        Wow. Growing up in the United States, did you feel different than other kids or did you feel just like any American?

Florizel:        I actually feel kinda different, because I grew up in violence in my house. All the friends that I had, American friends that I had, they never talked about any violence in their home or being mistreated or anything like that. I felt out of place.

Anne:        When you talk about violence, what do you mean?

Florizel:        They would hit me if I didn't do my homework. If I didn't know my times table, they would hit me, just to learn. They thought that by hitting me, I will learn faster. That was hard. I was the only one that received that punishment, since I was the oldest.

Anne:        Your younger siblings didn't get that punishment?

Florizel:        My brother did a little bit. The one in the middle, Wendy, she really didn't receive it, and the youngest really not at all.

Anne:        That seemed like a real difference.

Florizel:        Yeah, it was different, because growing up in the Mormon Church, you talk to your friends, and they all reunite and they have different kind of lifestyles than what I grew up with. Sometimes I would tell my parents, why couldn't I be like my friend Rachel. They'd say, "Cause you’re not… First of all because we're Mexican, and their parents didn't show them how to ... " They were just raised in different ways. It was kind of hard. It was really hard.

Anne:        Were your parents Mormon before they came to the States?

Florizel:        Yeah. They were actually Mormon.

Anne:        Is there much of a Mormon church in ... Were you born in Mexico City?

Florizel:        I was born in Matamoros, over by the border.

Anne:        There was a Mormon church that you-

Florizel:        They were actually Mormon. I have an aunt. She actually, I think she was Mormon her whole life. That's when they decided to join the Mormon Church. Then when we went to California, my parents, they know a lot of Mormons, they became friends with them. Until now, they're still friends with us. They're actually more family than my actual family.

Anne:        You went to elementary school you said in California and then middle and high in Utah.

Florizel:        We actually moved a lot. I went to two different elementaries in California. Then when we stayed in Utah, then that's the only elementary that I went to was _______. Then from there I went to junior high, then high school.

Anne:        Were you moving a lot because of issues associated with being a migrant?

Florizel:        Kind of. That's one of the problems. My parents were always afraid. I remember one time I was doing a report in school about immigrants. It gave me so much information about how I can become a U.S. citizen. I told my dad, I told him the plan. I was like, "Look, I was reading these books. I'm actually doing a paper on it. I'm thinking of becoming a citizen." He told me not to touch that subject, because if I did anything, they would come and deport all of us, just me and my mom and my dad. He told me just to leave it alone. Then when I was in front of a judge, he told me, "Why didn't you become a citizen when you were 18?" I told him I couldn't.

Anne:        It’s really loud.

Florizel:        Not because I didn't want to. It's because my parents didn't allow me to. They were easygoing with me in that situation when I was in court, because I was actually held for a month in the county jail by ICE.

Anne:        I haven't got to that point yet, but we definitely will. We will. You graduate from high school, and you wanted to become a citizen, but your parents were worried that since they were illegal that you would bring attention to the rest of the family, right?

Florizel:        Yeah.

Anne:        After high school did you go to work or did you-

Florizel:        Yeah. I actually worked. I worked at Wendy's for like three years. Then from there I had a problem with one of these two managers. Obviously, they were always on drugs. I think it was after Christmas. We were really busy. I think they were coming down on their drugs. I was just on my own, doing my own thing. Then they got upset and decided to fire me. My supervisor, my manager back then, I called her up. She was actually in vacation. I called her up and I told her what happened. She came back after that.

Florizel:        Before this whole thing started, my parents always told me that I was born in California. They told me that I was born in Harbor City, California. I had a green card. I had a Social Security card, but I never knew it was fake. I was really confident with myself, going, getting into trouble and stuff like that.

Florizel:        I went to work, and my manager told me to come back. Then she said that I needed to take a form to the Social Security Department. Then I told my mom, I was like, "Oh, can you come with me, so I can go and get this filled out?" She said, "No. Just leave it alone." She was like, "Just get another job." I was like, "I can't come back to work, because my parents won't allow me." So I insisted, I asked my mom, I was like, "What happened? What's going on?" Then she told me. She's like, "You know what? It's because your Social Security, we're sharing it with someone else." I was like, "Okay." She's like, "We can't get that person in trouble." I was like, "All right." I just left it alone. Then I got another job. They didn't question me or anything like that. Everything went through like normal.

Anne:        Did your mother tell you at that point that you had been born in Mexico and not in the U.S?

Florizel:        I actually found out when I was already detained by ICE, because ICE was like, "Hey, you know what? You were born in Mexico." I was like, "No. I wasn't born in Mexico." He's like, "I need you to call your mom." I called my mom. They already knew I was detained, because I had a warrant for my arrest.

Anne:        You got in trouble?

Florizel:        Yeah. I was hanging out with this girl and she stole some credit cards. She was 12 at the time. I was 18. I decided to take the fault for it. I was like, "I don't want you to get in trouble by your parents," since her parents were celebrating their anniversary in Las Vegas. I was like, "Okay, I'll just take the fall. It's really no big deal." Then my parents knew that I was arrested. I called my mom and I was like, "Okay, mom." She was like, "Immigration's got you." I was like, "How do you know?" She was like, "You were born in Mexico. You're not born in California." I was like, "What the heck? Why would you lie to me this whole time?" I asked her about the green card. She was like, "It's all fake." I was like, "It has the holographic stamp on the back of the green cards. How the heck do you guys do that?" She's like, "You know what? You need to cooperate with them." Immigration was really hard with me though. They wanted-

Anne:        Is that when you got detained or deported?

Florizel:        Yeah. I actually volunteered to come back.

Anne:        When you were 18?

Florizel:        I turned 19 by then.

Anne:        You said you'd been working for Wendy's, but that was through high school you were working?

Florizel:        Yeah. I actually moved a lot. My first job was, it's called _____. It was a survey company. We would call people, asking if they wanted to take a survey. Then I worked for Wendy's for three years. Then from there I went to ______. It's this Mexican restaurant. Then from there I went to this other, it was called ________, also a survey company. Then from there I just worked with my dad. He works construction.

Anne:        You were working through high school with this fake green card?

Florizel:        Yeah.

Anne:        You were young when you got detained. You were young. You were 18. You must've been in shock.

Florizel:        It was really shocking for me. I was actually really confident that my dad was going to bail me out. Then when three weeks passed by, two weeks, and I was like, "What the heck? Why am I still here?" I would call my mom. I was like, "Mom." She's like, "We're going to come get you anytime. Don't worry. Just hang there." I would hear deputies come in and get all the inmates that were going home. Then I would ask them, I was like, "What about me?" Immigration didn't want me to leave the jail yet, because what they wanted was me and my mom's visa. They wanted all proof how it got into the United States. They would call me at 4:00 in the morning. They would call me at noon. One time they actually, they took me over to, it was the immigration center. They left me in the cell for almost the whole day without water, without food. It was really cold. Then I would ask them, I was like, "Hey, but I didn't do anything." Then they would just tell me to shut up freaking Mexican. They were really rude.

Anne:        Basically you were with a 12-year-old girl who was selling credit cards. You took the fall. They immediately said you're illegal.

Florizel:        The thing here that happened is that we were at Target, and the card wouldn't go through. Since I was a cashier back then in Wendy's, I was a cashier, I told her that if you put a bag over the card and slide it, it will go through.

Anne:        Sometimes it's the metallic thing that isn’t working.

Florizel:        I did it. Then that's when the camera, when they told me, when I went to court, they said that the camera caught me swiping the card. I told them, "You know what? Yeah, I did it."

Anne:        It was a stolen card?

Florizel:        It was a stolen card. I really thought it was her mom's card, but it was a stolen card. She didn't really never told me that she had a stolen card. I just swiped it. It was no big deal. She signed for it and everything like that.

Anne:        Then did they just catch you immediately?

Florizel:        They actually went to my job. I remember I think I was working cashier. Then they told me, they're like, "I need you to come down to the police station," and like, "You have to follow the cop." I was like, "Okay." They took me to the room. They were like, "Okay." They told me about the charges. They wanted to charge me as a pedophile also, because we were looking at clothes and then I slapped her butt. They thought that I was doing something else, so they wanted to charge me as a pedophile, until they talked to her mom. Me and her mom got really well along, so they dropped the charges. They told them that it was just play. They wanted to charge me with a lot of things. They even charged me with distributing alcohol to a minor when I was 18. I couldn't even get alcohol. It was just so many things.

Anne:        Trumped up. Where did they take you?

Florizel:        After when?

Anne:        When they detained you, or where were you detained?

Florizel:        With immigration?

Anne:        Yeah.

Florizel:        They took me first to the _________. That's when I guess Obama's law was that all the county jails were going to be ICE holding jails. I remember that they separated all the Mexicans from the whites. I was with the white people. I wasn't with the Mexicans. That was just weird.

Anne:        You weren't with Mexicans?

Florizel:        I wasn't with Mexicans.

Anne:        Were the whites criminals?

Florizel:        Yeah, they were.

Anne:        The Mexicans were illegals?

Florizel:        Uh-huh (affirmative), the illegals. I guess they didn't have anymore room. There were just more older women, probably in their 40s, 50s, grandmas.

Anne:        How long were you there?

Florizel:        I was in for a month.

Anne:        What was it like?

Florizel:        It would just be just like a girls camp, but with older women and druggies and pedophiles.

Anne:        When you say a girls camp, what do you mean?

Florizel:        It's a room filled with 10 bunk beds. Then you share a bathroom, just toilets. Then you share one shower. What else? They would wake us up at 5:00 in the morning to do a head count. Then from there we would have to wait until 7:00 to get our breakfast. Then from breakfast we would do the cleaning. After the cleaning, we would have a deputy come in with her gloves and check places that you wouldn't even think to clean. Then they would just make a mess and make us clean it all over again. We'd be outside at the courtyard. We would be walking. Sometimes we would be reading, watching TV. Obviously we couldn't watch the news. What else?

Anne:        You couldn't watch the news?

Florizel:        No.

Anne:        Interesting.

Florizel:        Yeah, because I guess there was this ... The reason why we couldn't watch the news is because you'll know who's going to be coming in sometimes or who got detained or what's going on in the world, I guess. We got to a point where we did watch one of the news, and we saw this lady that I guess she ... What did she do? I think she was babysitting a kid, a baby, and she shook it, because it wouldn't shut up, and I guess it died. She went in, into that jail where we were at, and she actually got beat up by all the inmates. Obviously, I didn't do anything to her. Sometimes when pedophiles would go in there too, they would just treat them like ... They would beat them up and stuff like that. It's traumatizing to see why people are there.

Florizel:        There was this lady I remember. She was fine. She would talk to you. She'd walk around with you. Then suddenly she would be like, "Oh, everything's burning down. There's blood all over the walls." They're just like, "What the heck? What's wrong with you?" I guess she saw at the moment when her son lit his room on fire, and the moment that she walked in, her son shot himself. That's what she saw, blood all over. She would relive that. They would tease her. They would hit her. They would take her to the, it's called the hole. It's a miniature cell. They would just keep her there. They were cruel to the people.

Anne:        That seems pretty cruel. Did you have someone representing you? Did you have a lawyer?

Florizel:        Yeah. My dad actually hired a lawyer. What he told me was that the process that my dad wanted to do is to keep me in jail, but keeping me in jail, I was going to become a U.S. citizen when I got out. I was going to do time.

Florizel:        I remember that it was April 2010. The judge, he told me, he's like, "Okay, we're going to have the next court date, but it's going to be until June." I was like, "Okay, but it's April. I don't want to be there for another month, two months." I was like, "Okay." Then I told the judge, I was like, "If I may," I'm like, "It's been really great being here in the United States. I've done so many things. I've visited so many places." I was like, "I think it's time for me to move on. I don't want to be locked up. We're talking about my freedom." I told him that, "I would like to go to Mexico, because I don't find any future here in the United States."

Florizel:        Then the lawyer was like, "Okay, that's not what your dad had planned for you." I told him, "I'm really done following my dad's rules. I really don't want to follow his rules anymore. I've been in situations in my family that aren't pleasant, so I decide that I wanted to move on by myself."

Florizel:        The judge, he said that there was a lot of good things about me that I've done in the United States, and he respected the decision that I'd made, and if that's what I really wanted, he'll go ahead and grant it. He still told me, he's like, "Are you sure?" I said, "Yes. I really don't want to be here no more." He said, "Okay. You leave tomorrow."

Anne:        They put you on a plane?

Florizel:        Yeah. I actually went on a plane. They actually kept me separated from all the inmates. They had me, not in first-class, but they were all in the back and I was just there. It was a long process to get deported, because first of all, they would wake you up at 4:00 in the morning. You would have to get dressed, but you're handcuffed from ankles and your wrists. Then they took us to Salt Lake City. We were in the cell since 6:00 in the morning to 10:00. Then we went to Salt Lake City, where we were in another cell with 60 other people. We were just all cramped up. Then from there I remember that we left around 2:00. We arrived in Arizona. Then we went to Las Vegas. Then from Las Vegas, we went to Texas. Then from Texas, we took a bus to the border.

Anne:        You were flying these other-

Florizel:        Yeah. Picking up more inmates.

Anne:        Then you took a bus.

Florizel:        Then we took a bus, all of us took a bus. They already had my backpack. My parents had sent me a backpack with, it was like my personal stuff. Then they took off the handcuffs and they told us where to walk. I remember when I was walking, I was crossing the border, I had to go to the bathroom. I went to the bathroom. Then this girl came after me, and she's like, "You know what? Give me all your stuff. I know you have money." She was already in the same plane that I was. She said that she needed all of it. I just looked at her like, "What the heck? You were riding with me. We were in the same jail, in the same cell. Why are you taking my stuff away?" She's like, "I know your dad gave you money." She's like, "I need everything right now." The first thing I did is I pushed her into a stall, I closed it, and then I just ran off. I just ran.

Florizel:        I had a Virgin Mobile at that time. I topped it up really quick with my ... I had a top-up card. I topped it up. Then I called my dad. I was like, "Dad, you know what? There's this girl after me." Then I was kind of keeping it calm. My dad was keeping it calm. My mom was all screaming and stuff. Then he told me, he's like, "Okay, so your aunt's going to meet, she's going to be meeting you there. I need you to call her." I called her and I told her, "Hey, I'm already here." She said, "Okay, if you walk, if you look at this ... " She told me the instructions. She's like, "You're going to see a fountain." I was like, "Okay, I'm already at the fountain." She's like, "Okay, I'll be there in 10 minutes." When she arrived, actually there was two girls, two ladies. They came up to me and she's like, "What do you need?" They were like, "We're going to go with her to the hotel." My aunt's like, "You know what? I'm just here to help out my niece. I can't help you guys out." She took me. She took me to a, I don't know if it was a two-star hotel, motel, but it was just horrible, but it was better than being in a cell. Then what else?

Anne:        Your parents, they were still illegal?

Florizel:        Yeah, my parents were still illegal. My mom actually became a citizen two years ago. My dad's still illegal.

Anne:        Do you think that was one of the reasons why they couldn't help you?

Florizel:        Yeah.

Anne:        He hired a lawyer, but he couldn't do a whole lot more, I would imagine.

Florizel:        Yeah. I remember my dad said, I guess they were asking for $10,000 in bail. He had the money. The only thing here is that they needed to sign off, like needing a cosigner. They asked my uncle for help, and he denied it. All my dad's family denied help. I didn't receive any help from them at all. They basically disowned me from the family. The most help that I've gotten is from my mom's side of the family, which they are actually here in Mexico.

Anne:        Was it because they were illegal too or do you know?

Florizel:        Actually, all of my dad's family are legal, because of one of my uncles. The only thing here is that they don't want to help my dad. I don't know why. I guess my dad's in the process of fixing his papers, but it takes time. It's been, what, nine years that I haven't seen my dad. I haven't seen my brother, but I've seen my two other sisters and my mom.

Anne:        You've been here for nine years?

Florizel:        Yeah.

Anne:        You walk over the border. You're trying to escape these people who are trying to steal all your stuff. You meet your aunt. Is she going to bring you home or she just put you up at the hotel and then left?

Florizel:        No. She lives in Texas. I think it's really 30 minutes away from the border. Her job was to put me somewhere safe, until the next day she took me to the bus station for me to come to Mexico City.

Anne:        What was it like when you walked over the border?

Florizel:        It was really embarrassing. There are so many people there, just waiting to cross the border, to get to the other side. They know what's going on. I'm walking across the border. You look over to your left or to your right. Then there's just people staring at you like if you're just some kind of criminal, like you're a piece of crap, just like you're not worth it. It's embarrassing, because they humiliate you. In my head I was just like, "Walk faster. Walk faster. Don't make any eye contact with anybody." The U.S. guards are actually walking you out with guns, rifles. What are you going to do? If I want to cross back, are you going to shoot me, or what are you going to do? It's just really humiliating for someone to walk over the border, across the border.

Anne:        Were you scared?

Florizel:        I was scared, because I don't know the place.

Anne:        You must've been having second thoughts, like, "Why did I decide to do this?"

Florizel:        Yeah. I was actually having second thoughts. I was like, "I should've just stayed. I should've just listened to my dad's lawyer. My dad always says everything for a reason, but here I am." Then I was like, "Okay, okay, just stop playing mind games. Just go with it."

Anne:        When your aunt helped you get to Mexico City, was there anyone in Mexico City that greeted you or helped you?

Florizel:        Yeah. Actually my aunt went to pick me up, my mom's oldest sister. She went to pick me up at the bus station. She received me. She hugged me. She told me everything's going to be okay.

Anne:        Did you live with her?

Florizel:        Hm?

Anne:        Did you live with her?

Florizel:        Yeah. I actually lived with her for a week. My parents actually helped out her son, both of her sons, when they went to the United States. My cousin Alex, he said why can't I go live with them? I told him, I was like, "Okay, that's fine. I'll go live with you." He felt like he needed to help me out, because he got help from us. That didn't go so well. I guess at that time that I arrived, his mother-in-law arrived, and she's from Chile. They're just different type kind of people. I guess she was looking at what I was doing the whole time. I guess there's some stuff that she didn't approve. She was the one that got me kicked out of his house. When they kicked me out of his house, my dad sent me to Cancun, supposedly for a better opportunity, which they're actually a little bit more racist than here in Mexico City, because basically Cancun is just a place that there's a lot of tourism. I've always been a heavy girl. They wouldn't allow me to work at their hotels because I was heavy or because they needed a really skinny girl.

Anne:        I was going to say you're so beautiful, they should be-

Florizel:        I actually would always compare myself to the other girls. The only thing that the other girls would have is just their image, not their face, because they were ugly, but they didn't know any English. Their English was all really, really choppy. I actually worked there as a teacher at a daycare. I was actually giving English lessons. At the first daycare that I worked at, the two teachers were ... Actually, one of the teachers was actually really, really rude to me. She would call me names. She would tell me that I was stupid, that I didn't know how to work. Her sister, which she was actually the owner of the daycare, she would always give me tips of how to do my job right. She would always congratulate me for everything that I've done. The other one was just-

Anne:        Maybe she was jealous.

Florizel:        I guess so. They would always have me work 10-hour shifts. Sometimes supposedly the daycare would close at 8:00, and I would leave around 12:00, 1:00 in the morning, and then walk to my house. It was just really hard working with them. I decided to go to a different daycare where I was actually wanted. That was actually really fun. It was really fun. I had so much fun working at that place. I learned a lot of things. I actually learned how to domain Spanish, because I really didn't know Spanish.

Anne:        I was going to ask you, how was your Spanish when you got back?

Florizel:        My Spanish was really choppy, really, really bad. I remember when I first went to the store, I was like, "Can I get a Gatorade?" They're like, "What?" My aunt's like, "What do you need?" I'm like, "A Gatorade." She's like, "Gatorade." I was like, "What? How do you say it?" That was actually one of my first goals is to domain both languages. I can speak really perfect Spanish and really perfect English.

Anne:        How long did that take you?

Florizel:        That took me three years for the Spanish. It was really hard.

Anne:        You'd had some. You'd been your mother's translator for a while back when you were little, so there must've been some.

Florizel:        The thing here is that it's not just learning Spanish or what I learned with my parents. Sometimes my mom will text me in Spanish. I'm just like, "Dude, your spelling is so bad. Everything's just so bad, your verbs and your everything." I have a sister that went to Ecuador for a mission. She'll text me in Spanish, and I'll be like, "Okay, that's not how you spell it." Sometimes I'll correct them, or when they say something, they don't pronounce it right in Spanish, and that's when I come in and, "This is how you say it," or I'll use big words, and my mom's like, "What did you just say?"
