Claudia:        Okay. So this is Claudia. I'm interviewing Sam right now. So the first thing that I want to say, and I want to thank you for coming out, talking with us. I understand that speaking about these experiences can sometimes be very difficult because they bring about memories and sentiments and emotions that you don't really want to feel or think about. Claudia:        Yeah. My first question for you is, why did your family leave the United States? You mentioned that you were six months old when you left.

Claudia:        What was your life like in the States?

Sam:        My life was pretty much like a roller coaster. I could say I went through a lot. I lived my life drastically into a lot of drama, gang violence due to the society and community I used to live in, which is Los Angeles County. That was pretty much it. I try to stay away from gangs and all that, that were influence. That was pretty much it. My life was studying. That was my mother's saying, your only responsibility until you're 18 is get your studies. After that, if you want to get a job and you're 18, you're sold to do so. You're above age. But as I've seen, I guess... I had a stepfather, so I was isolated from my mother due to she'd make more attention to his husband. So I had to find love that I don't have in the house at the streets.

Sam:        That's when I started involving myself into negativity. At the time, gang was my escape. But in reality, now that I'm at this age and I have my kids and I have a wife, we see that all that gang stuff, all that, it was not worth not one thing. We were actually claiming blocks that weren't even under our names. We used to put our lives on the front line for that. It was just something that, it was not necessary. I didn't see no benefit to it, but thanks God, I put my life straight. I got my kids. They're my motivation. They're my model to my heart, and as well as my wife. I thank my mom for her not letting me go like that, Because she was always persistent. Make sure that I get the programs, because I was in probation since I was 13.

Sam:        It's a cycle that it's unbreakable. That's what it's made for. Probation, it's a cycle that you can't break because you're still... As long as you're still living in that same place, in the same society, it's there. To stay away from negativity, it's hard. It's easier to be in negativity than being positive until I looked up to my mom and my mom, I see my mom tearing, and what was going on with my sister, because my sister, she was in bad steps as well. So I was following her thinking I was doing the right thing, but then I've seen the reality of how much damage I was doing to my family.

Sam:        When I was 16, I opened my eyes and I told my mom, "Look, mom, I know I'm not living with you, but I love you and know that I'm not in negativity no more. I'm actually doing good. And I hope that you could forgive me, but I'm always going to be your son." My mom's always been there for me. She has never let me down. Since I was 16, all the way to 18, I stayed good. And then now I'm here and I have a great communication with my mother. I'm a mama's boy and I'm always going to be. That's pretty much it right here.

They provided me the last alternative option, volunteer deportation. That's what I took because I prefer my freedom than giving my time to just thrown away. So I took the easiest route, and came to Mexico.

Sam:        I've been here... I think on November, I'm making six years.

Sam:        Well, it was difficult in the beginning. I was depressed for two years. I couldn't overcome knowing that I was right here and my family over there. I couldn't do nothing. I felt useless in potential that just the border could actually do so much things, so much emotions. Well, little by little, I had to get the vision that I'm never going to go back over there. That's what I set my mind to. I set my mind that whatever I had over there, I'm going to have it right here. I'm already paying my house. I'm working on getting a car, and that's pretty much it. My family has been supportive. My mother has been supportive. I don't tell them to send me money over there because as well, they cannot. The wealth is difficult over there and that's pretty much it.

Claudia:        Okay, so I want to know... I'm going to backtrack a little bit. I apologize. You were in County jail for nine months, you said in your survey. Tell me briefly how the conditions were there, how they treated you.

Sam:        Well, the food was horrible. Beans with worms, white worms. I wouldn't eat beans. I'll just eat... The best thing you could get, it was a bologna sandwich. Even the mustard, the mayonnaise will come expired, even if it was on a little bag. So, I wouldn't eat, just the plain bologna. There would be rot food or just not nutritious enough, so you can actually go on day by day. But if you have commissary, yes, you will actually survive. The aspect of them providing you hygiene, it was all right. Yeah, it was all right for the hygiene.

Sam:        Well, the riots were really pretty intense in there. I’ll probably get..they'll come with the pepper spray grenades. When you're in there, well, you got to take care of business. You can't speak much about it. If you're on a side, well, you got to take your side to the fullest. And if it comes to the point, and if the big man calls shots, you can't stop until it's done. That's pretty much it. You got to take care of business in there. That's how I'll most define, I could put it. You got to take care, and you come in there and you're involved in a gang, you got to just come in there and take care of business pretty much.

Claudia:        So you kept being involved in gang activity while you were in the jail?

Sam:        It's a must. It's a must.

Claudia:        Okay. So now coming back to when you came back to Mexico, tell me a little bit more about that whole aspect of family separation and how that was for you.

Sam:        Well, the family separation, because I got deported, and the first thing I did was call my mom. That's the only supportive person I know, the only person that's going to respond to you, even when you're 40, 60, 80 years old. As long as your mom is still living there, she's going to respond. That's what I did, called my mother, had the response, and she stated, "Everything's going to be okay. They're going to come pick you up." And yeah, sure enough. They came to pick me up. Well, I had a bad experience in TJ. Got kidnapped. They asked my mother for amount of money, if not, they were going to start chopping me up. So they shot me in the hand and they sent pictures to my mother. They thought I was playing. They thought that we're playing. But then when they see the tattoos on my hand and my finger thrown off, so my mom started getting scared. I told her, "Don't send money."

Sam:        They kept on torturing me and torturing me and shot me in the leg and then hit me with a bat in the face. Then while I was unconscious, I was literally nobody no more. I was just like a blanket just thrown on the street. A lady picked me up. I remember an old lady picked me up and she called a taxi. Taxi came to pick me up, took me on a general hospital, and they put me on anesthesia. When I woke up, I was in a coma for a week, and I called my mom. I still knew my mom's phone number. They came to pick me up. That same day, I got shipped off to Mexico City in Mexico. I've been good since then.

Claudia:        Where did the kidnapping happen? Was it in TJ?

Sam:        Yeah. That was in TJ, Baja California, and on the front line. Once you cross border your vehicle, right down in front of the McDonald's, they'll come and kidnap you. They're just waiting for people that get transported. They'd look at them with good clothing. They'll see if you're a tourist and they'll persuade you. The moment they see your weakness, they'll grab you from there and then just put you on a vehicle, and they'll do what they do.

Claudia:        Who was it?

Sam:        A criminal organization. I really don't know.

Claudia:        How long did they have you for?

Sam:        About 48 hours.

Claudia:        All of that in 48 hours?

Sam:        Yeah. No eating. Yeah. They do that just to hunger you. They just want you to give up the money. They're like, "Call your mother." I'm like, "I did, she don't got money." [Spanish 00:18:09]. Like, "I ain't got nobody. That's the only phone number in there." Bang. There goes the first gunshot. And then, "We know you got money." I'm like, "I ain't got no money." So just to tell them [Spanish 00:18:25]. "I'm all by myself with you guys, if there's the money, you've seen it." They just stated, "No, you got money". And there goes the other shot. Then I got hit with a bat. And then at the end, I just got beat up and I'm shot. Thanks God, I survived my gunshot wounds. That was pretty much it.

Claudia:        What's been the hardest part of returning to Mexico for you?

Sam:        Not seeing my mom, that kills me, my brothers. Me not being there for them, me not being a role model for them, me not being there for my mother and for my sister. So that’s pretty much it. That was the reason I was over there. That was the reason. In my part, because I wanted to succeed in there, I have my passport, I have my California ID. Unfortunately, that was not the circumstances. I'm here and I have to make something out of my life.
