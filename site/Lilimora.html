Interviewee

Claudia

Interviewer

June 14, 2018

Mexico City, Mexico

Lilimora:        Okay.

Claudia:        Okay. So this is Claudia, and I'm interviewing Lilimora, whose ID number is Lilimora 323. So first of all I want to say thank you for coming here and speaking with us. I understand that talking about this experience can sometimes be a little hard, especially because you're reliving and retelling stories and emotions that you might not necessarily want to, but I think it's good to know how important your story is. And how important it is for people to hear it so that they can understand the human side of migration and the realities.

Lilimora:        Yes, that's correct. No problem.

Claudia:        So I guess my first question is why did you leave Mexico?

Lilimora:        Why did I what?

Claudia:        Why did you leave Mexico to go to the States?

Lilimora:        Oh. Because my parents separated when I was 10 years old. So we ended up living with my dad. He's the one that took us to Los Angeles actually. And from that time, when I was 10, I'm 32 years old, we never came back. We live over there. We went to school, and we did our life over there.

Claudia:        Yeah. I'm going to put this a little bit further out so that I can pick you up better. So do you remember your first day in the United States?

Lilimora:        Yes. We got there to Los Angeles and it was late, and I remember my aunt because she was already there. She gave us some food and put us in a room, me and my two sisters we went to sleep. And wake up next day to a new life. And it was okay, I guess. But I didn't like school.

Claudia:        You didn’t?

Lilimora:        No, because I didn't speak English, and everybody did. And it was kind of hard the first six months. I didn't want to go to school at all. But then I guess I did get used to it and learn. Because they give you the ESL classes.

Claudia:        The ESL?

Lilimora:        Yeah. So that was good and it helped out a lot. And that was it.

Claudia:        Do you remember any teachers or friends that you had that really helped you?

Lilimora:        No, I just remember the ESL teacher was really patient with me, especially. Because I would cry out of frustration. I guess I put more effort because I started watching TV in English every afternoon. And I learned. I know I have an accent, but that's just me. But I know English. I did speak English.

Claudia:        And so how was your life in the States?

Lilimora:        It was good and bad. You know like everything. But I always thought... like I went to, I finished high school over there. I did the normal things like everybody does. Goes to the mall. Watch a movie. Everything, like normal stuff. I had my friends. A boyfriend, and break up. And then I started working. I bought a car. I change another car. But I never had a license. So that was my first when I notice, “Oh, I am different.” Me and my two sisters. My two sisters are younger than me. So they were like eight and five when they got there, so they speak English better, and they were thinking they're from over... They didn't have no clue that it was going to be a problem later on. But since I was the older one, I mean I knew, I knew because my dad. I knew. But my dad said, "It's okay. Just get a car and somebody else will get it under their name or whatever, and I will make the payments." And I did. I changed cars a lot.

Lilimora:        And I had good jobs I think. I started working in places where I used to cook in the kitchen for three years, and then I changed to work in a check cashing place where I worked for seven years, and cashed checks, send money to anywhere like Mexico. But I never sent money myself to Mexico, because I never thought I was going to be back. And in reality, my family, my uncles, my tia. My kids I have to give over there too. Everybody. So, I was living like a normal life. My dad bought a house. I help him to buy a house in Los Angeles. My dad, he has a business even. But no papers either. He doesn't have papers.

Claudia:        What is the business?

Lilimora:        It's sewing machines. He’s a mechanic, so he has a lot of sewing machines. And he sells them, or repair the sewing machines. He is really good at it. And I love my dad. Yeah.

Claudia:        What was your favorite job in the United States?

Lilimora:        The check cashing place. Because I liked to do that. Cashing checks, giving money orders. We sell a lot of the lotto tickets, the scratchers. I liked that job a lot. You know? It gives me a lot of satisfaction when I used to cash checks for people that didn't have no IDs, and we make them the IDs and they would cash it every week. You get to know a lot of people.

Claudia:        And so your two kids. Are they American citizens?

Lilimora:        Mm-hmm (affirmative). Yes they are. But they're over there. When I got deported, my dad told me I was not going to take the kids, because why would I do that when I'm going to struggle over here? And I am struggling a lot.

Claudia:        How old are they?

Lilimora:        They are right now, they are 10 and eight.

Claudia:        So tell me how you ended up back in Mexico.

Lilimora:        Well, I guess when I was younger, my 20s or whatever, like I said, I always drive car. I drive a car. And I crash a time. But I never had a license or... They took me to jail because I crash and I didn't have a license, so that was one thing. But that was a like couple of years ago. And I never went back to court or anything, because I thought, well, why would I go back if I don't have a license? By that time, they were not pushing on the deportation. They were not doing deportations or anything, so I went to jail and got out in a week. And I thought, this is fine. I just ran across. And I did. But we never change address or nothing.

Lilimora:        And they had my information so I got stopped by the cops again for no license and got my cars taken away in the impound. I never went again. A couple times. So why wasn't I registered? They had my information, I guess, and when it was everything like Trump and all that, when they were doing deportations, they started going through the list of people that owns any kind of things like that. And I was one of them. And immigration went and picked me up at my house. And I was there, and I gave myself up because there's most people living there. They didn't have no papers. So they said, "If you don't give yourself up, we're going to go in and take everybody that has no papers." And why would I affect everybody? So I did.

Claudia:        Was it your family that lived in that house?

Lilimora:        My family, and besides them, my dad rents... He has the garage, like and then he has another little house out in the back. My dad has with his house. He rents those places to people. He help people have “no fucking” job. I'm sorry. People has no papers.

Claudia:        Yeah.

Lilimora:        So they were going to search and the thing with the garage is not legally converted. You know? It has to be for cars, and my dad rent that place for people. So it was going to be all bad. So I just given myself up. And I fought my case for a year and a half in detention.

Claudia:        How was detention?

Lilimora:        Detention wasn't bad. It wasn't bad at all. They provided me a lot of things. My dad pay a lawyer to fight the case. But we went to court three times out of this year and a half. We went three times to court, and the judge always deny me my bond, because my dad was post bond. Even my dad wanted to put his house. [Emotional] And the judge didn't want. He said I was in trouble because I crash. At that time I crash with my car and I left. But I left because I didn't have a license. I didn't hurt nobody. I crashed to a tree. You know? So therefore, we fought the case for a year and a half. And when I saw that nothing was happening, I just signed to go to Mexico. That's it. [Emotional] And I've been here for a year and a half. I don't like it… I'm sorry.

Claudia:        No, it's okay. Well, if you're comfortable, can you tell me a little bit about what the past year has been like in Mexico?

Lilimora:        It's been sucks. Because, a lot of people that live here, they have their life. They always been here. I know in Mexico there's an amount who you know and who you grow up with, and some of these are yours. Even despuestos, you know. I know a lot of people because I'm a social person. I don't really have a social person. I'm always happy or try to see the best thing out of things. [Emotional] But once you start speaking English, they don't like you. Or they treat you different. They don't help you.

Lilimora:        Here to live somewhere, I stay in the hotels. I stay in a hotel every day. Every day. I want to rent an apartment. Maybe I don't have the whole money or whatever, but they want the first deposit, the second rent, and I don't know how they call it, but someone else to sign for you. Who's going to do that here? They don't know you. Who's going to give you a name or whatever, to say that you're good to pay? Nobody. Because they don't know you. They don't mess. You're not part of their circle. And a lot of people here is like that. Especially here in the city, like they don't... I sometimes have been short with money, a couple of pesos or whatever, and they're like, "No like we cannot like we can do nothing about it. Go away." Or things like that. You know?

Lilimora:        It’s a couple pesos…  To me, even when you go to a place, and or you go with people, that say like in TeleTech and there’s a lot of people they look like “Oh, they're homeless." Right? And they work in Teletech. And call centers. And you go to a place, and they look at you different. You order food, and you're the last one to get your food. And they're always doing things like that to people here. I think there's a lot of discrimination here for us. A lot. They don't like us. And I feel like it's because they hate me or sometimes because they think that we had the opportunity to do something else, and we didn't. And now we're here taking the jobs, or taking their whatever. But yeah.

Lilimora:        I think there's a lot of discrimination. And the worst thing is that I know that we're not better, but our mentality is different because we live over there. And we can't do so many things. Bad depression. A lot of people has depression when they don't even know they do have depression. Because you leave everything there. And then people treats you like shit. But that's my experience. You know? My experience has been bad, really bad. And people here me robaron el teléfono. They never know my phone got stolen with me. I've been with my luggage everywhere. I try to keep my job but it's hard to stay a stable place. I've been late to work and I get fired for two times I've been late because I need to pay my room first.

Lilimora:        And I try not to call my dad for nothing for money. My dad sends me money, here and there. That's $50. $100 sometimes. Pero, I try not to ask him, and when he calls me, like when I call him and, "I'm okay. I'm okay." But I know I'm not okay. Because he has my two kids. Why would I ask him for more? And as long as they're with him, I'm good. And that's it.

Lilimora:        And there's no shelters. I want people will have a shelter. It’s like right here. Maybe a shelter for woman and some shelter for men. To help you for I don’t know a month, maybe. Where you can work and have a place to get a locker maybe. And you can save that money. And then get a place, because that's what’s been my problem. I don't have a place to stay. And I know I can get a job. I know I can get a job, but I lost all my papers. They were with my luggage. My Act de Nacimiento, my CURP,  all that we need right here. And I lose it because I leave my luggage in an internet café, or in the puestos de carnitas. You know? Can I please leave my things here? Things like that.

Lilimora:        Pero, I think it's necessary, a place where there's a shelter for immigrants like us. Like for woman, that has a couple lockers where we can leave our things for a month. I just need a month and somewhere to stay, and I don't have to pay rent on nothing, and then I think I can make it like that. It's been hard. Every day I have to pay my room. Right now I haven't paid my room. 200 is going to be for my room. 180 they charge. Now it's raining every day and it sucks. Like my clothes looking. I'm wearing this because I don't have my clothes. And now sometimes I look ridiculous, but I just have to keep pushing. And do my thing. But it's hard.

Claudia:        How has it been to be separated from your family?

Lilimora:        Bad. Really bad. Like I said, there's a lot of depression even. I'm not a depressed person. I'm not. Every day I see something good, and I think, okay, it's a new day. It's going to be good. And I do generate money. However, I'm a really active person. But it's bad, and I would think every peso. You have to earn it. Here you have to earn every peso. Every peso. And every peso is like, I have 20 pesos and I eat. And you have to drink something and you have to pay the room too, where you stay. And it's difficult. It's difficult, but I know it's going to be good. I guess.

Lilimora:        And when you steal, and in the states even if you don't have no money, because you grow up over there, so you have a community that knows you, your family, your dad. My dad was always stay there, and he knows people, and I know people. And he has a house in the States. All our life we live there. We invest money on the house. Neighbors know us. You know? Our neighbors are Chinese. Family that we know. Next door is the Guadalajara people from Guadalajara. You know that they know us. Anything, any problem, I will have, I will run to them and they will help out because they know with them.

Lilimora:        And right here's not like that. It's not like that. It's not like that. That's bad. We need to have a community of immigrants or something that will help each other out or something, because they don't have… A lot of people, they make new families here. I've seen people I know a lot of people from TeleTech, the call centers. It's different because they do say, "Hi," whatever. They speak English. Or any person that speak English, I get attached to, because I feel at home. But sometimes that's bad too, because not everybody has the same intentions.

Claudia:        Yeah. So you work at TeleTech?

Lilimora:        I used to work there.

Claudia:        You used to. Are you working somewhere?

Lilimora:        I'm not working nowhere. I don't have my papers either. And it's been two weeks because I got fired because I was late two days. But I was try to tell them I don't have a place to stay. Like I have to secure my room first. And they'd say, "When you have a place where to stay and you're more, you know? Then come back and we can give you a job." So I know I can get a job. But now I don't have my papers, and he's just like, "Eh."

Claudia:        Do you know how to get them, or are you-

Lilimora:        Yeah. I know. I know how. I know how. I just going to have to go to the - I know places already in Buena Vista. You just go there. And they will get it for you in two weeks. In two weeks you will get your INM. And the Acto de Nacimiento, I know I have to go to the quiosco, but I have to pay 28 pesos, which I don't have 28 pesos. And the court, you can get it on the internet. Things like that I can get. And the job is... I like working out there. Call centers. I'm good on the phone. On the phone I'm really good. Customer service. I like customer service.

Claudia:        Why do you like it so much?

Lilimora:        Because I like to help people. And the customer service. Once I'm on the phone, I'm good. I have another call center two stations away. I ride over to one. Then I was there for six months when I just got here. And they liked me a lot. Pero, and I don't want to go there when I'm looking like this, and all stressed out. And I'm all skinny and everything. Because I don't have a place. And even if I had place, like I said for a month, then I'll be good. Fine.

Claudia:        Do you know where you're staying tonight?

Lilimora:        In the hotel, because I'm going to go pay 180 right now. And that's where I'm going to stay for-

Claudia:        And that's what you've been doing every day for a year?

Lilimora:        Mm-hmm (affirmative). For like six months.

Claudia:        Six months?

Lilimora:        Mm-hmm (affirmative). The other six months I work in another call center, and I was good there. But I had a boyfriend where I was staying with. And I had this boyfriend, I'm telling you, I had this boyfriend that I met at TeleTech. Because he knows English and speak English, and I thought, okay, he's good. And I got attached to people. I'm telling you, I got attached to people real quick and I think they're from over there. Are you from Los Angeles too? Oh yeah. But it was bad.

Lilimora:        I was staying with him and he used to hit me. Domestic Violence. And all these bad things. So I just got away from him too. That's why I'm on the street, because he was like, "Woman, leave the house." And so a lot of things and I didn't go. I didn't go because I didn’t have a place to stay, but he start hitting me more. Didn't let me talk to my kids. So I say what am I doing with him. When he's treating me like that. I was over there. I've been through a lot of things. And I'm not going to let this go. Treat me like shit. Pero, I have to leave. He said, "Well, then. Leave my house." And I left. And it's been hard, as you can tell. Because I think I was going to stay with him because I don't have a place to stay. Pero, it's okay.

Claudia:        Did you ever denounce him to the police?

Lilimora:        No. No, because it was the same thing. I don't believe in the police here, because they're all not good. They don't do shit. He came out of jail for a couple of hours, then be out. And do it again. And just pay them. Here the police is no good for nothing. They have seen him here. Where they have seen us fight not once, and they didn't do nothing. They told me,  “He didn't hit you." And I'm like, "Look what he's done to me. He's going to kill me" and things like that. And they're like, "Just go away. Don't tell him nothing. Just go away so he can go away. Just go away." That's not good enough. It's stupid. They don't do nothing here for nobody. Until they see you learning and happy.

Lilimora:        So I just decided to stay away from him. And that's what I'm doing. But it was hard. Sometimes I want to go back, just for him to let me stay. And I walk past. Sometimes you get attached to people just because you feel like, oh you know. They're from the same place you are. And it's not... I learned the hard way that... Not because I speak English or whatever they come from, they're good people. They're not. They try to take advantage of you too. Because they're going through the same thing, without them knowing. I even understand, because I feel like he's going through the same shit, and because he misses his family, and he goes through depression, but he doesn't know. Se desquita conmigo. Pero, why would I suffer no more? Six months, and he wasn't good enough for me to notice. And he will make me feel like I'm not worth it if I don't live in there. But I know I'm worth it. But that's why it's been hard.
