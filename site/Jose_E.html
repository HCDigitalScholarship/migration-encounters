Jose Rolando

Interviewee

Sergio Diaz

Interviewer

June 1, 2019

Mexico City, Mexico

Sergio:        All right. So, my name is Sergio, I'm here conducting an interview. Thank you, Jose De Santiago Perez, for joining us today. I first want to start off by thanking you for being part of this interview. I know it's not an easy thing to be able to talk about your experience, share your story, share everything you've been through. And we really want to thank you for that and acknowledge you for everything that you're embracing, for being open about it, for being so welcoming. So, I guess the first question that I have for you is, why did your family migrate to the US?

Jose:        Well, my family migrated to the US because, like I mentioned earlier, my father used to beat my mother. According to her, there came a time where she couldn't take it anymore and she decided to leave my father. But, she couldn't go just next door, to the next town, because she knew my father and how Mexico works, was influenced in the political game. And, she knew she couldn't get away from him just going next door, to the next town. So, she decided to go head north, and that's how we ended up in America.

Sergio:        And was your hometown violent where you grew up?

Jose:        Oh, I grew up as a little kid just remember playing. I really don't remember anything about- the only violence I knew was when I used to see my father beat my mom. Because, I was young, but I still remember seeing my father beat my mom. So, that's the only violence that I encountered in Mexico at that time.

Sergio:        That's tough. When you and your mom left, what did you leave behind? What do you remember of Mexico that you ...

Jose:        The only thing that I can miss from Mexico was obviously my childhood friends. Because, my mother just didn't bring me. She brought all of us. Me and my siblings. So, when I look to the right, I see my brothers and sisters. My mother decided to head north first, so that was something that we missed first was my mother because she wasn’t in the scene for a month. So, when we headed north, we met up with my mom.

Sergio:        So, she left you in your home town?

Jose:        Yes, she left us without us actually knowing because we were kids. So, if there was something I was looking forward to, it was seeing my mom as a kid.

Sergio:        She did that to protect you and your siblings?

Jose:        Yes.

Sergio:        How old were you? You said you were ...

Jose:        Yeah. I remember I was about five going on six.

Sergio:        Do you remember your first memory in the US?

Jose:        My first memory in the US, I actually remember the road to America, and I remember getting the bus from Mexico. Somebody instructed us to play asleep, and then we woke up. We actually, trying to play asleep, fell asleep and woke up in the road. And hamburgers was what we had for lunch. And that was my first taste of America, because I had never ate hamburgers.

Sergio:        That's amazing.

Jose:        And they were just, "Are we there yet?" And they informed us that, "When you see some buildings, we're there." And then eventually we got to see the skyline of Dallas, Texas, and we're like, "We must be here." And then that's when we met up with my mother. And that's actually my first memory out of America.

Sergio:        So, did you go to school in the US?

Jose:        Yes, I went to school in the US. I did from first grade all the way to high school. Yeah.

Sergio:        Is there any teachers or people that you remember from school?

Jose:        I definitely remember my teachers because I didn't know English when I arrived in the US, so I always had to have bilingual teachers. I guess she was my second grade teacher, her name was Miss Rodriguez. And I actually had to have her two years because I had the opportunity to bump up to third grade when I was supposed to go to the third grade, but they didn't have a bilingual teacher. So, they suggested that I stay in second grade, so I got held back in the second grade. And Miss Rodriguez played a big role in that, because she suggested it. And that's why I remember Miss Rodriguez. She was in my elementary.

Jose:        And middle school, I remember Miss Lewis. When I was in middle school I was already hanging out with the wrong people, the wrong crowd. Gangs, drugs. And Miss Lewis always just kind of taught us to, "If you're already street smart because you're in the streets, be book smart too because this is where you get your book smart." So, that's one thing I always remember about her. According to her, "I'm not going to stop y'all from doing what y'all go to do. Y'all already got that in y'all because y'all in the streets. Y'all can spot a gang from a mile away. But, I need you to spot these books from a mile away too. Because, without it, you ain't going to be able to do nothing." So, that was Miss Lewis in my middle school days.

Jose:        And then in high school, we got Miss, I can't even remember her name. But, she was an old vet teacher. And she was real strict on us because she made sure we knew our history as far as Blacks and Mexicans go. Our people didn't have it easy, so why do we throw away what we have in front of us. So, that was something that she really made sure she touched. And, that's part of being in America.

Sergio:        So, you mentioned you started getting involved with the wrong crowd. One of the things for me, I grew up in Pomona, California. I was born in the US and I've grown up in a neighborhood that was also really challenging. I have a lot of friends that ended up in jail and shot and got killed. And so, when I was in middle school, that's when I was hanging out with people that might've not been the best of friends, getting into fights. Was there ever a moment where you thought ... when was the moment that you realized, "Oh wow, I'm part of a gang, oh wow, I'm involved in the wrong crowd"? What led up to that?

Jose:        Well, I never had that wow moment, "I'm part of this." I just had that, "I want to be part of this." And when I was part of it, I wasn't like, "Wow, I'm part of it." Because, you work your way to that. So, when you work your way to that, it's like you deserve it because you've done whatever you had to do to be at that. So, there was never like a, "I'm part of this." You just know you are, regardless of whatever anybody says, because you know there's rules you've got to go by. And when you do them, you're in. People can say you're not, but you know you are.

Jose:        I've been in the streets since I was like seven years old, and I got here when I was like six or five. So, I've just been running the streets as a kid. I just grew up in the streets as a kid. I remember my mother, single mother of five in America without any help from the American government because we're not American. So, therefore we have no rights to nothing as far as help from the government. So, we don't get no food stamps. We don't get no medical. I don't even know as far as medical goes, but we wouldn't get no government cheese like other people did. And a single mother of five, I had to help my mother. I remember as a kid, probably I was like eight, and mopping the neighborhood Washeteria. Because, the lady used to give me 2, 3, dollars. But, because my mother came from work, two jobs and she used to sleep, and there's no milk for my baby sister, so I kind of, out of my kid zone, go try to find some money to buy milk for my little sister. So, behind me not having a father at home, I was able to be in the streets all day. And I had to do what I had to do.

Sergio:        While doing what you had to do, was that more like a friend group thing that you found? Or, was that more of like, you knew you were looking to be part of?

Jose:        Well, when I arrived from Mexico, my family are from Mexico, and usually what they do is work. So, they're going to teach you anything that's work. So, first I started off as working. I used to help what we call a hobo, that cleans cars at the car wash. I used to help him dry the cars. And he used to give me 2, 3 dollars. So, I'm helping mop the Washeteria, and over here. So, that was part of working. So, before you know it, I started hanging out with the wrong crowd. It's no more working. It's stealing. So, instead of going to pay for a gallon of milk, I steal a gallon of milk. So, that's how I started hanging with the wrong crowd. And it just grows and grows. Your morals disappear because you practice it so much that it's just like, there's no shame. Somebody said, "You still ain't in?"

Sergio:        Is that any different from other people that were US citizens that you knew?

Jose:        Well, they were doing the same thing, because the majority of my buddies were US citizens. And, we were doing the same thing. I grew up as an American. They call themselves Mexicans too. There was no dividing us. Until you get to a certain age that they start asking me for, "Where's your social?" "Let me ask my mom."

Sergio:        You worked in the US?

Jose:        Yes.

Sergio:        What did you work in?

Jose:        My first job in the US, you could say legit job was as a busboy. I still remember the name of the restaurant. It's called Cheddar's. And awesome food, as a busboy. But, to work there, I had to go find some, what you call papers. And I got me the fake identity with my real name though. And I started working as an 18 year old when I was 14, as a part-time job. Because, the demand in my household was big. My mother was still a single mother. Yeah, so I just had to help out. And that was my first job, at 14, working at Cheddar's. It has a bar. Where everybody thinks that I'm 18 not 14. I grew up pretty fast too as far as my structure. So yeah man, that was an experience too that I lived.

Sergio:        So, what was your favorite job?

Jose:        My favorite job was, my older brother, I have an older brother, he's two years older than me. He started working at a tire shop and he told me, "You want to get your-" I forgot exactly the words, but insinuating that you want to be on your own? "You want to do what you want to do? Get you a job. Come work." So, I decided to go help my brother on the weekends at the tire shop. I picked up on the tire shop business, and I actually enjoyed it. And that's actually, to this day, I love changing tires. You don't have to pay me to change a tire because, I don't know, I just like it. I don't know why. I just like it. So, that's actually my favorite job.

Sergio:        Have you thought about making a business here in Mexico with the tires?

Jose:        Yes. I have looked into it. I thought about it. But, there's no funds. So, without funds you really can't. And if anybody knows about the tire shop business, there's money behind a tire shop business. And, I haven't just been a worker. I mean, I have been a worker, an employee. But, I've also been a manager of a tire shop, so I know a little bit more about how the business actually works. And there's money in a tire shop business. I love doing it. Why not make money with something you love to do?

Sergio:        So, what was your least favorite thing about the US?

Jose:        My least favorite thing about the US was going to jail. I love everything about America. Oh, and FYI, in Mexico, they only say it's one America. So, we call the US America, and Mexicans call the whole, they say Mexico and America are America. So, we call the US America.

Sergio:        All right. Least favorite thing was jail. How did you end up in jail the first time?

Jose:        Well, the first time I ended up in jail, my mother called me, I was working at the tire shop. She's like, "What did you do last night?" I'm like, "Nothing. Why? What happened?" She's like, "Well, some detectives came looking for you." And I said, "For what?" She said, "You tell me. Well, they left a card for you to call." So, I called them and they said that it wasn't me. And I said, "All right, well they got the wrong person because I didn't do nothing. I was at work." And before you know it, they pay me a visit and they pick me up. The police picks me up and they take me in for questioning. I was out on a previous bond for possession of heroin. And when they picked me up, it violated my possession of heroin, and they booked me. So, I was incarcerated. And that's how I ended up in jail.

Jose:        So, they booked me together for the possession of heroin and aggravated robbery that I did not commit. So, before you know it, I'm being charged with possession of heroin and aggravated robbery. And that's how I ended up in jail. And I had to prove my innocence. And I wasn't successful at it, so I took probation. And they let me out on probation. That's how I ended up in jail at 17 and got out when I was 18.

Sergio:        So, you came in when you were 17 and you came out when you were 18?

Jose:        Yes.

Sergio:        And then what happened next?

Jose:        After that, I was on probation. I was into drugs and I was into heroin. And I started consuming again. And I knew I was dirty, so why go, oh, because they had me with the piss test or UAs twice a month. And I knew I was dirty, so I'm like, "Why go check in when I know I'm dirty?" So, I stopped reporting. So, when you stop reporting, they put out a warrant for you. At 18 I decided to flee to _____, to work at a tire shop over there too, because I was in the tire shop. And I started a new life in _____. And that's what happened. Broke my probation and I was living in _____.

Sergio:        So, one thing, there's always a big stereotype about Mexican American men being in the US committing crimes and all of these things. What would you attribute the reason you became involved with gangs or with [inaudible 00:20:21]?

Jose:        Well, today as a grown man, back then I used to blame it on my father because I grew up without a father. And my cousins around me had a father and they walked a straight line, according to what I was seeing with my eyes. But, today I come to realize that it's not because of a father, because I also know people that don't have a father, and you can say they walk a straight line. And today I'm biblically influenced, and the Bible helps me walk a straight line. And when there's no biblical influence in a person’s life, there's no direction. So, that's my answer to that.

Sergio:        What are some of the favorite things you got out of the US? Favorite things you liked to do?

Jose:        I mean, there's too much to, everything. I mean, I lived in America and we went swimming. We went to field trips. Buddies of mine invited me to their house parties. There's parks. Just everything about America is my favorite. I really don't have a favorite. America is my favorite. Whether it's good or bad, you enjoy it. I don't really know how to explain it. Now that I can see, you don't realize this when you're in America. But, when you're out of it, you're like, "Whoa." I always considered myself as a Mexican because I was born in Mexico. So, when I had my American buddies, as far as US citizens, I'm like, "You're not Mexican. I'm Mexican. I'm an original Mexican. So, I used to make fun of them because they claim they're Mexican. I said, "No, you're not. I am."

Jose:        When you got deported the first time, I come to realize that I was not a Mexican, as I used to claim. Why? Because, Mexico rejected me. They told me, "You're not a Mexican." In Mexico, they call us gabachos, gueros [foreigners, pale skinned], whatever. And we're like, no, because we know that in our mind that's a white person, and we're not white. We're Mexican. But, it doesn't come to settle in until you're in another country. I'm like, "But, I used to tell my buddies I was a Mexican. But, I come into another world and tell me I'm not a Mexican." And that's when I came to realize, I'm a full-fledged American. I just never realized it. So, yeah man, it's just ...

Sergio:        What kind of things did you do that made you feel American? Or, what kinds of things do you still do?

Jose:        America is a culture, a people, and a home. That's what America is to me. This is my culture, it's my people and it's my home. So, I only found that in America. So, when I come to Mexican, I don't find no culture, I find no people, so therefore there's no home. America's all I know, and I find it extremely hard to let go. Because, without it, then what am I? This is who I am. It'd be denying who I am. I go by Jose De Santiago in America, and I come down here to go by Jose Rolando De Santiago Perez. That's real weird. And to this day I still practice with my name, Jose De Santiago. But, legally I got to write Jose Rolando De Santiago Perez, because that's how it goes down here. If I don't, I make it harder for myself because it's a different person. And then when you try and get my papers in order, it doesn't add up. It's the math. You want to struggle or you want to get it right? So, I decided to get it right, because I've been getting it wrong for far too long. So yeah.

Sergio:        So, what was… you were released and then how were you deported? You were deported twice?

Jose:        Yes.

Sergio:        How were you deported the first time?

Jose:        My first time deported was one of the most hardest thing I ever encountered, because I was incarcerated to begin, on a three-year sentence. I did it all the way to the door, meaning I pulled the whole three, out of the state of Texas. And be I left, the last month, everybody thought I was an American. So, when I used to tell my buddies, "Oh, I'm going to Mexico." Like, "What?" My incarcerated buddies. So, now they started referring me to people that are from Mexico. So, I started looking into the matter, and they start lacing me up with just information. But, no kind of help for useful information. If anything, fearful information, because they started telling me about how the cartels, how they prey on people like myself. And all the illegal activity going on in Mexico.

Jose:        So, I'm involved in gangs at the time, so I want to act like none of this affects me. But, inside it does because it's just a reality that I'm going to a country that I'm not familiar with. And not just that, the violence is at its highest at the time. So, I was actually frightened to leave. I was in no rush to get out of jail. I was frightened to get out of jail. I was safer in jail. And, once I do get to what they call Huntsville, the walls, where they release everybody, I was hoping to get released there because everybody was getting released was getting released in America. But no. Unfortunately ICE got there. When I get there, I tried to pull what we call a okidoke. I'm like, "Why you ain't letting me out with the rest of my buddies?" "You speak English?" "Of course I speak English. I'm an American." "Where were you born?" "In Park Lane Hospital." "What year?" "'1990." They're like, "Hold on, let me check your paperwork, because we have you here as a Mexican national." I said, "Man, you've got the wrong person." And I gave it everything I could until they're like, "Nah, you ain't fooling nobody." But, I had them going first.

Jose:        The immigration officer asked me if I was afraid of Mexico, but because there were people around me, I didn't want to say yeah. My ego wouldn't let me say yeah. But, he sensed something. He didn't tell me though. And then, as I'm going through the deportation process, they sent me somewhere else. This man put me in an asylum. So, I was in an asylum for two and a half months, just dry, sitting there. And I said, "Well, will I have a chance?" I started looking into the matter. And before you know it, I started seeing people that had no hands, legs, that actually were in a way that their life was in danger in their country. I don't stand a chance. So, I started doing my math and I said, "I waive all my rights, and deport me." After I waived my rights, they deport me, after two months and a half. Then they sent me to an immigration center. It's a ICE detention center. I was there for 25 days. And just holding me until they can get a bus filled, whatever they're doing there. And then they deported me.

Sergio:        Before we continue, how long before ... so, the judge issued the order. It sounds like you gave up voluntary departure. Is that the word that was given, or was it a deportation order?

Jose:        I never see no judge. The only judge I seen as far as immigration goes is when I was, in 2011, when I seen a judge on my violation of probation, that's what I went and did the time for because I violated. The judge was looking into my paperwork, and she was like, "How did you even get probation?" I'm like, "Excuse me ma'am?" And she's like, "You're an illegal. How did anybody give you probation? You should've been deported already." And I'm like, "Excuse me ma'am, I don't think you're an immigration lawyer." And that was just my reaction, because I believe her job was to sentence me, not to be worried about my legal status. So, that's the only immigration scenario I ever had.

Jose:        And them when I was in asylum, I never asked for asylum. The ICE person put me in asylum. And then when I was there I was trying to get out of it. But, I was in a sense, I was afraid of going to Mexico because of everything I hear about Mexico. I never been to Mexico. When I waived my rights, they sent me to immigration center. I don't see no judge or nothing. It was just to hold on until we get a bus.

Sergio:        So, after you sign the paperwork to self-deport, how long was it until you actually wound up in Mexico?

Jose:        Like a month.

Sergio:        A month? Okay.

Jose:        A month. And as I'm walking to, I don't know where I'm getting deported, even though they telling you can get deported to here, it doesn't click in because you're telling me something, it's like telling me to remember something that I'm not familiar with. It's hard. Only thing I remember, I'm being deported and I wait in the bus till everybody gets off. I said, "I wonder if this man will let me walk back." The bus driver. So, I was playing it slow until then. And he's like, "You've got to hurry up." So, I hurry up and I'm walking slow, and I think that's when time stopped in my life. Because, I realized that I was actually getting deported. Not until I was at that bus did it click on me, "Jose, you're getting deported." Through the whole time, because when you're in prison, bus after bus, you get bus at the prison. They move you all around Texas, when you're in prison. So, the bus situation wasn't anything new to me.

Jose:        But, when I was in that bus and he's telling me to hurry up, and I have no chain on me, I realize that I'm leaving the country that I love, and for good. And as I'm walking, I'm talking baby steps. And the bus driver, he instructed me, he said, "I cannot leave until you cross that line. Cross that line boy." And he has a 12 gage in his hand. I have baby steps. I'm taking baby steps, and I just can't walk. Until he says, "I said cross that line. You can walk as slow as you want after you cross that line." So, I stop at the line and I look at him. I turn my head and look at him, and then he just, how you say, cocks the shotgun, "Keep going." But, it was the hardest thing I could ever do, but I did it. And then when I did it, it's like time started moving again. But, it was a different time because it was a time that you're not aware of.

Jose:        You feel like you become a victim. You feel like everybody is after you once you cross that line. You think everybody is out to get you. So, once I got to the, what they call the INS, Instituto Nacional de Inmigración [National Institute of Immigration], I just learned that at the time, I didn't know nothing about it. Because, I recently did some studies on Mexico. And, I got there, they gave me a sack lunch. What made me comfortable was that I seen the Mexican marines there, so that kind of gave me a little bit of, "Okay, this might be a little bit safe." And they gave me a sack lunch in a paper bag and a phone call and a bottle of water and a paper ID. Oh, and they offered to pay half our bus ticket if we had some kind of funds on us. And if we didn't, they have a place called, La Casa Del Inmigrante [The House of the Immigrant], where they can help you out. And, I decided to get on a bus and get out of there and go to wherever it is that I'm from. So, that's how my deportation process went.

Jose:        They gave us a ride to the bus station, paid for my ticket, half my bus ticket. And I called my family, in this case my sister. And she was asking about me, if I was all right. I'm like, "Why?" And she's like, "In about 10 minutes I got a phone call, they were asking me for ransom money because they had you." So, when I got that, the fear jumped back in my life. I clicked and didn't leave the station, not even to get a taco, and I was starving. Because, the fear was still in me. Shit. And then, anything that I picked up in prison regarding Mexico, as far as, don't let people feel your accent. Because, they can pick up on your accent that you're not from around there. And that's a sign of money, meaning that you're worth something. Why? Because you have Americans in America, and they have money. So, I just stayed quiet until my bus got there. I stayed quiet until I got to where I was from, because I was scared to speak. I was scared for them to identify me. And that's how my process went.

Sergio:        So, when you were in detention, what were the conditions like?

Jose:        Horrible.

Sergio:        Horrible? Compared to prison?

Jose:        Compared to state prison, compared to federal prison. State prison and federal prison are two different things. So, comparing ICE to federal and state prison, is horrible. They violate so many things that it's unbelievable. I actually got moved out of a prison behind me filing on them because they ...

Sergio:        Filing a complaint?

Jose:        Yes. Filing complaint, because I also, how do you say? I advocate for people? I believe you should not violate a person's rights. And if you do and I can help, I will help. I have been helping. So, behind that, they moved me. Because, they won't do it to me, but they'll do it to them. But, since they can't write English, they can't file. I told the man, "You know what you're doing is wrong, right?" They said, "Yeah." I said, "I'm a write you up." They said, "You got to learn first." So, he didn't mean it for me, because he wouldn't violate my right. He violating somebody else's rights. Meaning, they don't know how to write. So, that's the kind of atmosphere that you live in ICE.

Sergio:        When you filed a complaint, was it for you or for someone else?

Jose:        For me first and then I said, "Might as well help the rest of them." When I filed a complaint, they had nothing but illegal people, they doing it to an American tank, where they live real nice. So, they're aware. They treat the Americans nice. And I don't mean nice as far as something extra. They just give them their portion. The ones that are illegally, they give them the scraps. So, I'm accustomed to live a certain way because I've been incarcerated before. It isn't my first rodeo. I said, "What's going on here? What's this? What is that? Hold on, let me write to the Warden. Let me call my family." When you have your family calling up here, the majority of the people in there can't call. Why? Because they're family's in Mexico. My family is in America. And they'll call somebody. They're going to send an email. They'll call somebody.

Jose:        So, when they know that, "Get him out of there, he's starting too much mess. Put him where we've got the Americans." So, after that, I was filing with the Mexican Council. And when they came to visit me after all the filing, I'm like, "Look, this is what they did to me." And I said, "I can't continue filing on something that's not being nice." But, I do. There's certain people that have been filing. I've been helping them out, and they know a little bit. I've been instructing them on how to continue filing. So, you can continue with them. But, they instructed me that, since they started filing with me because I have a lot of time to do, they are going to be there for a little bit, so they can't continue the process. So, they needed me. And I said, "Well, I can't. That's basically it." That's how I describe the ICE centers. They're horrible.

Jose:        They're, how do you say, inhumane?

Jose:        Yeah. There's not enough food. The phone costs are super expensive. What I mean expensive is, if I'm in Texas and I'm calling Dallas, it should be like a local call. They're charging me $10 for 15 minutes. And I call Oklahoma City, where it's another state, and they're charging me $3? There's a conspiracy going on in there. Immigration is America’s new cash cow. And I can't figure that out as far as the little studies I've been researching. When you read books you get informed, and then you get inside living it, you put A and B together. Post, Texas has probably a thousand residents. In just the prison that they have in there, it's a private prison, has over 2500 inmates, illegal inmates. Post, Texas, just by the population of inmates, has more population than the population that is in Post, Texas, as residents.

Jose:        Yeah, so, the living situation in ICE is horrible.

Sergio:        Back to when you were returning to Mexico, taking the bus, where did you end up? What is the hometown that you went to?

Jose:        Where I'm from is called San Luis Potosí. And once I got to the city, that is San Luis Potosí, something about it kind of gave me some sort of calm. I felt like I was out of the danger zone. And there is danger zones in Mexico. And if you are a tourist going to Mexico you can look up the hotspots. I didn't know that. If I would've known that, I would've have my family send me one. So, once I got to San Luis, I feel a different atmosphere, and waited for a relative that was supposed to come pick me up. My family are from, I really don't know. They're just from the mountains, somewhere in the mountains. So, I figured, catch a taxi and come get me. No. More like, catch a bus and get off a bus and get on another bus, and then they catch a taxi and they come get me. So, it took him about five hours to come get me. So, I waited there for five hours. And eventually my uncle got there, and he took me to where we're from.
