Lizzy:        Okay, this is Lizzy, and I'm interviewing Kris. Let's start off maybe back at the beginning. Could you tell me a little bit about how you came to migrate from Mexico to the U.S.? How old were you?

Kris:        Yes, I was actually five years old. I was five years old. So, it wasn't really my decision. My father was looking for something better for us. So, he took the decision to actually go to the United States. Don't know much of how it happened. All I remember is that I was in a car, and I crossed the border like normal. I just remember him saying act like an American citizen. [Chuckles]. I was like, "Okay." He bought me a happy meal, some glasses, and new clothes. He was just like, "Don't say anything. You're an American citizen."

Lizzy:        Just play it cool.

Kris:        Just play it cool.

Lizzy:        Do you have any memories of that first day in the U.S.?

Kris:        Yes, because after crossing the border we went to a hotel. It was a hotel. It was actually pretty dirty. I just remember meeting up with my mom, because I'm not sure how she crossed. I just remember we met up crossing the border, and then we stayed at a hotel for one day. A car came for us, and then that car took us to the airport. We took a plane to Atlanta, Georgia. That's where we stayed.

Lizzy:        So Atlanta is where you grew up?

Kris:        First year in Alabama. Next year South Carolina, Columbia. And then Atlanta, Georgia.

Lizzy:        Okay, all over the South. Yeah, I can hear it in your accent, that southern accent.

Kris:        Yeah, I liked the city. I liked the most with Atlanta.

Lizzy:        Atlanta.

Kris:        It's a big city. Very nice.

Kris:        Yes, it's pretty sad, because I didn't know English. So, I didn't know how to communicate with anyone. Then I wanted to ask for my lunch at the cafeteria. I had a cousin that speaked English, and I told her if she could help me ask for my lunch. She was like, "You'll need to learn someday. So start now." I started crying, because I was six years old, and I didn't know what to do. Then one of the teachers knew Spanish. So she came to me and said, "Why are you crying?" I'm like, "I don't know. I just can't ask for my food." She was like, "I'll ask for it," and she helped me a lot.
