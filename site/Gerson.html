Mexico City, Mexico

Claudia:        So this is Claudia and I am interviewing GS101. All right. So again, thank you so much for coming and speaking with us because we came here. But I do want to say, before we begin, that I understand that sometimes talking about these experiences can be a little difficult, especially because they bring a lot of emotions that you don't necessarily want to feel. And I apologize if that is the case today. But I do think it's good to know how important your story is and how important it is for other people to hear it so that we can shed light on the realities of migration on the human side of migration.

Gerson:        Okay. Yeah, no problem.

Claudia:        So my first question for you is, why did you or your family decide to leave Mexico? And how did you cross the border?

Gerson:        Well the reason why we first went to the US is because my dad left Mexico City when I was four. So my mom, trying to follow him, you know, she decided to start saving money and we had a couple of jewelry that my dad had sent us. So my mom decided to surprise my dad, going to the United States one day. The way that we crossed it was literally crossing through the border, Piedras Negras, I believe it's called, the city where we passed through. And it was a rough experience to actually go through there because it was a full week of trying to go into the United States. And then when we actually crossed and we were able to get to Arizona, we got lost in the desert for two days because the guide that was guiding us to cross, basically took half of the group with him and then the other half stayed behind.

Gerson:        So it was really crazy cause, I mean, it was hot, it was cold at night, these wild animals, snakes. I mean, it was a crazy-ass experience. And I mean, I was six at that time. I was really young. My mom had a lot of... she felt she had a lot of injuries because of the same reason. It was a real, real tough way for us to cross. And once we actually were able to cross, we got to Arizona, and from Arizona we went to Chicago, Illinois.

Claudia:        And so, is that where you were pretty much all the time in Chicago?

Gerson:        Nope. Afterwards we got there, we were there like for three months with my grandma, from the part of my dad, and we were waiting for my dad to come and pick us up. So then when my dad found out that we had crossed the border and we were in the United States, he was a bit, I don't know, I wouldn't call it shook, but there was something going on because it was really suspicious that he just did not come and get us. So then afterwards, like a month later, he came and he said that we would have to go back to Mexico City because he didn't have a job and he wasn't stable and that basically he couldn't take care of us at that time. So my mom decided not to listen to him and told him that we had gone through a lot of things to just go back to Mexico City just like that.

Gerson:        So my dad decided to go back to Arkansas and said he would go and get an apartment for us. But the reality was that he had another family. So after like two weeks my dad left us, he called my mom and told her about he had another family, and that he was going to have a new baby, and that he couldn't take care of us. So obviously, it was a tough decision... I mean, a tough news for us, mostly for me because I was young, and my mom decided to go to Denver, Colorado. We stayed there for almost a year, but she decided we were alone, she was working and most of the time I was alone, I had to go to school alone. So then she decided just to go back to California because most of my family and my relatives are over there.

Gerson:        So we went to California for almost over than 11 years. And that's where I basically grew up and I got all my ways, I can say. So it was tough living in California too, because there's a lot of violence and there's a lot of... Being Mexican, it gets you surrounded in this environment of bad things because... At the beginning, when I was in school, I was really a good kid and I was an A straight student. I was doing really good in school. I mean, my teachers would always tell me that I was going to be able to get the scholarships, and things like that, so I can go to university. But then, so many things happened in my life that kind of made me basically change my whole outcome, discussions with my dad, with my mom.

Gerson:        The actual fact that my dad wasn't living with us really really changed the whole situation for me. So I started hanging out with the wrong people, started doing, I mean, the bad things. And, I mean, at the end of the day, it's like everything happens for a reason, right? So I ended up messing up my life. I wouldn't say my life, but I guess like at that time I messed it up because I was just so used to being in the United States.

Claudia:        You didn't think that you'd ever leave?

Gerson:        Yeah. I mean, honestly, I saw so many of my friends going into juvie and prison, and they would go in, coming back, see them like a month later in the streets be like, "Hey, what's up? What happened?" "Oh yeah, yeah, whatever, this happened, but I'm out." "Okay." And then I decided to take my own decisions and go to the wrong path and started doing bad things and I got caught up and police one day got me, and the situation for me wasn't the same because at the end of the day, for me, it wasn't like I'm going to go back to the streets, I'm just... I could have been locked up for a long time but, I guess, one of my uncles, he's an attorney, so he took my case, and like I mentioned, I was barely like 17 at that time.

Claudia:        Sorry.

Gerson:        No, don't worry. So after that, my uncle helped me to be able to get a voluntary departure. So when I would turned 18, everything was at risk that I was going to be taken to feds, to federal prison, right?

Gerson:        So my uncle fought for my case, we had to pay some money, and thank God, I was able to get released. But, I mean, I was literally taken to the border and I was left in Ciudad Juárez.

Claudia:        So, I'm going to backtrack a little bit. So were you a part of a gang when you were in California?

Gerson:        Yes. Yeah, I was part of a gang.

Claudia:        And when you say bad things, what sort of things made you get caught up with the police? If you're comfortable saying.

Gerson:        I mean, it's not like I did something... Yeah, I mean, I feel like I did something bad because I was smuggling drugs. I got caught up in that situation. I started selling drugs, and then one day I was driving and I had drugs in the trunk, and I felt like somebody had already put me on the spot. And somebody told police that I was selling. And next thing you know, police stops me and they start searching, they can't find nothing on me. And they're like, "You know what? We have permission to open the trunk." And at the end of the day, there was a gun in there and there was drugs, and the whole situation just changed for me, from being free on the streets and living a good life, I guess. I went into juvie and I kind of understood that the path that I was taking, it was the wrong path, right? And I was doing bad things at the end of the day.

Gerson:        I mean, I guess I'm grateful and I'm lucky to say that I didn't do nothing else, a part of that. But at the end of the day I messed up my future maybe in the United States, being able to get a citizenship or something like that.

Claudia:        What gang was it? I don't know if you can say.

Gerson:        I would just...

Claudia:        All right. Why did you, then... going to switch that question. Why did you join in the first place? Like what was attractive about it?

Gerson:        Well, for me, it was a gang in 4th Street in Santa Ana, California. And, I mean, the reason why, is because when I hit the first year of junior high, so I was in sixth grade, right? So I started meeting these people and, like I mentioned, I was an A straight student, I was doing pretty good. I really never had to worry about nothing. So one day I met this guy in school, he starts talking to me, he starts telling me that... he start showing me this other world that maybe when you're not related to all of that, it's completely new, and it just becomes radical to your life because it changes your life forever. And I started hanging out with his... My friend's brother was the one that was completely affiliated with that gang.

Gerson:        So my friend started pulling me, "Let's go to my house. Let’s go to my house." And then my mom, she never really got suspicious about it. And I would go to his crib, his house, I would stay there, and next thing I was gang banging with them. I was part of that gang and I was asked to do things. And then I started meeting people. People offer me money to do certain things, and honestly, the California lifestyle, it's good but it's pretty expensive, you got to be able to have a good job or something... a good income, to be able to live a good life. But my mom, she had a job and everything, but I mean, I can't say I was rich over there, I can't say I was living a good life.

Gerson:        So when I started doing this things, selling drugs, I started seeing so much income for me that I was able to help my mom, I was able to get so many things that I wanted. And I guess that that really, really was attractive for me. And then I said, "Screw school, why would I want to go to school if I can make more money here?" I decided to... I was still going to school and that's where I was making most of the money, because I guess I was just contaminating all these people in school with things that they never seen before or tried before. But it was just me wanting to get this money.

Gerson:        And because I kind of feel pressure at the same time, because you're in a gang where most of them are over 25. And then me and my friend were the only young bucks there... we were like 17, 16 at the time. So, I mean, I guess pressure is put upon us, but it's also a decision that maybe you're young and dumb and you don't really know what you're doing till you see reality when reality hits. So, I mean, money was my most motive for me to start doing things.

Claudia:        And so, you ended up in juvie and then jail and all that. How was that experience? How was the treatment in those centers?

Gerson:        Well, it was kind of messed up because you had to fight a lot. There were so many things because you're from another gang, you're Hispanic, you're Mexican and most of the people there were Blacks or... Yeah, most of them were Blacks. So, I mean, I've always gotten along with Black people. I was always cool with the Black people that I met. But it kind of puts, like I said, a label on you because you're representing this clique and you are a different part of... basically you're just a completely different part of them. So once I got into juvie, I was able to understand that, I mean, it's not how they tell you it is in the streets, because the streets is like, "Oh, Black people hate Mexicans, Black people hate White, Black people..." and the other way around, right?

Gerson:        So that's maybe a way that I answer and I was like, "Damn, what's going to happen. I don't see a lot of Mexicans here." But then the people that I was representing or that I was throwing... yeah, I was representing, I guess, never came up and saw like, "Look at me. Hey, what's up, what are you doing? I never said nothing." And I was just all crewed up in there. And, I mean, my friends were actually Black in there. So I started talking more to Black people, and Black people were cool with me. So then more people of my gang started falling there, young people too. And they started seeing that I start talking to these people, and I started getting in trouble with my own gang.

Gerson:        And then it's like, "Why are you doing this? Why are you doing that when you know you're not supposed to be doing this." "Yeah, but we're not out there no more." It's like, "We're in here, it's completely different. You got to do anything to survive and you just can't be like fooling around." And I mean, I had to find my own people, I had to... Black people stood up for me. And, I mean, it was a crazy experience, but that's something that I feel like really shaped my... I just became a better person whenever I came out of there.

Claudia:        Yeah. And so tell me about coming back to Mexico, how was that?

Gerson:        Well, coming back to Mexico was really rough too because, like I mentioned, I was just left in the border, and go back I had to find a way to... I mean, luckily I have family members in the border, so some of my cousin picked me up and he bought a plane ticket so I can come back to Mexico City because most of my family's here, and my mom wanted me to come to Mexico City and not stay at the border. So I came back to Mexico City, I was trying to study, it was kind of messed up because obviously I was seeing in another way. I just didn't fit there, the way I dress, the way I talk, the way I drive myself, it was completely different from the way they do it here.

Gerson:        So I experienced a lot of discrimination when I got here, and as I tried to study, they revaliated on my studies so I had to go one year back into high school. So I go to high school and the second day that I was in high school, I fight this guy because he didn't like me because the way I was and the way I looked. So he was like, "Nah." He used to call me Cholo because I used to have that experience of... I mean, that appearance, all bolded, sagging my pants and Dickies and... It was just a different lifestyle, I guess.

Gerson:        So everybody was like, "I know you're not from here. Where are you from?" And they were trying to check me. And at the end of the day, if streets shows you something, I guess it's just to not let nobody talk over you or nobody put you down. So I fighted this guy, I started feeling a lot of pressure in school, people didn't like me, I would always be alone in lunch time. I mean, when it was recess and things like that, I was always alone. And people would look at me like if I was a bad person.

Gerson:        So then I started having problems with my own teachers, and mostly my English teacher, because she would get mad because I would correct her. I would tell her, "Oh, you're wrong," or... Most of the teachers here in Mexico City don't know English. And it's crazy because they are teaching it. So that's the craziest thing about it. But I was like, "All right, so I'm not going to say anything. Keep doing your thing and I'm just going to be here." But things got... in my end, I feel like it really became tedious just to go to school. So, I just decided to quit school. My mom was pretty pissed at me because my mom was still in the United States, but she was coming back because of me.

Claudia:        Does she have residency?

Gerson:        No.

Claudia:        Or citizenship?

Gerson:        Nope.

Claudia:        She was coming and crossing?

Gerson:        She just came back, she basically deported herself. So, I mean, she had no reason to come back because she was working over there and stuff, but I mean, she's always been too close with me, and then she knows all the things that I had to go through in juvie and how close I was to actually hitting the feds. So, I mean, I guess she didn't feel like she had no reasons to be in the US no more, and she just decided to come back. So she came back after like five months, but I mean, honestly I was into depression big time because I was just going through all these things.

Gerson:        My mind was telling me, "You got to go back, you got to do this. You're not from here." But then again, you're not from over there. So you don't have a green card to be like, "Oh, I'm going to go back." Or, "I just can't cross the border." Because I knew if I would do that, that would automatically give me five years or ten years in the feds. So it was like, "Do I really want to risk that? Do I really want to be free here or do I want to be locked up over there? Or do I deal with the people here?"

Gerson:        So at the beginning, the same thing, when people started knowing me, and around the places where I lived, people would be like, "Nah, you feel like you're better than us because you're coming from the US. But here you ain’t shit," things like that, right? And like I mentioned, I've never really let nobody talk nothing bad to me. So I would lash out, admittedly snap, and get in a fight with these people and be like, "Damn it. Why am I really doing all of this? Why am I really going through all of this when this is supposed to be my city or where I was born?" But I mean, discrimination, I had a lot when I got here. But I mean, it's something that you just got to deal with it.

Claudia:        And so, what was the hardest part about being back?

Gerson:        I guess that, discrimination. Yeah.

Claudia:        And is your mom still here with you or did she go back?

Gerson:        Yes.

My only family for me, it's always been my mom. So I can't really say I feel separated from none of my family because my dad is over there. My dad is over there, but I've never lived with him. Like I mentioned since the beginning, he just decided to bounce on us and just do his own life and... So for me, I've never really considered my dad to be that close to me. So it doesn't really bother me that he's over there and to see him.

Gerson:        Obviously there is other things like not having a dad in your life and a man figure that could guide you and tell you that you're doing this wrong, you shouldn't be doing this. So that's also the reason why I feel like I messed up so much because my mom was always working, trying to give me something, and I didn't appreciate all the efforts that she was doing, and I just started doing the bad things. Why? Because, first of all, I kind of liked it, and second of all, because there was nobody to guide me like that. I was always alone. So I had—it's like I would ditch school, I would do bad things in school, and my mom would never find out.

Gerson:        So for me, like I said, my actual family is my mom and I was really grateful when I saw her again. Claudia:        Yeah.

Gerson:        Because that depression, at the end of the day, you find a way to cure it. You find a way how to relieve all this depression and all this bad things that you feel upon you. Because when I was over it, it was like, "Damn it." And that got me hooked into a lot of bad habits that later on in my life, it was hard for me to leave. But luckily, I mean, I was able to move on, I was able to leave a lot of things and I found this girl that right now I live with her, she's my partner. So whatever I was getting, I was like, "Okay, I can live with this." But obviously I wasn't stable economically. Economically I didn’t feel stable. Where I didn't feel like I had the same lifestyle that I had like when I was in the United States.

Gerson:        So then I started finding out about call centers and things like that. Those were the things that I guess helped me to make a little bit more of money to be more stable. And I mean, right now Hola Code, the fact that they are giving us this... that we're learning how to code and become a software engineer, it's something that I would have never thought of, and probably not even in the States, because even if I was arrested in the United States, I feel like I would be either dead or in jail.

Claudia:        Yeah.
