Interviewee

Interviewer

June 7, 2019

Mexico City, Mexico

Sergio:        So, first question. I'm with Jairo, doing the interview. What is the reason why your family went to the US?

Jairo:        Well, you can say the reason was, well, the reason my family went to the US was for a different life. You know what I’m saying. They come from big families. You know what I’m saying. They come from agriculture. Where I'm from, there's nothing but agriculture surroundings. So, big families, little money, no money. You know what I’m saying. They saw how we were going to end up. You know what I’m saying. But you can say they saved me from… um…. the type of lifestyle that's there. They gave me something that, uh, no other person from my hometown has, that experience in life. You know what I’m saying. I tell them a story about my childhood, you know what I’m saying, and it's like a movie to them. Like, "Which movie you see?" I'm like, "Y'all tripping. It's my life." It's a whole different experience that you grow up with.

Jairo:        But I mean the reason was, they gave us that schooling, the American studies. You know what I’m saying. That learning that was given in the US. Also, a different lifestyle. But, they did it, I guess, for that lifestyle. For a different life. For better opportunity. You know what I’m saying.

Sergio:        So, what's the first memory that you have of the United States?

Jairo:        Man, my first memory was, it's crazy because I remember when I arrived, it was like a DART station. I remember I was with somebody, I didn't know who. But, I remember I was with somebody. I remember seeing all white. I remember I ate a little fucking sandwich. You know what I’m saying. I was at the bus stop, I remember. I would see that bus stop. I would imagine that bus stop. It was all white. I'm guessing it's the DART, in Dallas. The DART bus stop. But I was young. You know what I’m saying. So, but that was my first memory since a kid. I was three when I was brought here.

Sergio:        What do you remember then?

Jairo:        I don't know. It's just crazy. One of my memories, I would remember it as a youngster. That, and I remember eating dirt in Mexico. [Chuckles]. You know what I’m saying.

Sergio:        You were eating dirt in Mexico?

Jairo:        Yeah. I remember like, I was young like and they always said I would leave and go back and forth from one house to another one. You know what I’m saying. And uhh, where I live at, it wasn't always cement. There was dirt. So, I remember it as a young kid, I remember it was raining. I picked it, I tasted the dirt. You know what I’m saying. It tasted good. When you smell that rain and it hits the ground and it smells like wet floor, that's what I remember. But yeah, I remember that and that DART location.

Sergio:        So, you went to US school. Who do you remember from your schooling?

Jairo:        Man, yeah. I went to the US schools man. But, most I remember is always getting involved into something. It was always me; you know what I’m saying, being Mexican, the other kids being African American. So, we always got into it. You know what I’m saying. I would get into it with Mexicans and Mexicans. But I remember there was always a fight. You know what I’m saying. I had nobody to defend me. You know what I’m saying. I had no cousins. I had nobody. So, I had to stand my ground, you know what I'm talking about? I grew up. You know what I’m saying. In the neighborhood, it was all blacks. You know what I’m saying. It was me and three other homeboys you could say. Little homies. My other homeboy, " Like yeah, I got you." We would take care of each other. But, most of the time it was just me by myself. So, I had to stand my ground.

Jairo:        I remember since a youngster I got into it, you know what I’m saying, with African American, with black people. You know what I’m saying. But, I remember getting jumped, getting chased by these people. You know what I’m saying. Like It was a crazy life. You know what I’m saying. Since a youngster to my high school years. Even after high school, I still got into it. You know what I’m saying. We would cross roads and like, "What's up?" You know what I’m saying. For the disrespect they would give me. I had to give them, you know what I’m saying…I had to teach. "Yeah, I might be one deep. Yeah, I might be by myself. But, just because I'm one deep by myself, you're not going to disrespect me for being Mexican, you know what I’m saying, or my people.” So, anytime they would disrespect my people, we got into it. "What's up man? What you saying my nigga? You ain't white my nigga. I don't know why you disrespecting us like that. You don't like being disrespected, why you disrespecting us though like this?"

Jairo:        So, since a youngster, middle school, high school, elementary, preschool, You know what I’m saying. it was always there.

Sergio:        So, you always have. Do you remember the altercations that you had with African American students and with ...

Jairo:        Yeah, like I mean, I would like I don't know if you know. You know what I’m saying. They like to put in fear into people. You know what I’m saying. They're loud. But, once you get into it with them, you know what I’m saying, they'll soften down. They'll give you their respect. As long as you stand for it, You know what I’m saying. you stand your ground, you don't fold. That's what it was. You know what I’m saying. But, most of the altercations were because I was a Mexican. You know what I’m saying. There was, uh, in Dallas, in the pen, they got into it, you know blacks and Mexicans. Blacks and Mexicans, they brought that out to the streets. I remember my homeboy. My homeboy was black. You know what I’m saying. We was in pre-K together. You know what I’m saying. That nigga one day just comes out the blue, starts hitting me. I'm like, "What the fuck's wrong with you dog? You're my homeboy dog. What you doing?" I remember we was walking. We walked from the cafeteria all the way to the slide… boy hitting me. I got tired. Turned around. "Man, I told you to stop man." Turned around, started swinging. We was all the way by the soccer field. I brought that boy all the way back to the slide. "I told you. Why you fucking with me?" After that, you know what I’m saying, it was hell. He brought his cousins and his uncles. You know what I’m saying. Like It was always like that, dog.

Jairo:        In middle school I was at a basketball game and this boy come out. You know what I’m saying. He just cut me. I'm like, "What you doing man?" I'm like, "What? It's whatever. You want?" I remember that boy hit me twice. I was like, shit. You know what I’m saying. He had come hard. I'm on the floor, what the fuck? You know what I’m saying. My glasses fell off. I made sure my glasses was straight. If I broke those glasses, I was not going to get another set, so I had to save them. You know what I’m saying. High school, same thing. I remember the homeboy was fighting another person, and this black kid comes in trying to sneak one from behind like, "What's up?" "What, me and you? Whatever." After that man, every day I had a fight. You know what I’m saying. It was to a point where I had to line my fights up. People come up to me like, "What's up?" I'm like, "Hey man, I already got two fights after school, You know what I’m saying. So, you're going to have to wait till after them." People look at me dumb like, "Are you serious? You just schedule me to a fight? Where they do that at?" You know what I’m saying. I'm like, "Shit, yeah fool. I mean, I'm already busy for after school."

Jairo:        It was crazy because it would be like masses. Went and got guns pulled out, all crazy stuff like that. But we would stand our ground. You know what I’m saying. I even got into it with my own people.

Sergio:        So, do you think that you got into so many fights because of ...

Jairo:        Being Mexican.

Sergio:        ... Mexican?

Jairo:        Just because we were different. You know what I’m saying. We were lesser than them. There was a history, where I'm from, like, like, they were scared of black people. I wasn't. I'm like, "Nah man. You know what I’m saying. They going to respect me." And I brought that mentality down here. Like I don't care who you are, who you run, who you shoot, how many you kill, man. You're going to respect me. You know what I'm talking about? Yeah, but most of the times it was because I was Mexican. You know what I’m saying. Because I was big. I was always big. So, they looked at me as an easy target. But yeah, most of the times it's because of that disrespect towards who I was or how I was.

Sergio:        So, it was just because you were Mexican-American. Did they know that you were undocumented too?

Jairo:        I mean, yeah, they knew. I would always represent. You know what I’m saying.  I would've told them all where I'm from. Because, I'm not from here. I'm from Mexico. Since I was a youngster, I remember I would tell my teacher, "Yeah, I'm from Mexico." You know what I’m saying.  I was always representing who I was, where I was from, since an early age.

Sergio:        So, you would always say ...

Jairo:        Yeah. I'm Mexican. I'm not no Chicano. I'm not from Texas. I would've loved to be from Texas, but no, I'm from Guanajuato, Mexico. That's where I'm from. GTO I would say.

Sergio:        How do you think that affected you growing up?

Jairo:        How it affected me growing up saying I was Mexican? Well shit, I mean, it made people look at you differently. You know what I’m saying. Like, “You really out here saying you're Mexican? You know when people call immigration?” I was a person that really didn't care. I'm this and if you don’t like it, I'll move around. That's what I liked about it.

Sergio:        So, that sounds like you were in a lot of fights.

Jairo:        A lot. I even got some on YouTube, bruh. Got into it with this big black bull. Taunting me. But I'm talkin’ bout, he's huge bruh. Huge monster. You know what I’m saying. But this bull. My homeboy was fighting his homeboy. These some grown people. We was young. We was 15, 16. These niggas was like 22, 21. Boy, talkin’. You know what I’m saying. smack. I'm like, "Man, you're going to need to shut it bruh." He looked at me like, "What you going do?" Like, "What? Man, it's whatever." "You want it?" "It's whatever man." I wasn't going to say yeah, but I wasn't going to say no either. I was like, "Whatever." Once you say it's whatever, it's going down.

Jairo:        I'm over here trying to duck and weave, bob and weave. Tried to reach for him. Man, I couldn't reach his face, dog. He was huge, bruh. I'm over here like, "Damn man." Tried to jump. You know, I had to hit that Super Mario punch, dog.

Sergio:        So, aside from being involved in a lot of fights, were you involve in other kind of...

Jairo:        Illegal activities? Well, I mean, with certain pride and respect, you, you know what I’m saying.  You get involved with a lot of stuff. You know a lot of people. So, you could say, I mean, shit, I was young. I was money hungry, dog. Sometimes, It was a time when my parents had it tough, so I mean I had to do what I had to do to bring something to the table. You know what I’m saying. I was able to help out my parents, pay a couple bills. You know what I’m saying. Pay a couple of rents. You know what I’m saying. I was young. I wanted an Xbox, but I had to give my dad that money. You know what I’m saying. It didn't hurt me. I'll do it. "Man, I got more houses I can rob. I ain't tripping. Take that. I come back with a thousand more tonight.” You know what I’m saying.

Sergio:        How did you get that money?

Jairo:        You know what I’m saying. People borrow, let me borrow things and I would sell them, you could say. And uh, sometimes, You know what I’m saying. I knew people that did stuff and I knew people that sold stuff, so I was that middleman. I connected this, connected that. I mean.

Sergio:        You were the connect.

Jairo:        You know what I’m saying. Some people like to call it the middleman. But yeah man. I was doing things. You know what I’m saying. Moving myself around, since I know people, people know me. Sometimes people would ask me to take care of them. You know what I’m saying. Like a bodyguard. You know what I’m saying. But, with that came other things. I taught people this. They taught me that. You know what I’m saying. But yeah.

Sergio:        So, what were some of those things that you learned or some of the things that you did?

Jairo:        Man, I learned how many grams go into certain things. You know what I’m saying. How much to put in certain stuff. Or, how to do, you know what I’m saying. How to talk. How to handle business, what prices had to be. You know what I’m saying. So, would be looking at stuff like that. But, I mean, bag, bag stuff up, stuff like that. Where was the good stuff. I moved around. You know what I’m saying. Where to buy cheap guns or connect people with stuff. You know what I’m saying. That's what we did. One thing led to another. You know what I’m saying. Yeah.

Sergio:        Did you ever get involved in like drug activity too?

Jairo:        I mean, I'm not going to say yeah, but I'm not going to say no. You know what I’m saying. We had to do what we had to do. You know what I’m saying. But I was mostly a user, bro. I was mostly…I became a user instead of a provider. I would provide whatever I had, if I had it. If not, you know what I’m saying, I was using it. Because, at the points where you're the one connecting, you're trying to get people the good stuff, so you're trying the stuff out. Make sure you got the best out here. You know what I’m saying. Made sure I always had the best. I made sure it looked right. The quantity. People always would look for me because of the quantity and the quality. You know what I’m saying. I had that good stuff. I had it for the low. It was a little time.

Sergio:        Did you ever claim a gang?

Jairo:        Nah man. We ain't got no claim gangs. Up in Texas we ain't got no gangs. What you do. You got your little claims, little cliques, stuff like that. But, I had homeboys. Some homeboys was Bloods, others was Sureños, others was Crips, others was Rolling 60, some were Tango Blast. You know what I’m talkin’ bout. I even had some white brothers who were Aryan Brotherhood. That straight Nazi type of stuff. You know what I’m saying. But me, it's just me. I had a little clique. You know what I’m saying. It's like a group of us. You know what I’m saying. Brown and Black Soldiers, stuff like that we called it.

Sergio:        So, it was just like a clique?

Jairo:        Yeah. We were a set group of people that were known. We got our backs. Cause I mean. I'm telling you, when you have a lot of beef, not too many people want to hang out with you.

Sergio:        [Sneeze]

Jairo:        Bless you.

Sergio:        So, what led to you being um ...

Sergio:        [Sneeze]

Jairo:        Bless you.

Sergio:        ... deported? Thanks.

Jairo:        Man, like I tell you. You know what I’m saying. When you become the connect, so you try out a lot of things. You know what I’m saying.  I started smoking that PCP when I was a dishwasher, man. I was with this homeboy from East Dallas. He was an Eastside Homeboy. Like, "What's up bro? What's good?" You know what I’m saying. "I'm from over here." I was like, "Oh shit, you know from over there?" "Homeboy was out from PV.  One of the old Eastside Homeboys. You know what I’m saying. So, I was like, "Man, I got my [inaudible] in the east with his little cousin Sam. He's my boy." "Oh yeah?" I'm like, "Yeah." He's like, "What you do? Do you smoke?" "Man, shit man.” I was fucking with that cheese at that time. So, he's like, "Man, you tripping bruh." He was smoking that wack, so we ain’t know who was tripping harder, me or him. We were both fucked up. You know what I’m saying. But like, yeah, he was like, "I smoke wack, that PCP." "What the fuck is that dog?" Like, "Shit, you never tried it?" I'm like, "Fuck, no bruh, but let me see."

Jairo:        But, I don't know if you've ever seen PCP parts when people get tripping, get whacked out, You know what I’m saying. They run around butt naked in the hood? I never did it. It gave me an effect. You know what I’m saying. Like trip out. You know what I’m saying. So, I remember I had smoked a couple of sticks, A couple of Forties. I was at the 7. And every time I would go to that 7-eleven, I always had an altercation with the Arabian guys, man. They would always just ask me for my fucking Texas ID. And these niggas already knew me. Like "You know I'm a Mexican man. Why you always tripping? Let me see your ID. You ain't from here either nigga. Let me see your Arabian ID?" He goes, "Man." And I'm telling you, when I got arrested there, I remember me and the cops got into it. You know what I’m saying. It had to be to a point where there was seven of them escorting me to the vehicle. They couldn't get me in. They had to pepper spray me. You know what I’m saying.

Jairo:        I remember on my way to the jail, You know what I’m saying. I was out here tripping, cussing them out and stuff like that. But, I mean, that's what led to my deportation. That, and I had a lot of charges. You know what I’m saying. I got caught up with illegal substance. I got caught up with a firearm, you know what I’m saying, without being registered. Hollow points, one in the chamber. Like, You know what I’m saying. They had me. I remember at 16, they was trying to give me conspiracy theory. I'm like, "What the fuck you going to do? What you going to put me down for that?" "Because you're always making mess. You're always involved in something." I'm like, "Fuck, it's not my fault." Like stuff like that. One day I guess they get tired. The government gets tired and fuck it, you’re going back.

Sergio:        So, how were you treated in the ICE facility?

Jairo:        In jail? I mean, the food sucks. I'll tell you right now. I was hungry all the time dog. You know what I’m saying. You've got to work something out over there. That food at the jails man, it's really bad. Give them some salt. Teach people how to use salt. But, Nah I mean, other than that dog, it was a’ight. Couldn't complain, dog. It was colder than a motherfucker for a minute. Couldn't complain though. It's better than nothing. Have you ever been to jail here? Oh man, it's crazy. I'd rather be in jail over there than here, dog. It's crazy. They don't feed you like over there. They really don't care if you die right there in the cell. I'm like, damn. You know what I’m saying. Like, everybody's musty. No AC.

Sergio:        So, when you came back, what did you do when you came back?

Jairo:        When I came back, like I said, I was a security guard. You know what I’m saying. I was working in the fields because my grandpa has a couple of land. You know what I’m saying. So, I would help him. Every time he used to, how do you say that shit? When you spray poison for the animals, so they won't eat the crops? You know what that's called?

Sergio:        Insecticide?

Jairo:        Yeah. There you go. I remember we used to do that. Take our little wheats, little mustard plants. Yeah, we used to. That's what I used to do though. A lot of stuff. I used to do all that stuff.

Sergio:        So, when you were back, did anyone ever try to recruit you for a gang or for like a group here in Mexico?

Jairo:        They told me about it, but I mean, no. Nobody ever came across me for that stuff. And that because I'm not, I mean, I don't know, I don't respect regular laws. How am I going to respect no, You know what I’m saying. gang laws, no organized crime laws? You know what I’m saying. I'm the kind, when I don't like something, I'm not going to listen to you. I'm guessing that's why they didn't. You know what I’m saying. They don't like that kind of people. They want somebody that's dumb. They put in the corner, they give you a gun to defend it. You know what I’m saying. I'm not dumb. I like to say I'm not dumb. You know what I’m saying. But, yeah.

Sergio:        They talked to you about it?

Jairo:        They told me about it, but I'm like, "Nah, I'm good bruh." I'm like, "I'm not going to listen to you dog." "Nah, but you got to listen to me if you want in.” I'm like, "I'd rather be broke than be listening to you dog."

Sergio:        So, what? They offered you money and you were like, "Nah, I don't want to follow rules"?

Jairo:        They were trying to offer me my spot, work. But, I had to sell it for this price. And I was like, "Nah. I'm going to sell it for my price, and I'm going to sell here, I'm going to sell there. I'm going to sell wherever I feel like it." "No, you're going to listen to me if you want to." I'm like, "Man, fuck you then, man. Don't give me nothing, dog."

Sergio:        So, it didn't work out. Did you still sell it on your own or?

Jairo:        No. Once they cut you off, they cut you off. It's kind of hard to get it from somewhere else. That, and you get involved in a lot of stuff man. I really ain’t have nobody to look out for me, so I'm like, might as well leave that alone. You know what I’m saying. And it was good too man. I can't even lie. If not, and I would've been dead right now. Disappeared. But yeah, I mean nah bruh. I don't listen to regular laws. I'm not going to be listening to their laws.

Jairo:        Acting up? Fooling?

Sergio:        ... affected by drugs or ...

Jairo:        Yeah. But, not like in the US bruh. I mean, here, it's either you take what's here or you don't take nothing at all. And all that, I had a whole buffet of drugs. I wake up in the morning. You know what I’m saying. Pop a couple of pills and smoke a little something. For lunch I would have something, a whole difference. You know what I’m saying. Like nah, bro. I almost lost my life when I was 19. I overdosed in the States.

Sergio:        At the hospital?

Jairo:        Nah. At the spot. You know what I’m saying. We had the spot. I couldn't take care of him. We were cheesed out. You know what I’m saying. We were off that heroin. We mixed it with Xanax. So, I mean, it was an explosion of happiness. And uh, he fell asleep on me. You can't fall asleep on that, or it's a deep sleep. And, um, I took him. "Hey man, throw that nigga in a cold shower, throw some ice on his ass and give him about a gallon, two gallons of milk. Y'all pick him up because I can't take him." I could barely…you know what I’m saying.... and I would have signs, like I would vomit blood and shit like that. I didn't care though. As long as I was high, I wasn't tripping.
