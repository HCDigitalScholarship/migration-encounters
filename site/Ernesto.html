Anne:        Okay, this is Anne and I am interviewing EGama3, and I want to start just by thanking you for telling your story to us.

Ernesto:        No, actually thank you because of what y'all are doing. It's very important.

Anne:        We're trying, we're trying, but it's really important for people to hear your stories, and I know it might be painful. So, I'm sorry for this.

Ernesto:        It's okay, thank you.

Anne:        So, Ernesto, or EGama3, I didn't hear any of your survey, so tell me how old were you when you came to the US.

Ernesto:        I was around seven, eight years old, when I left to the States. It was just my dad's decision to get a better life. He used to be a cop here in Mexico, so I'm guessing he didn't like the lifestyle or something like that, so that was his main reason to go to the States.

Anne:        Was he escaping any sorts of violence or criminal activity, do you know?

Ernesto:        I mean, not criminal activity-wise. He was a good cop. I'm guessing he felt that he didn't want us in that type of lifestyle, because most of my family on my dad's sides have been cops, so I'm guessing he didn't want that for us, and one of the main reasons why he took us to the States.

Anne:        So you were seven or eight. Had you been to school here in Mexico?

Ernesto:        Yeah, I was in school. I'm guessing it was elementary or kindergarten or something like that, but yeah, I remember being in school.

Anne:        Do you remember the actual trip to the US?

Ernesto:        Oh yeah, I mean I remember how we crossed the river because I mean that was pretty much how we entered the States.

Anne:        How did you cross the river? Was it a boat?

Ernesto:        No, it was one of the tire things that go inside. They put three of us ... It was me and my other two brothers and they just pulled us from one side to the other.

Anne:        Could you swim?

Ernesto:        I don't think so. I was trying to reach the floor but I wasn't able to touch it, so I'm guessing it was pretty deep. I'm guessing there was a current to it. You really did need the floatable thing that they had.

Anne:        Do you remember your first few days in the States? Do you know what it was like? Did it seem different or was it ...

Ernesto:        We arrived in Texas. Even though we were with some of my dad's friends, it did feel weird. It doesn't feel like home. Everything's new. Even the streets were new. It was pretty different, yeah. It took me a while to adapt to it, I guess.

Anne:        Did you feel like you were leaving friends, relatives? Was there a sense that you were leaving people behind?

Ernesto:        Yeah, my uncles, my grandpa, all the people. I did feel like, "Well, why can't they come with us?" I guess it's part of life's teaching, so I just guess you deal with it pretty well.

Anne:        So what time of year was it?

Ernesto:        Hmm, I would say it was October because my birthday was a month or something after we crossed.

Anne:        And then you started school immediately?

Ernesto:        No, it took us six months til my dad got his things figured out and then, yeah, he took us to school, started elementary school.

Anne:        Texas?

Ernesto:        Uh-huh (affirmative), in Texas, because we were just there for like a year. After that I guess my dad didn't like it and took us to Atlanta, so that was where pretty much I had most of my life here.

Anne:        So, what do you remember about school?

Ernesto:        Well, it was pretty nice. Nothing compared to like here in Mexico. You eat your breakfast, school bus goes and picks you up. That was pretty new for me. Yeah, I actually did like going to school and all that. It was pretty nice.

Anne:        And you had two brothers? Older or younger?

Ernesto:        Yeah, they're younger. They're younger, and my sister. I have a sister too.

Anne:        Younger?

Ernesto:        Yeah, also younger.

Anne:        And they all came with you?

Ernesto:        Yeah, everybody. We all took the same trip. My little brother's back with me here in Mexico now. He also got deported three years ago.

Anne:        So you're growing up in the States. You've got a crew of kids in your household. Did you make friends easily?

Ernesto:        In Texas I did because there was a lot of Hispanic people, so you could talk to them. It wasn't that complicated, but when I moved to Atlanta, I did kind of struggle there. I didn't know how to speak English. It still was taking me a while. So kind of didn't socialize that much until my English started fluent. I started talking more and more, but in the beginning it did cost me a little bit.

Anne:        How long did it take?

Ernesto:        It took me a year, definitely. It was a year until I actually was talking in class.

Anne:        And was there anything about school that was especially good or especially bad?

Ernesto:        I think a lot of things were good. We did have a special teacher helping us, like all the Spanish kids that didn't know English. They helped us with our homework, keeping up with class. I thought it was pretty nice. I had a bit of help.

Anne:        And your parents, what do they do?

Ernesto:        My dad started working at a company, they make glass for perfumes and all that, so that's where he worked at. I mean, he still works there many times, so…

Anne:        Oh really?

Ernesto:        Yeah.

Anne:        Yeah. How about your mom? Who is she?

Ernesto:        She was working there also. She doesn't work anymore, she just stays home. But yeah, she used to work there as well, at the glass company.

Anne:        So was that hard for you guys, if your parents were both working?

Ernesto:        Yeah. There would be a period of 12 hours where we didn't see them at all, so we had to get up early, take the bus. If we missed the bus, we had to walk to school, so pretty much we tried to, you know…

Anne:        How far was that?

Ernesto:        Walking to school was like a 25 minute walk. So yeah, we were trying hard not to miss the bus. We would just get up, get ready, catch the bus. Once in school, like, okay, you catch your practice, start school.

Anne:        And then you get home.

Ernesto:        Mm-hmm (affirmative), get home. Your parents still aren't home, so make something to eat for everybody, make sure everybody's doing their homework.

Anne:        So you were in charge?

Ernesto:        Yeah.

Anne:        At age eight or nine?

Ernesto:        Eight or nine.

Anne:        And do you do well in school?

Ernesto:        Yeah. It was just like ... I mean, my parents would be home around 6:00, so they took over after that and they didn't stop me from doing my things back in school. I was also in sports after they had more time in the house.

Anne:        Did you enjoy the sports?

Ernesto:        Oh yeah. I played soccer. I was on varsity team, and then I started track. I tried football but that wasn't my thing, so just stuck with track. That was it.

Anne:        So you started after-school activities at some point.

Ernesto:        Mm-hmm (affirmative).

Anne:        And did you feel responsible for your younger siblings?

Ernesto:        Yeah, in the beginning I did because Dad has to work, Mom has to work, I’m the oldest one, so got to take care of the kids. Especially when you're getting used to everything, so that was a challenge. Once you grow older it's just like, oh, okay, your schedule has a routine and you're okay and keep with it.

Anne:        Did you all speak English in the house or did you speak Spanish?

Ernesto:        No, in the house it was fully Spanish. Me and my dad didn't speak Spanish. My mom spoke fluent English.

Anne:        Oh she did?

Ernesto:        Yeah she did. So my dad was like, "No English in the house."

Anne:        So did she learn it when she went to the States or did she already know English?

Ernesto:        Yeah, she had already taken some courses here in Mexico and I'm guessing being in the States helped her develop her pronunciation and all that.

Anne:        So how long did you go to school?

Ernesto:        For like 10 years.

Anne:        Did you finish high school?

Ernesto:        Yeah, I finished high school.

Anne:        And you said you played varsity soccer and track?

Ernesto:        And track. Yeah, I was on track team.

Anne:        And then what? Did you think about college?

Ernesto:        Yeah, but I mean, before I could even walk to get my diploma and all that, that's when I got deported. I got pulled over driving without a license and that was my ... the cost for me to be back here.

Anne:        So, in Georgia, you can't get a license without ...

Ernesto:        The social security number, I guess. My dad gave me a car because he knew I needed it, so I was driving ... I knew if it's red, you stop, trying to follow every single rules not to mess up. I guess this day I didn't or something happened and I got pulled over. He asked me for my license. I told him I didn't have it and he just took me in.

Anne:        How old were you?

Ernesto:        I was 18.

Anne:        What month? Right before graduation, you said?

Ernesto:        Yeah, that was before my high school graduation. I really came within a month.

Anne:        But you'd finished up and you were ready to graduate?

Ernesto:        Yeah, it was just the ceremony.

Anne:        Wow. So you had a car and you tried your best to not get stopped. Do you remember what you did wrong to get pulled over?

Ernesto:        Well, he said I took a turn where I wasn't supposed to. I mean, I didn’t consider… I lived around there so I pretty much knew every single turn that you couldn't do and that was what he said, so I don't know what I did wrong. So I was just like, "Okay." If I argue, I might end up losing myself, so I just stayed quiet and waited until I got in contact with my dad and asked him what is there to do.

Anne:        Had you been scared of the authorities before then?

Ernesto:        Before then, I really didn't interact with them, but the ones you see at school, but that was pretty much the only interaction I ever had with a cop.

Anne:        And your parents, do they have licenses or green cards or not?

Ernesto:        My dad does. I don't know how he got his but he has a driver's license.

Anne:        But he didn't at the time or he did at the time?

Ernesto:        As far as I know, he's always had it, because before he took us to the States, he was already living there. He was there for two years before we got there with him, so he had all that stuff settled. Us, we couldn't get it.

Anne:        So, you get pulled over by the cops. Had you been involved with gangs or anything?

Ernesto:        No. My dad was pretty strict. I mean, he was a cop, so everything he said had to be. So that's why we tried not to get in trouble, tried to be involved in school, just to not be at home just thinking about other things. Just keep my mind busy on something good is what helped.

Anne:        Did you have friends from all different nationalities and socioeconomic groups?

Ernesto:        Oh yeah.

Anne:        So you felt like you were integrated?

Ernesto:        Mm-hmm (affirmative), yeah, definitely.

Anne:        And what were your favorite things about living in the US?

Ernesto:        Me—commodity, luxury. Some things you do miss from Mexico, but over there is completely different. I mean, the culture, the people, everybody, we can say they mind their own business, and over here it's completely different. That's, I don't know, lifestyle I guess. That's what I pretty much miss from the States.

Anne:        So when you got picked up, did you try to fight it?

Ernesto:        No. I mean, I just tell them I didn’t have my driver's license and he just told me to step out of the car.

Anne:        But in terms of like being deported, did you try and fight that? The deportation?

Ernesto:        No, I don't know if my dad didn't know what to do but he was just like, just, “Don't do nothing. If they ask you if you want to sign your voluntary departure, go ahead and sign it." That was one of the options, so I was like, "Okay, I'll sign my voluntary departure."

Anne:        So were you detained at all?

Ernesto:        I was detained for six months.

Anne:        Six months?

Ernesto:        Yeah, I was in a detention center, Stewart Detention Center in Georgia. I was there for six months.

Anne:        What was that like?

Ernesto:        To me it was like being in prison. You had your cell, you were with somebody else. They were pretty strict about the times and all that. Even though they had us separated by colors, it was like, "Oh, I can't really go over there because they're red and they're killers. They may do something to me because I haven't done anything." But yeah, it was very scary.

Anne:        Were the guards nice to you or not?

Ernesto:        Not all of them. Some of them were but the others, most of them were pretty ... they would make fun of you. They'll bully you in any way that they could.

Anne:        Did your parents come visit you?

Ernesto:        No. They were afraid that if they would go there, they would get caught, so no.

Anne:        And your siblings, no one?

Ernesto:        Yeah, no. Nobody.

Anne:        That’s lonely.

Ernesto:        Yeah. I was scared. It was a first-time experience that everything, the people, just waking up every day was a new challenge, a new thing to do. It's like, "What am I doing here? Why can't I just go home?"

Anne:        Yeah. Before that happened, had you thought about going to college?

Ernesto:        Yeah, definitely. I was going to find a way to keep going with my education, but I never expected to be back.

Anne:        So you're in detention for six months. Why six months? Just waiting for your trial or a judge?

Ernesto:        Just waiting for something to bring me back. I exactly didn't know what they were waiting for. Every time I would ask, they would be like, "Oh, we'll get back to you next time," and that was pretty much the answer every single time. So one day they said my name. I remember, because it was in December and I got back here December 15, so when they said my name, I just jumped. "Okay, he's taking me back."

Anne:        And did you see a judge at that point when they said your name? Did they bring you before a judge?

Ernesto:        No, no, they didn't bring me ... That was just before they took me to the detention center where I saw the judge.

Anne:        So before you went to the detention center, you saw the judge?

Ernesto:        Yeah.

Anne:        And you signed the voluntary departure?

Ernesto:        No, that was ... Well, I'm guessing that's where the process started because I signed my voluntary departure, it's like three months after when I was in the detention center.

Anne:        Did you have a lawyer or anything?

Ernesto:        No. There was somebody standing there but he said there was nothing I could do and since I wasn't going to fight the case, I'm guessing that's why he didn't say anything.

Anne:        It's crazy that you'd have to sit there for six months ...

Ernesto:        I know.

Anne:        ... if they knew you were going to just go on your own.

Ernesto:        Yeah, I don't know. I'm guessing they were getting all the… gathering people together and making one trip, I don't know what was their idea.

Anne:        It was like someone's trying to make money ... So, they call your name and they just ... Do you get on a bus? What happens?

Ernesto:        Yeah, from a bus, they took us to an airport and then from there, they flew us to Texas. On Texas, they just opened right on the border they opened the gate and they were like, "Welcome to Mexico."

Anne:        Were you scared?

Ernesto:        Yeah, I mean no, no, it was tough. All you saw was people offering you money, like, "Hey, you want to trade your dollars? Hey, you want to trade your dollars?" I hadn't even seen Mexican pesos. I mean, to me, they're like plastic but I didn't know that, so I was like, "That seems fake, seems sketchy." So I just kept my dollars and went to a pharmacy, bought some aspirin and I paid with the dollars. That's why I got the pesos, and then I just called my dad. I was like, "Well, I'm here. What can I do?" Nothing. He just sent me more money so I can get a bus to come to Mexico City, and that's what I did.

Ernesto:        I had to stay at a house for refugees. There was like a house-

Anne:        A shelter?

Ernesto:        Uh-huh (affirmative), for all the deportees. But there was a lot of people in there. You could barely sleep. That's where I spent the night until I could get the money and just get the bus ticket and come to the city.

Ernesto:        Yeah.

Ernesto:        And then back here in Mexico with nothing. I mean, my dad had a house, but ...

Ernesto:        No, mm-mm (negative). No, I was a kid. I remember a few stuff, but I didn't know. I didn't move around. I didn't know all the dangers that are here. I mean, I've been here for eight years now, so now the way I see it, it's like, "Okay, it's just part of Mexico City," you know? I'm kind of used to it now. But when I first got here, yeah, it was scary. Getting back with the people, with society here… I don't know, people are harsh. People are harsh.

Ernesto:        Yeah, he had a house here.

Ernesto:        Nothing, it was just there, abandoned for 10 years that I was over there.
