Lizzy:        He had already been living there?

Jose Luis:        Yeah. He left back in '88.

Lizzy:        Okay.

Jose Luis:        And he went back for us. I think it was in ’92? '91, '92. From there, we just got our visa so we can go over there legally.

Lizzy:        What kind of visa?

Jose Luis:        It was a tourist.

Lizzy:        A tourist visa, okay.

Jose Luis:        We weren't planning to stay. We were just going to go in, get to know California. And then from there, my dad decided just have us stay. From there we just stayed.

Jose Luis:        Not precisely. It was just basically my dad. He was making money at that point. He said we were going to be better. From there, we just stayed basically.

Lizzy:        Okay. This was in Orange County?

Jose Luis:        Orange County. We were in San Clemente.

Jose Luis:        The grocery store. Yeah, they took me to the grocery store. I was like, "Wow," because I have never seen a store that big and clean. I was six years old. I was, like, amazed. From there, that was the memory that I won't forget. It was my first time. I wanted to get everything, basically. It seems like it was just yesterday.

Lizzy:        Really?

Jose Luis:        Yeah.

Lizzy:        How old are you?

Jose Luis:        I'm 37.

Lizzy:        Okay. You still remember that clearly, 30 years later?

Jose Luis:        Yes.

Jose Luis:        Basically it was a drastic change, but for good, basically. It was something impacting your life basically right there like I see it because you see something that amazes you. It's something that… it's right there in your mind.

Jose Luis:        Probably, it took not too long. I don't know. I can't remember how long, but I started right away. I started second grade. Then from there, my life started. I had so many wonderful experiences in school. I even participated in the baile folklorico.

Jose Luis:        It's like a ranchero, you know?

Lizzy:        Yeah. You did this in elementary school?

Jose Luis:        Yeah, elementary school. That was nice. It's good experience. My life started right there.

Jose Luis:        Ms. Johnson, Ms. Chambers, my math teacher, that she was really strict. I love math. And Ms. Johnson, she was my history teacher for sixth grade. Ms. Chambers, it was for fourth grade. They were really, really nice and very helpful. Very helpful. And that’s why… when you have a good memory like that, it's hard for you to forget those things, especially people that they help you. There's a lot of memories. A lot of memories.

Jose Luis:        Then when I graduate sixth grade, then I went to middle school, high school. High school it was the best but at the same time it was my worst.

Lizzy:        Okay. Why is that?

Jose Luis:        I was a—honor student. I became an honor student, had some good grades. Like they say, once you hit high school, it's a whole different thing. It’s a whole different thing. You have to be strong-minded and know what you want. I started hanging out with wrong friends, and that's where everything changed it. My mom, she was always on top of me. My freshman year was wonderful, and then sophomore. My other two years, my mom just let go of the strings, and I start walking the wrong side of the street basically, you would say. Hang out with the bad influence. That's where everything started.

Jose Luis:        They told me, "Hey, bring your car or something." I told my dad, "Hey, can I borrow the car?" "Just be careful." In less than six months, I wrecked two cars. Two cars, for what? For just taking my friends to another town that I didn't even knew what they were going to do, and to beat up some other guys. That's burning the car basically.

Jose Luis:        When I tried to straighten my life back, it was already too late because I was already… Basically, I had a record already. No driver license, reckless driving, suspended license, when I never had one. That's how everything started. The saddest thing is for high school it was when I was working, because I started working, and knowing that it was your graduation day and you were at work. You're not going to graduate. Why? Because you didn't make the credits.

Jose Luis:        At that point, I had my best friend that we were always together. He went over to my job and said, "Today is our graduation day." I'm like, "I know. And we're not graduating," because he didn't graduate either because he was missing credits too. He even started crying. I was about to cry, I was like, "We're going to make it again. There's more opportunities."

Jose Luis:        I was going to Horizons, I don't know if you heard about it? I thought we were going to Horizons to make up the credits, but at that point I met my first wife. I was already working. So we didn't got married but we were living together for three years. And then we just didn't work out.

Lizzy:        If you don't mind, I want to go back to that memory about the graduation day because that sounds like a very powerful memory for you. Was that your hardest? You said that some of the worst times in your life was in high school, was that?

Jose Luis:        Yeah, that was one of the biggest one, and because I started experiencing more about discrimination.

Lizzy:        In what way?

Jose Luis:        Basically the year was about to end, and you have to go turn in your books. That day, I wasn't feeling good to go to school but I went and just turned in my books. I was just walking to the library. Once I was walking, there's another guy said, "Fucking Mexican. Why don't you just go back to Mexico?" So at that point I just turned and basically, sorry about the word, but I just beat the shit out of him. So I didn't like that. I got afraid because I didn't went to school that day and beating up a student, basically it was going to be one of my worst.

Lizzy:        So, this was another student that said that to you?

Jose Luis:        Mm-hmm (affirmative).

Lizzy:        Was it a classmate or someone that you already knew?

Jose Luis:        It was basically, they were at lunch. I was just walking to the library and there was that comment. And basically, I just turned and beat him up.

Lizzy:        What did that feel like inside when you heard him say that comment, someone that should be your peer?

Jose Luis:        Sad and anger. Because basically at that point, I knew what it was but I didn't consider myself like that because I was raised over there since I was a little child. I never needed papers, documentation. I never had to. So I didn't knew about it. Basically, that comment just turn my anger basically and just went at him and just ran. I just ran and since there was a sheriff right there, because at high school there's a sheriff now, they were looking for me and they caught me.

Jose Luis:        They took me to the office and said like, "Well, why didn’t you came to school? Plus, you beat up a student." I just stood quiet. "You're lucky if their parents doesn't press charges." Then when I told him, "So if he press charges, can I press charges too?" "Why you?" "Racist. He told me, 'Fucking Mexican. Go back to Mexico.'" "Is there a witness?" "Yes, there is." "Why didn't you just came and report it? Why you had to beat him up?"

Jose Luis:        That was one of my mistakes, but I didn't think about it. I just reacted and I apologized about it. I said like, "Well." When the principal told the parents what he said, they changed their mind about pressing charges.

Lizzy:        They decided not to press charges?

Jose Luis:        Yeah.

Lizzy:        Okay.

Jose Luis:        Said like, "Well, you're going to be expelled." I'm like, "Okay. That's fine." My mom, she started crying. I was like my head down basically. I was disappointing my parents. That was just my mom at that point. I was afraid even to go home because of my dad. Once we got home, my mom just told him, "You see what he have done?" to my dad.

Jose Luis:        My dad here in Mexico, he was a professional boxer. So, every time that we had on the weekends, my best friend, some other ones go to the house and we practice with my dad. My dad told me, "I didn't show you what you know to start picking up some fights, because you can hurt someone." By knowing boxing, it's basically a responsibility too, because with one single hit, you can really hurt someone.

Lizzy:        So your dad was disappointed that you had used what you learned from him?

Jose Luis:        Yes. That's why it was basically my worst in my high school. Probably, if I had a chance to repeat it, I would just tell my mom not to let go of the leash.

Lizzy:        To let go of what?

Jose Luis:        The leash.

Lizzy:        Not to let go of the leash?

Jose Luis:        Yes.

Lizzy:        You wish that she-

Jose Luis:        Not to give me too much freedom like I had. From there, they received a call from the school saying that they were going to give me another chance to go back to school, but I decided not to. So, they're like, "What are you going to do?" "I already have a job." So, I started working. I left my job and I went to a better one. I started working as a shift manager at Del Taco.

Lizzy:        And now I'm wondering, your decision not to go back to school, was it related to the experience you had gone through, the fight?

Jose Luis:        Yeah. Actually, yes. Emotionally, probably I got hurt because of that, but I told them it's something that I have to let go and continue my life. So basically at that point, every time that they call me names or something, I react. I don't know. It's just something that I picked it up or I don't know. From there, I should say I have a short temper.

Jose Luis:        Basically, if I see someone arguing in the streets like the other day. I came over here and sign my ... What's it called? My resign, because I was going to be rehirable, and they told me, "No, you ain't going to be rehirable." I'm like, "All right."

Lizzy:        This is at?

Jose Luis:        At ____.

Lizzy:        At ____. Okay.

Jose Luis:        I'm like, "All right." From the inside, I was mad. I took the Metro home. I was just doing my own business and everything. There's a guy wanting to get off at the Metro. Once he gets off, pow, right in the back of the head, smack me. "You son of a bitch." I go like, "I'm not in the mood."

Lizzy:        Why do you think he did that?

Jose Luis:        Supposedly, he couldn't get off on time. But I'm all like, "What am I going to do?" We were all smashed in and everything, so I couldn't move more. I'm like, "You son of ..." I'm sorry, but I broke his nose. I was like, "Hey!" Boom. I just had enough of it. People is just trying to take advantage, especially here. If you put your guard down, someone's going to take advantage.

Sometimes, I see people like the cops pulling someone out, bribing them with some money. I'm used to, "Hey, give me my ticket, and I'll just..." That's how I'm used to it. In here, just with 50 pesos, do whatever you want and they'll let you go. That's why it's really corrupted here.

Jose Luis:        When I first got here, I wanted to apply for police department, I’m all like, "You're too old already." I'm like, "Nice."

Lizzy:        Too old?

Jose Luis:        Yeah. They wanted people from 30 and down. I'm like, "30 and down, okay." Without experience, not knowing how to defend themselves, that's why people, they take advantage of. Well, after high school... Let's go back.

Lizzy:        Let's go back. I was just going to say, I want to hear more about your life in the US after high school.

Lizzy:        You moved in with her?

Why?" "You just hire her." Basically, I was one of the top performers at work. Every time they wanted me, I was there. I was really responsible with everything basically.

Jose Luis:        My job at these times were wonderful. "So basically, whatever you want, I have your back. If you want to fire someone, just let me know. I'll back you up. You will have a good reason for him to be get fired." "All right." So, I had the backup. When I told him, "Hey, hire her. Hire her." He's like, "Why? She's ugly." "You just hire her."

Lizzy:        A little workplace romance?

Jose Luis:        Yeah. She started like, "Well, I don’t have any openings!" I'm like, "Hire her in the back! Washing dishes!" "Well, she doesn't know English." "Well, just hire in the washing dishes." "Okay." Well, short story, she hire her. So like, "She's going to be in my shift." I'm like, "Come on, dude."

Lizzy:        That defeats the purpose.

Jose Luis:        At the end, she put her in the middle shift, and I was working late at night. I was coming in at 6:00 and leaving at 3:00 in the morning. That's one of the things.

Jose Luis:        Most of the employees, they knew how I work. When I told them, "Hey, when it's time for fooling around, we can fool around, whatever you want. Do any comments, whatever. Do whatever you want. Even if you want to go sit down, sit down without taking a break or anything. But when I tell you, you do something, I expect for you to do it. At that point, and right." "Okay."

Jose Luis:        So, I started bringing her in. Then one day, I put her in the line making food. So like, "Don't tell him. Okay?" "Just with me, you're going to be in the line." "Okay." Like, "Put her in the line." "All right." He put her. He was like, "When did she learn that?" "I told you. When I commitment to something, I do it. She knows already."
