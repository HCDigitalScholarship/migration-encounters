Ivan

Interviewee

Anita Isaacs

Interviewer

June 15, 2018

Mexico City, Mexico

Ivan:        Are you from California?

Anita:        I am originally from Canada.

Ivan:        Ah, you're Canadian.

Anita:        But I'm also American. I'm also from the US.

Ivan:        Yeah. Yeah, yeah.

Anita:        So, let's talk about some of what you've told me. Maybe we can talk about sports. It seems you're a huge sports fan.

Ivan:        A little bit.

Anita:        What's your team?

Ivan:        Baseball, I like the Angels. I'm Angels, and the football, I like Green Bay.

Anita:        Good choice.

Ivan:        Yeah. Soccer, I like LA Galaxy. That's it. I like boxing too. I love Mayweather. Of course, in soccer I like the Mexican teams.

Anita:        What's your favorite sport?

Ivan:        My favorite sport is football.

Anita:        American football?

Ivan:        Yes. Yes, I play-

Anita:        More than soccer?

Ivan:        Yeah, I played football in high school.

Anita:        Really?

Ivan:        Yeah.

Anita:        What position did you play?

Ivan:        Linebacker. Linebacker. Yeah, yeah. I'm short, but I was good at it. I was good. It's been a long time, but yeah.

Anita:        So, you went to the US as a kid.

Ivan:        Yes.

Anita:        You were six years-

Ivan:        I was six years old.

Anita:        And you went straight to-

Ivan:        Elementary.

Anita:        Yeah, you went to California right away, right?

Ivan:        To California, yeah. Yeah.

Anita:        Do you remember what it was like, when you arrived? I sometimes ask that question.

Ivan:        Yeah, I remember when I arrived I was-

Anita:        That first day, what was it like?

Ivan:        It was beautiful. The houses. It was clean. I thought everywhere was Disneyland, because it looked like the Disneyland houses, you know how every one's different. I remember we lived in a garage. We couldn't afford a room. But it was my dad, my mom, and my sister. We had one bed, and a TV, and a sofa.

Anita:        So four of you?

Ivan:        I was excited. Yeah, it was the four of us.

Anita:        So four of you shared a bed?

Ivan:        Yeah. Yeah. Yeah. My dad was, he worked for Quicksilver. Quicksilver.

Anita:        Quicksilver, like the clothing company.

Ivan:        Yeah, clothing company. My mom worked as a housekeeper.

Anita:        As a garment worker?

Ivan:        He was in packing. He used to pack stuff. I liked it. I loved it. My first word in English was "please".

Anita:        Please?

Ivan:        Please, yes.

Anita:        Wow. Who taught you that?

Ivan:        Because when I got my first day in school, everyone, like the whole kids, "Oh please, teacher. Please, please." Everybody was saying "please", so I was like, "What does this mean?" It sounded nice, you know. So that was my first word.

Anita:        Did you know what it meant?

Ivan:        No, at first I didn't, but then I learned.

Anita:        Did you still like it when you found out what it was?

Ivan:        Yes, I loved it. Of course. Yeah.

Anita:        Did you like school?

Ivan:        I loved ... I was really good in school. I was on O-Club. I was really good. I actually got a sponsor to go to college and all that, but my parents got divorced when I was like 11. That affected me a lot. The American dream became a nightmare, because my mom had to work a lot, and I got introduced to drugs when I was pretty young, when I was 13. I got involved in gangs. That changed my life. It changed my life. But now I've been clean for seven years now. I do counseling. I do AA, and I-

Anita:        Can you talk about what happened a little bit, with your mom and your dad? What happened?

Ivan:        My mom had three children here in Mexico. My dad was kind of machista. You know, he told me, "It's only us. Forget about them." And my mom brought them to the States without my dad knowing. When he found out, he got really mad. He left the house.

Anita:        I'm sorry.

Ivan:        My mom had to work a lot. It was only my sister and I at the house. I remember in school the Mexican-Americans and the Mexican immigrants, we got discriminated a lot by the Mexican-Americans. They used to call us “wetbacks.” They used to call us names. We didn't know a lot about the language,  you  know the culture. And one time, one of my friends got bullied by one of the Mexican-Americans. I defended him, so I got in a fight. And they beat me up, like five Mexican-American kids.

Ivan:        When I went back to the neighborhood, they saw me bleeding and all that, all these guys. "What happened? Who fucked you up?" "It was those guys, the guys from the other neighborhood, the Mexican-American kids." So the next day the whole neighborhood came over to defend me. To me, that was like, "They love me," you know? "They care about me."

Ivan:        I had a lot of insecurity with my dad, because he left. (emotional) It affected me a lot. I love my dad, so they became like my family. That's why I got dragged into it, in the beginning.

Anita:        How old were you?

Ivan:        I was 12.

Anita:        You were 12 when this happened?

Ivan:        Yeah. I was 12. I still did good in school. I started working when I was like 15, with a fake social security and a fake ID, because my mom didn't make enough money. So I started working pretty young. I used to work like from 6:00 to 3:00 in the morning, so I would only sleep like three, four hours, and then go to school. Yeah. Yeah.

Anita:        Mexican-Americans discriminated against-

Ivan:        Yeah, Mexican ...

Anita:        Immigrants?

Ivan:        ... immigrants, yes. We got more discriminated by them, than with White people or Asians, or Blacks.

Anita:        Really?

Ivan:        Yes.

Anita:        Why? I mean, you're a psychologist now. How do you explain that? What do you think?

Ivan:        I think it's more in the culture that, I think that's why the culture is like this. We are very patriots, you know we love being Mexicans. But there's a lot of envy. There's a lot of, "If you do better than I do, I don't like you." Americans don't have that. It's part of the culture. They felt like Americans, you know? And when they ... I don't know. It's-

Anita:        You mean, they felt that they were better than you, or they were insecure that you were going to be better than them?

Ivan:        No, they felt better than us. They felt better than us. But then, I tried my best learning English. I got into the football team, and I got adapted to the culture. I even denied I was Mexican, when I was young. I always told people, "I'm a Mexican-American." It was a lie, I know, but because I loved the ... I grew up over there. I felt American. I love the politics. I love history. I was involved in ... But then, when I was like in high school, in my freshman year, I wanted to leave, because in the history book they only had like four pages about the Mexican history: the Aztecs, the Mexican-American War, and a few things more. You know?

Anita:        Yeah.

Ivan:        And about the Revolution. But I wanted to learn more. Like, Americans didn't like you because you're not White. You have to be White. But Mexicans didn't like you either, because you don't have their culture. So we're like in-between.

Anita:        You felt in-between in the US?

Ivan:        Yeah. And even here, too. Yeah.

Anita:        Yeah, but in the US too, you felt in-between.

Ivan:        Yeah. Yeah. Yeah. And the US.

Anita:        So why were you so fascinated by Mexico? As a-

Ivan:        Because I wanted-

Anita:        ... as a teenager?

Ivan:        ... to belong to something. I wanted to be proud of something, something that might motivate me to do good, you know? Like I wanted to, I'd learned a lot about the Aztecs, the Mayans, the Aztlan, the Olmecas, and I wanted to know why America was such a great country. It wasn't Mexico if ... I love geography. I love the culture and all that, so why, if we're such a good country, we have all this natural incomes, like we got the oil, we got the land, we got all this, we're beautiful. Why are we a terrible country? That was always-

Anita:        "Why are we a terrible country"?

Ivan:        Yeah, a third world country.

Anita:        A third-world country.

Ivan:        Yeah. Why? Why? Why? Why the Americans succeeded, and why we didn't, you know? That was always, it got into the history and all that.

Anita:        So this is as a young teenager you're talking about.

Ivan:        Yes. Yes, that was as a ... I'm in love with history, with politics. That's part of me. But when I got into those two drugs, and gangs, I failed my objective. I failed it. I got lost.

Anita:        But it seems to me like you were doing both at the same time.

Ivan:        I was kind of doing both.

Anita:        And you were working.

Ivan:        Yes.

Anita:        How did you manage to do all these three things?

Ivan:        My mom, and when my dad was with us, they always encouraged us to work hard, to do good, to always ... like, if you don't work, you don't deserve a plate, you know? To us, working was a priority. Unfortunately, they didn't really encourage me with the education. But I liked it. I liked it. I liked going to school. Right now, I'm 35, and I'm going to school.

Anita:        So they didn't emphasize school as much as working, your parents?

Ivan:        No. No, no. It wasn't a priority in our family. No.

Anita:        This came from something within you.

Ivan:        Yes. Yes.

Anita:        Yeah. How did the gangs that you were hanging out with feel about your interests in history, and politics, and school, and-

Ivan:        No, they thought I was stupid. Once I hit prison, I got involved with trafficking drugs. When I hit the prison, they encouraged me to, "Man, bro, like" ... It becomes a different ... like the homies in there, the people that were involved in the gangs, like educate yourself. We need smart people, you know? We have a lot of stupid people, naïve people already. We want people to study. And I love reading, so I became a reader.

Anita:        In prison?

Ivan:        In prison, yeah.

Anita:        Okay. I want to come back. But I want to go back.

Ivan:        Yeah, yeah, yeah.

Anita:        What did the gangs say? How did the gangs think of your interest in all these things, or did you keep it secret from them?

Ivan:        It was kind of like my private life. Being a gang member, it was about taking care of the neighborhood, and being in fights with other gangs. It's about the money. You know, hustling. I didn't steal. I never stole anything or assault anybody. It was most drugs, drug-selling. You know, selling drugs and all that.

Anita:        In the end, you left history and all those passions and education. You chose the other.

Ivan:        Yeah.

Anita:        Why?

Ivan:        Because I became an addict at a young age. I became an addict. Now I'm... My first drug was meth, methamphetamines. That was powerful. I never did weed, marijuana, cocaine, nothing. I stayed with meth, and it became a problem in my life for a long time. For a long time. I think that's why I got deported from the United States.

Anita:        So you trafficked drugs from Mexico into the US?

Ivan:        No. We used to manufacture it. We used to cook it, and send it to-

Anita:        You manufactured it locally, in the US, and then-

Ivan:        Yeah, in the US. Yeah.

Anita:        That's why we can't buy Sudafed anymore.

Ivan:        No. No.

Anita:        It's your fault! Every time I get a sinus headache, I get mad at you guys.

Ivan:        Yeah, yeah. I was part of that. I was part of that. But that's my past. That's the past. My life changed here. I'm actually, now I'm grateful that God brought me here. Yeah.

Anita:        So you get involved in all of that, and then what happened? You got arrested?

Ivan:        I got arrested. I would get arrested and get released, get arrested and get released. After 9/11, the whole game changed. All of a sudden I had an INS hold.

Anita:        You had a what?

Ivan:        An INS hold. Yeah. And they told me I was going to get deported. I got deported, and then I went back. But then I couldn't stop doing drugs. It was really hard. I got married very young. Then my wife is over there, my daughter too.

Anita:        How old were you when you got married?

Ivan:        I was 20. 20 years old.

Anita:        Was this to a gang member?

Ivan:        After I got married and my daughter was born ... I left the gang side of it like when I was 18. It wasn't for me anymore.

Anita:        So you didn't meet your girlfriend through the gangs.

Ivan:        No. No. She's actually a good Christian girl. Yeah, she doesn't smoke. She doesn't drink. She goes to school. She's going to college. She treated my daughter ... I mean, she's an excellent mom. But she got tired of my addiction. There were times when I would try to work, but there were times when I would relapse and start in on that process again.

Anita:        Did you ever go to rehab?

Ivan:        I never wanted to go to rehab. I'm religious. I believe in ... I'm a Christian, and I always thought that through Christianity I was going to stop doing drugs. But it takes more than that. It takes more than that to get me off. Now that I'm studying psychology, it's psychology, it's your body, your biología, your social and your spiritual, all those four things are combined. You can't not, just through prayer, or just because I feel good in the sermon I'm going to stop doing drugs. It takes more than that.

Anita:        Let me ask you a question, because of how we're having this conversation. Part of this project was also so that American citizens can better understand migrants. This is why I love what I'm doing, so that we understand we're all human beings. We make mistakes. Some of us don't make mistakes, but for kids, we grow up, we ...

Ivan:        Yeah.

Anita:        If you were to explain to ... If like I say, "Okay, I met this incredible former gang member and meth dealer." And somebody says, "What are you talking about?" How would you explain so that an American can understand why kids like you are drawn into this world? Like what is it about ...

Ivan:        Because, it has-

Anita:        Is it something about US society? Is it something about the way you live? How do you see it?

Ivan:        It has to do with the culture. It has to do with, a lot of us, when we immigrate to the United States, you see we come from these third-world countries. All of a sudden, you're able to reach to anything. Everything, it's available to you. If you want to go to high school, college, you have all these good, positive options. But what happens with my culture is that we're running away from poverty. We want money. We want to eat. We want to have a house.

Ivan:        But when we get there ... like I remember I wanted to get, I wanted to do things right, you know? I wanted to get a job. I wanted to go to college. But since I was an immigrant, I couldn't. It was too expensive. You are always under the table, you know? And with, I believe the family's the most important thing. That's something Mexicans have, very strongly. They believe that, in family. But when you go over there, we as migrants, we have to work two jobs. My mama had to work two jobs. And unfortunately, they separate it, and you don't have the resources or the encouragement to ...

Anita:        Let me ask you another question about that. Is there something about, a lot of people talk about their families breaking up.

Ivan:        Yes. Yes.

Anita:        Is that the strain of the amount of work you need to do? Is that, why do so many Mexican families break up, in the States?

Ivan:        I believe if you don't have have ambition, ambición, if you don't have una meta-

Anita:        Yeah, a goal.

Ivan:        ... a goal, you have nothing. You could have the best family. You could be very smart. But if you don't have ambición, we don't have a purpose. You get that through education, through ... I believe a lot of Mexican-Americans, a lot of Mexicans that migrate over, they're very naïve. They don't take education as important as you guys do. And because money through drugs, through trafficking, it's easy, we get involved with that. We want the good cars, we want a house. And since we're illegal, it's really hard to get that, so we go through that.

Anita:        Do you think if people weren't illegal, that there would be less gangs, that there would be less trouble? That kids would ... I mean what is it? Is it the strain of having to work so hard, on the family? I'm just trying to figure it out, as somebody who's sympathetic. I want to tell the positive. What can we do?

Ivan:        A lot of, we lose our culture. I think it has to do with the culture a lot, because we don't belong to nobody. You know? We lose our culture. We don't have a vision, and we don't have a purpose. A lot of these young kids don't have metas, they don't have goals.

Ivan:        Right now I'm a counselor. I've worked in the clinic, and here it's a little bit different. Poor people in Mexico is different than poor people in the United States.

Anita:        In what way?

Ivan:        Right here, the economy, you could be poor but you're good, right? In the States, if you're poor most likely you're an addict. Most likely you're lazy, because you have all these opportunities over there, if you're a citizen. Like I remember when I was in county jail, they told, "If you're convicted and you want to go to college, the government will pay you to go to school. But you got to be a citizen."

Ivan:        I wanted to join the Army, when the war was going on. I couldn't, because I wasn't legal. Like, I wanted to belong to something. I wanted to be part of society, and we're always under the table.

Anita:        Gangs offer you a sense of belonging.

Ivan:        Yeah. Gangs, that sense of belonging. At first, you go with their sight. Oh, they have cars. They have girls. They've got money. Unfortunately, I believe there's a meth epidemic through the whole country, and now it's infected the Mexican communities too. We have it in the northern states of Mexico, in Sonora, in Sinaloa, all those northern states. We have a lot of ... I believe that has a huge impact on our community. Yeah, it's a big deal. That's why I love to do what I do, because I'm a survivor, you know? I'm not an addict. I'm a survivor. I consider myself a survivor. I consider myself with a purpose.

Anita:        Does your message get through to kids here?

Ivan:        Yes. Yes. I sponsor about 20 kids. Young adults. I do coaching leadership. I do AA, and I do spiritual. I'm a Christian as well, but I'll only work with people that want to receive the word of God, you know? If you're not Christian, I respect that. We have other ways to work with you. The plan here is to have a spiritual awakening, to have a purpose, to have new values, new principles, that you put before your emotions. Mexicans are very emotional. We're high tension, almost coléricos… nos guiamos por nuestras emociones. (we are driven by our emotions) It has to do with the culture, too, you know what I mean?

Anita:        I do know what you mean. Are there as many broken homes in Mexico, as there were of Mexicans living in the US?

Ivan:        No. There's more broken homes in the US.

Anita:        Why is that?

Ivan:        Because of the culture shock they get, when they arrive to the United States, I think. That's what I think.

Anita:        The culture shock. How does that impact your families?

Ivan:        New language. New society, new rules.

Anita:        Do you consider yourself Mexican or American?

Ivan:        American. American.

Anita:        You answered that right away.

Ivan:        Yeah. I have no doubt.

Anita:        What about you is American?

Ivan:        I have the same... First of all because of the religion probably, too. There's a lot of more Protestants over there. The way I was brought up, the spirit of the American dream. Now I'm doing my American dream in Mexico. Yeah.

Anita:        Is it possible?

Ivan:        Yes, it is. Because it's not a matter of a border. It's tu identidad… tu pensamiento. (it’s your identity…. your way of thinking) It's in your mind. It's not the country. It's the spirit.

Anita:        Do you think that if you hadn't lived in the US, you would be doing all this coaching work and working with drug addicts? Is that something also that comes from your experience in the US? It's a bit of a leading question. I should have asked that in a different way, but what do you think?

Ivan:        Yeah. I believe in predestination.

Anita:        What?

Ivan:        In predestination.

Anita:        That things are predestined.

Ivan:        Yeah. My belief is that God allowed all that, so I could do this now. My experience, my dad leaving the house, the gangs, the drugs, my failure in marriage, all that, now I can use it as a positive thing to help others. Like, I don't see it as a bad or a good. I see it as an experience, now. And with that, if I'm talking to you, I could have empathy with you, and I could work with you, and I could guide you through my experience. Yeah. Yeah.

Anita:        How many children do you have?

Ivan:        I have a daughter.

Anita:        A daughter who's an American.

Ivan:        American, yeah.

Anita:        Do you see her? Have you seen her?

Ivan:        Through Messenger, on her mom. She's legal in the States, but she can't leave the United States.

Anita:        Her mom.

Ivan:        Her mom, yeah. Once she gets her documents then, like, she's going to be able to come. I lived in TJ, Tijuana for four years, because my dad would bring my daughter to the border. I was two hours away from the border. But in Tijuana, in the northern states, there's a lot of drug addicts.

Anita:        You had to get away.

Ivan:        That wasn't helping. I needed to get away. It was, first I have to love myself in order to love you. First I got to be right with me, you know what I'm saying, to be able ... I had to learn that. Yeah. At first it was, "Oh, my daughter. My daughter, I want to see my daughter." But I would relapse. I was always try to do good. Like I always wanted to function in society. But something about meth addiction, you can't function. If you're a cocaine addict, you could probably work and have a family, and still manage your addiction, if you smoke marijuana, or alcohol. But with meth you can't do that.

Anita:        Why? What does meth do to you?

Ivan:        Meth, there's a few processes when you become, is when you only try it, when you only experience, when you become abusive, and then when you become a dependent. There's three steps. With meth, you go from experiencing to depending on it. It's very addictive.

Anita:        Is it very quick?

Ivan:        Quick. Quick. I believe it's more addictive than heroin, because it eats in your mind. Eats in your mind. I believe it's only one out of 100 meth addicts makes it.

Anita:        So you're one of 100.

Ivan:        Yes. I'm special. I am very special. I believe that. I believe God has a purpose in my life. Yeah.

Anita:        So what happens to the others? Do they just overdose and die?

Ivan:        Yeah. You don't get an overdose with meth, unless you do it like a lot, a lot. But it affects your central nervous system, your dopamine. I mean, it's terrible. You get meth mouth.

Anita:        You get organ failure, don't you?

Ivan:        Yeah. You don't sleep, you don't eat. You get paranoid. You can get schizophrenic with meth. I mean, it's crazy. It's crazy. Yeah.

Anita:        Why did you do meth, instead of coke, or heroin, or anything?

Ivan:        Because the first drug I tried, it was meth, and when I tried coke, it didn't do anything. Meth is 10 times stronger than cocaine, 10 times, and 10 times more addictive.

Anita:        How old were you?

Ivan:        13 years old, when I first started.

Anita:        You were 13 years old.

Ivan:        Yes. I was 13.

Anita:        You were a little boy.

Ivan:        I was. I was a little boy. Yeah. Even when I was in football and all that, I would smoke in the bathrooms. I would start shaking. I thought it was, I couldn't control it. But that's what we want to stop here. Prevention. We're doing a lot of prevention here in Mexico.

Anita:        But despite that, you still played high school football?

Ivan:        Yes.

Anita:        Despite being a meth addict.

Ivan:        Yes. Yes.

Anita:        How'd you manage to do that?

Ivan:        It was hard. Meth isolated me a lot. I’m… soy muy extrovertido, (very extroverted) extrovert. Meth got me to the other side, introvertido. (introverted) I had to stay quiet and all that. If there's drugs, I think drug addiction in the United States, I mean you know the United States is the number one in the world that consumes drugs. That's why you have all these problems. I think it has to ... It destroys the family, especially with migrants. Yeah. I think it's one of the main reasons why Mexicans fail at the American dream. To me it's a big problem. That's what I-

Anita:        Do you think that if American society was more welcoming-

Ivan:        Yeah, you know what?

Anita:        Is it, who's ... I mean, we know-

Ivan:        My experience-

Anita:        Let's put Trump on one side, right?

Ivan:        Yeah. Yeah. My experience with Americans, it was great. They loved us. They loved my mom. They had always helped us. They always gave me, I always, "Why are they so happy? Why do they succeed?"

Anita:        Why do what?

Ivan:        Why do they succeed? Why are they so ... They have a purpose. Like most Americans go to college. Not every, not in general, but the people I used to hang around with. I always wanted to imitate the American culture, you know? Even here in Mexico when I work, I'm like, "You look so confident of yourself." Because I got it from over there. I loved the American culture. I loved it. I loved it. I always dreamed of having a house, drinking tea and reading my book, and I'd decorate my house American style, you know?

Anita:        How would that be?

Ivan:        I'd do it. I have it now.

Anita:        What is it like, American style?

Ivan:        Oh, I love it. I love it.

Anita:        What does your house look like?

Ivan:        Like Americans'.

Anita:        Which is what?

Ivan:        I got cuadros. (paintings) I got roses and I got my bookshelf, something Mexicans don't have, because we don't read that much. I got my sofa. I got my carpet, you know? Because I'm used to it up there.

Anita:        You don't have a white fence though.

Ivan:        No, I don't. No I don't. No I don't. I don't. But yeah, it's-

Anita:        Well. One last question. In prison, what was it like, being not a citizen?

Ivan:        It wouldn't count that much. It's more about the culture.

Anita:        More about what?

Ivan:        More about the culture. At the end I became like the Mexican-Americans. But I never discriminated my people. Never. I always helped them. I was like, "Bro, you need something? Bro, you need a translation? I'd help you out." I was always like that. In prison, Mexicans are divided into three groups. The Northerns, the South, and the Paisos, which are the migrants. I was a Southern, because I belonged to a gang in Southern California, and I got tattooed so I couldn't deny it. But after a few months, I started ... That's what-

Anita:        Who were the Northerns?
