Vic

Interviewee

Sergio Diaz

Interviewer

June 4, 2019

Mexico City, Mexico

Sergio:        My name's Sergio and I'm here doing the interview with Vic. Hi Vic, thanks for joining us. So first of all, I want to thank you so much for your time and everything that you're going to share with us today. We know that it can be really difficult to kind of share some of these stories and remember things that might not have been as pleasant, but the stories that you share and everything that you've been through is really going to help us uncover some things that we could do better, and hopefully provide better recommendations on how to improve the situation. It's not just for you, but for other people going through the same thing. So really thank you for that, and thanks for your time.

Vic:        No problem.

Sergio:        So the first question we have here is why did your family migrate to the US?

Vic:        For a better future. They see no future in Mexico, so they wanted to go set something better for me and my sister.

Sergio:        Leaving, when your family left, what did you guys leave behind?

Vic:        Well, they left all their family behind. They went to somewhere that they had no clue what the regulations and rules were, so it was pretty hard for them to start with. I mean, they grew up all their life right here. And to go to somewhere where they had no one, I mean I think that's pretty... They left behind a lot.

Sergio:        How old were you when you first got to the US?

Vic:        About to be two years old.

Sergio:        Two years old, okay. What's your first memory of the US?

Vic:        I mean, when my parents got their first apartment. But I mean, I was really little, so I don't remember much when I got there.

Sergio:        How about the first memory that you have, that you say, "Oh yeah, I remember this."

Vic:        Yeah. Well, it must have been when we got our first apartment. Because everything else was struggling in the beginning, until they got their apartment, that's when everything got better. I think that's why that's the only memory that stands out.

Sergio:        And did you go to school?

Vic:        Yes. I went to kindergarten. Well, preschool and then kindergarten. And then I graduated high school and all that, and I had to start working but on my own, once I graduated, because financially, we were unstable and stuff. So I just had to find ends meet through my part.

Sergio:        So when you were going to school, do you remember any teachers or friends that you went to school with?

Vic:        Yeah, I actually kept in touch with one of my teachers from fifth grade, and then I still keep in touch with friends, even though I'm over here.

Sergio:        Was there ever a moment that you realized, "Oh hey, I'm undocumented?"

Vic:        Yeah. When my friends were going to Sea World and stuff, and then my parents would tell me, "No, you might get stopped." And I was 10 years old, so I didn't really know what was going on. So I just had to understand in my way of form, you know?

Sergio:        What was that like?

Vic:        It was hard. Not to be able to join a soccer team because they travel. Like I would meet qualifications and stuff to be on the certain soccer team, but I couldn't be in that team because they traveled through airplane and stuff, so it was really hard. I couldn't do things that other people could have.

Sergio:        So you were really good at soccer?

Vic:        Yeah.

Sergio:        Did you play in high school?

Vic:        Yes, I did.

Sergio:        What position?

Vic:        Midfield.

Sergio:        Yeah. Did you ever get any high school awards for it?

Vic:        No. Once I got high school, my mind was on something else because my parents separated during high school, so I had to take care of my sister on my own and that's when my focus on school was fading away, and more into financial like stability. So I was looking more into jobs and stuff. By the time I reached the junior high, I was already asking my dad if I could help him out or someone else for extra money.

Sergio:        So when your parents split up, what happened?

Vic:        Well, it was hard because I can imagine, my parents got here, they were struggling, and at the point that they separated, I really like understood that we were undocumented so I was just like, what is my dad going to do? What is my mom going to do? They're two undocumented people in like this big world and I really didn't know what to understand of it, what was going to come from it.

Sergio:        So where did they go after they split?

Vic:        My dad rented his own room and we rented an apartment. So we stayed in the same city but different homes.

Sergio:        Then you had to—you said—be the one to take care of your sister.

Vic:        Yeah.

Sergio:        Is your sister a US citizen?

Vic:        Yes.

Sergio:        What was it like to have... You were having to be the person to step into that?

Vic:        Well, it was big shoes to fill because the only thing I knew how to make was mac and cheese or rice and stuff or cereal, but that's why it was really hard. And then I had to focus on making sure she did her homework and then sometimes I would lose focus about my homework. So yeah, it was hard.

Sergio:        What was it like… Yeah. How did it feel when other people that were the same as you were going on these soccer trips and being documented? Did that affect the way that you wanted to compete and how competitive you were?

Vic:        Well, yeah, because they would tell me, "Let's go to Arizona, let's go to Miami." And then I'd be like, "How am I going to do this?" I would talk to my mom and she'd tell me, "You just have to tell them you have family issues or something.” So that’s why I couldn’t. My mindset wasn't always at the greatest point that it could be. When I was little, yeah, because I'm a little kid, I don't know what's going on. But once I started realizing there's more opportunity, more doors could be open, then that's when you start getting shut down because you don't have the rights to do this or that.

Sergio:        Did any of your friends know you were undocumented?

Vic:        Just a couple, I mean very few. Only like two.

Sergio:        So before you found out, what did you want to be in life?

Vic:        Well, I always looked into architecture. So engineering and stuff. I mean I think that's really cool. That's what I wanted to study. I was actually trying to go to a trade school for machinery, and then work my way into architecture and stuff, but I couldn't do it because my social security wasn't going through and that was through DACA. So once I hit that point, I was like, "Man. What am I really going to do for myself? I'd rather just start working and find my ends meet through some other way." But I really did want to keep studying after I graduated high school because there's nothing good that comes out of life if you don't study after that. And especially for someone like me, that's coming from a low end family, it's really hard.

Sergio:        You also had to take good care of your sister.

Vic:        Yeah, exactly. I was just focused more on money, but there's no way of really making money because I couldn't go to school, I couldn't get this decent job because I didn't have certain scholarships or stuff like that. That's why I really was focused into going to college right after that, but if a trades school wasn't accepting me, then what made me think a community college was going to accept me?

Sergio:        For me, one thing I was really surprised about is that you had, as an undocumented person, had to take care of a US citizen sister.

Vic:        Yeah, exactly.

Sergio:        And step into that role. I don't think that would have been easy. Did you ever think about that? Like, "Wow, why is the system treating me like this if I have my US citizen sister?"

Vic:        Well I mean, not really, because at the time, I wasn't really focused on too much things like that. Yeah, life isn't fair but it's unfair for a lot of people. I'm not just going to put my case out there just because I didn't have papers. A lot of people don't have papers or documentation and they still make it in life. So that's why I wasn't really focused on little things like that. Those things just put you down, at the end of the day.

Sergio:        Did you ever feel that was unfair for your sister?

Vic:        Well I mean yeah, because she's watching us struggle. At this point, she doesn't even know what to do in life. She studies and stuff, but her brother's in one place that she grew up with, and then her parents are in a different place where they're crying their eyes out, not knowing what to do, if their baby is going to be safe the next day. You know what I mean? Like I said, there's a lot of struggles and it's hardship a lot in Mexico. So, they worry a lot. They're on my phone every single day. They call me nonstop just to make sure I'm okay.

Sergio:        You worked in the US?

Vic:        Yeah.

Sergio:        What was your favorite job there?

Vic:        I think working mechanic with my dad. I think that was the best part. I like hands on work. Customer service is fun, but when you got customers yelling at you and stuff, just because they paid extra money for something that they did buy, it's hard. That's why Walmart, I wasn't really too much into it.

Sergio:        So you want to work there as an auto?

Vic:        Yeah.

Sergio:        Okay. What kind of cars would you work on?

Vic:        Well, my dad, whatever cars came in. Nissans, Fords. Nothing luxurious. You know? The most luxurious was my dad's Mercedes, but that's pretty much all we got luxury and stuff.

Sergio:        What was your favorite fix? Like, when the car came in with a problem.

Vic:        I didn't really get that much training. I only worked with him for a year, but it was probably changing the oil. The simple things. You know? But watching my dad undo a whole engine, that surprised me because all the pieces that it carries inside.

Sergio:        What's your least favorite thing about the US?

Vic:        The racism. That's pretty much it. The racism. I mean, you got black that hate Mexicans, Mexicans that hate white, white that hate black and it just never ends. It's not fair for no one. How are you supposed to live safe or calm if you're going to have hatred like that all over you go?

Sergio:        Did you experience any discrimination in the US?

Vic:        Not really. I didn't tend to stand out a lot. Going to high school, no one's really going to put me out there because my English is as good as theirs. That's why they're not going to put... I've seen other people put down people that just came to the US. I've seen that, but it hurts, but there's not much I could do. I'm not going to risk my high school career just to get suspended because some guy was getting talked down to. Which I wish I did do something, but at the end of the day, it’s not… it’s really not worth it. You could just talk to that person afterwards and let him know, "I know things are rough, but keep your head up."

Sergio:        Was there anything that you ever wanted to do that you couldn't because of your status?

Vic:        Study. I really wanted to get into studying, but for some reason, that's what tripped me out. I tried getting into a trades school and they wouldn't let me get in there for some reason. Just because of my social security. I mean I thought that because I had the DACA, that meant I had a good social security status. That's what bummed me out. That's why afterwards, I didn't even look into community college and stuff.

Sergio:        So after that moment, how did you end up in Mexico? How did you end up back in Mexico?

Vic:        I got deported and then... I mean, it all happened so unexpected. The first thing I did was get a hotel right there in TJ, and then I came all over here because my grandparents were here.

Sergio:        What happened on the US side that night that you got caught by police? What were you doing?

Vic:        Oh, I was driving under the influence of alcohol.

Sergio:        But before. Like how’d you get drunk? Who were you drinking with?

Vic:        Oh, it was just with some friends. And then I remember some girl just got her apartment and then we said let's go over there, and I guess we were supposed to crash there and I didn't want to crash there, I'd rather go home. After that, once fresh air hit me, I guess that's when my liquor hits, got to the fullest, and I blurred out. And I was driving recklessly on the freeway. I didn't get pulled over, exiting the freeway, I actually crashed. No one got hurt or anything, but the axles on the car actually broke, so I had nowhere to go. But luckily, I was by myself and stuff.

Sergio:        So you were at a housewarming party that night?

Vic:        Yeah.

Sergio:        Everyone else that was there, were they US citizens or?

Vic:        Yeah. I've never really hung out with people that were undocumented. No one really throws their status out there. "Hey look, I'm undocumented." No one actually says that. It's really hard to point them out. There's people that don't understand English or speak it very not fluent, so that's the only reason you can tell, "Oh, this guy's been in Mexico for a while."

Sergio:        Did they know you were undocumented?

Vic:        No. No, they didn't.

Sergio:        Why?

Vic:        Well, that's something me and my family always left as, you know, don't let that bother you, don't let no one know, it's not their business and stuff. It was that type of thing.

Sergio:        I see.

Vic:        Yeah.

Sergio:        So after you were caught by police, then...

Vic:        They gave me a breathalyzer and then they took me in. They actually let me out the next morning. Or I believe it was the next night, they actually let me out and then they told me, "Here, you have to present yourself to this courthouse and stuff and we're going to settle things in court." I was like, okay. After that, everything went fine. And that's when I got my second DUI and that's when everything got under the radar, and once I went to one of my court cases and they actually took me in, and they told me that I was get taken in by customs of ICE...

Sergio:        So what happened to your second DUI?

Vic:        The same thing. Just driving drunk and stuff.

Sergio:        With a friend?

Vic:        Yeah.

Sergio:        At a friend's place?

Vic:        No, I was with my girlfriend. We were coming back from the movies and stuff. And for some reason, I don't know what happened, the car just slid to the right and then just flipped. But we weren't doing nothing, we were literally just driving but since we had the bottle in the car, they didn't even take me in that day because I wasn't even drunk, I had more of a smell of liquor than actual drunk, because they took my breathalyzer and stuff, but since I was driving drunk and the car flipped, that's the reason they pulled us over and stuff. Because they didn't take no one in, they didn't take me or my ex-girlfriend in.

Sergio:        So they stopped you guys?

Vic:        Well, I mean...

Sergio:        They charged you with a DUI?

Vic:        Yeah, a second DUI.

Sergio:        And then you went to court?

Vic:        Yeah, they took me to court and then that's when I had more cases... I just had finished my first DUI, paying everything and stuff, so it was perfectly fine until... And then we came back from the movies and they took me to court once again, and that's when I kept showing up to court, it was little by little, I guess the system picked me up as undocumented. It keeps coming up in court, what's going on, and that's when Immigration was really tough. One DUI, yes, I understand they'll forgive you, but the second DUI, that's really difficult to get out of. Especially when it comes to driving drunk, because you're putting other people's life in danger.

Sergio:        And that was a year and a half ago?

Vic:        Yeah. No, actually, it was like two and a half years ago.

Sergio:        So what happened after you got caught and they put you in the system? How'd you end up with ICE?

Vic:        Well, I guess they just hand me over. They picked up my case. Like I mentioned, I probably picked up the radar in the system or something and then ICE said, "Go for this guy, he has a court case right now, so just come pick him up."

Sergio:        So they picked you up in the state court?

Vic:        Not the state court, it was the city court.

Sergio:        Where at?

Vic:        ____. No, I believe it was ____. Yeah.

Sergio:        So they showed up to city court in _____, and how did they pick you up?

Vic:        Well, I was already in handcuffs and stuff and they told me, "Alright, you're going to be released." Well, usually when you're released in city court, they release you with everyone and this one came as a surprise, that I was being brought out by myself, and they put my hands behind my back and they handcuffed me and stuff, and when I walked out, I actually seen the car of ICE, the ICE truck. So I was like, “damn.” And the first thing that came to mind was run, but I mean where am I going to run to? I might run to hug my mom and say goodbye, but that's not something I'd be able to do because I'm not going to go on the run forever either. That's not a way to live. I messed up so I'd rather own up to it.

Sergio:        Was ____a sanctuary city?

Vic:        A what?

Sergio:        Sanctuary city?

Vic:        I don't know.

Sergio:        That’s weird.

Vic:        Yes. I don't remember, it was ___

Sergio:        In California?

Vic:        Yeah, in California, in the same city area.

Sergio:        Aside from DUI's, were you involved in any other crimes?

Vic:        I had a vandalism case. I was just walking through the train tracks and they picked me up and stuff. I had a black backpack and they thought I was out there doing, you know?

Sergio:        Graffiti?

Vic:        Yeah, something. I was like, what's going on? I don't even have things on me. And they got me for vandalism. He was like, "You can't be crossing right here." I'm like, “Dude, it's city property. I can be walking wherever I want, so I'm not trespassing on some people's property, I'm just walking in the railroad tracks,” and he just got me for that.

Sergio:        Aside from that, there's nothing else? When you were taken to ICE, how long were you in detention for?

Vic:        Year and a half.

Sergio:        A year and a half?

Vic:        A year and months, yeah. A year and months or a year and three months or a year and a half.

Sergio:        How was the facility?

Vic:        The facility was cool, the food was bad. They even had a protest in there about the food. But besides everything else, I mean, they give you sweaters, they give you the clothes that you need, the food was the only thing that was bad, but you got big screen TV's to watch all day. You're not locked up all day, you're actually outside working out or doing whatever you want. You're just locked up, you're being detained. You're in detention, but other than that...

Sergio:        Were you treated well?

Vic:        Yeah. There wasn't that much discrimination. The people were nice. The workers, they were working through college. It was people in college, they were young so they understood us. They really didn't mind us that much.

Sergio:        So there was other people your age?

Vic:        Well, there was people my age, there was people that were 18, there was people that were 40, but I mean I'm talking about the workers.

Sergio:        The facility?

Vic:        Yeah.

Sergio:        They were college… ?

Vic:        Yeah, they were college graduates or getting their degrees and stuff. So that's what actually surprised me. There weren't actual police people there.

Sergio:        So you were there a year and a half to fight your case?

Vic:        Yeah.

Sergio:        And then what ended up happening for your eventual deportation?

Vic:        Well, that morning they woke me up, at night, I was going to make a phone call to see that my mom had the lawyer stuff all worked out and stuff, and my call wasn't going through. And this was a Sunday night. The only time that your call doesn't go through on a Sunday night is because people are getting deported the next day and they're taking people out of the system. And I actually went to go check out if my status was still in the system and it wasn't.

Sergio:        Your A number?

Vic:        Yeah, exactly.

Sergio:        What's your A number?

Vic:        I don't remember that.

Sergio:        Don't remember?

Vic:        ____. Something like that. But no, it's ____

Sergio:        Do you remember the UIR number?

Vic:        No.

Sergio:        The one that you had, the 1-800?

Vic:        No, that one I don't remember, but yeah, I remember my A number. But yeah, I went to check my commissary money and stuff and when I brought that up, it wasn't allowing me, so that's the only reason I knew I was getting deported the next morning. And I actually had a bond case the next morning and I had the following day, my actual hearing for my case. And they woke me up at like six in the morning telling me to wrap it up, get your stuff and we're going.

Sergio:        What year was it?

Vic:        2017. 2018.

Sergio:        Do you think it had anything with Trump?

Vic:        Hm?

Sergio:        Do you think that had anything to do with Trump?

Vic:        Well, I mean, it has to do with all government. They're all into this. They're trying to benefit deporting people to get more money into the States. You have immigrant people making people's money in the US that I don't think they like that at all. I understand, but still, we're there just to work. We're not there to cause trouble. I understand we all come from different parts of the world, we all come from different groups, different status. Like poor, rich, wealthy, whatever. But it doesn't mean we all got to get that same image of us, that we're drug dealers, that we're gangsters and stuff. I really don't appreciate that, but I understand he's trying to protect his country.

Sergio:        They deported you before your bond hearing.

Vic:        Yeah.

Sergio:        Before you even saw the immigration officer?

Vic:        Exactly.

Sergio:        So the judge didn't say, "You are getting deported."

Vic:        Yeah. If I'm not mistaken, I wasn't even signed by the deportation judge. I was thrown out by my ICE officer.

Sergio:        Did they say anything when they threw you out?

Vic:        No. I told him, "Hey, I want a phone call." He's like, "You'll get a phone in TJ." I told him, "I'm not going to sign anything." "You got to sign stuff." I've heard them hurting people if they don't want to sign. They'll put your hand in the back and twist it until you might even break and stuff, but I was like, why even go through all this hassle? They're deporting me illegally, so might as well just see what comes from it.

Sergio:        So when you arrived in TJ...

Vic:        I got to the Mexican Consul, they gave me paperwork so I could have, what is it? Medical records, be able to go to the doctor's for the next two years. So they gave me type of insurance for myself, medical forms and stuff. That was pretty cool. They gave me pretty much an ID and stuff, but still, in Mexico, they don't respect that. They told me that some guy tried to give him a Mexican ID that they gave him at the Mexican Consul, and they still took everything he had, even his shoes. This happened by a policeman.

Sergio:        Mexican police?

Vic:        Yeah, the Mexican police, they said, "No, this is invalid. I need actual paperwork." And he's like, "I don't have anything, what do you want?" He took his phone, he took his money and his shoes. So that's why I was like, damn. But it was pretty cool, because they told me, "Hey, if you don't have nowhere to stay, there's actually..." I forgot what it was called, but it was a little home shelter, a little shelter for deported people. And they'll give you jobs and stuff, to live for at least, I believe, two weeks or two months or something like that. But my mom's neighbor actually came, picked me up and took me to the hotel that we actually booked for a week.

Sergio:        Sure. Was your transition back to Mexico society hard?

Vic:        Yeah, it was. It was different. Like I mentioned, that's the only thing that I have, the English, but other than that, I'm pretty sure it would have been really difficult. It would have been hard to actually go through life without the English that I carry.

Sergio:        Was it hard to be away from your sister and your family?

Vic:        Yeah, it was difficult.

Sergio:        Were you the main provider for your sister and your mom when you left?

Vic:        Well, no. My mom was working and stuff, but once I started working on my own, I started... I had a girlfriend, I moved out and stuff. It wasn't until the last two months that my mom actually took me back into her home. That's why at the end, we weren't even that close, but my mom always checked up on me and made sure I had food, I had somewhere to stay and all that, but that's why I wasn't really that much of a big factor in their life after the last year that I was there.

Sergio:        So when you came back, were you still with that girlfriend from up there? No.

Vic:        No, I wasn't.

Sergio:        That was hard.

Vic:        Yeah.

Sergio:        [inaudible 00:26:29]

Vic:        Exactly. I was going court cases, and then she had her own stuff. School, work. That's why I was just like, we both decided not to stay together.

Sergio:        So when you came back here, you were able to get a job pretty easily.

Vic:        Yeah.

Sergio:        Where at?

Vic:        Well, I got a job as a waiter right there where I lived, at the little plaza. But once my friend told me, because he was deported too, we stayed in the same place, he was in  Guanajuato. He told me, "Hey, this place called TTECH right there by where you stay, they're actually taking people. You want to work for them? We get referral money and stuff." I was like, "Sure." It's a decent job. I don't see myself answering phones all my life, but that's why I'm trying to find option B and stuff.

Sergio:        Do you want to go back to school?

Vic:        Yeah, I want to study. Yeah, I really want to study to either be a teacher or be an architect. In architecture, engineering. I got English, so if I get certified in anything, I think that's a big plus.

Sergio:        Is your high school diploma going to be helpful?

Vic:        Yeah. They asked me for it here, but their expectations in studies, because our English studies and Spanish studies is two different things. I don't think they're going to meet at the end of the day. I think I still have to go to some type of high school here or something.

Sergio:        Yeah. Did you experience any discrimination as a migrant?

Vic:        No. Not really.

Sergio:        Have you been approached or recruited by groups of bad people in Mexico?

Vic:        No. I tend to stay away from people here. I only stay with my girlfriend and my baby, that's it. And my family.

Sergio:        What do you miss about the US?

Vic:        Honestly, honestly, the beach. I miss the beach, you know, being able to wake up every morning and just do whatever I want, make myself a milkshake, open my fridge and be able to grab whatever I want to eat. It's really hard to live out here. I mean you're living day by day here. Over there, you're buying groceries for two whole weeks and they're going to sit there and they're going to last you two weeks. Here, you got to go day by day. You buy a week's worth of groceries, you're wasting 1,000, 2,000 pesos and that's like half your paycheck right here.

Vic:        So that's what I miss more. Being able to eat whatever I want, being able to travel whenever I wanted to. Not travel out of the state, you know, but travel to San Francisco or to the beach or to the mountains. Los Angeles has everything. That was the best part about Los Angeles. That's why I love it, but other than that, it's all the same here. You wake up to work, you go home to sleep and eat and that's it. You do it all over, it's the same thing with the States but it feels like you got more freedom, more availability to do whatever you want, whenever you want.

Sergio:        So do you feel more like a US citizen or like a Mexican citizen?

Vic:        Well I mean, honestly, not really, well I mean... yeah, I grew up there, but I have to recognize where I came from. I wasn't born there and I did know that since I was little, so that's why I never felt myself actually like a US citizen. That's why I don't really consider myself a US citizen.

Sergio:        Do you feel more comfortable here than you did over there?

Vic:        Not really. I mean it’s a new lifestyle. I got to go in the metro to do this and that, when I could have just got an Uber or taxi. I could still do it here, but over there, I had the money, I had flexibility to do things that I can't do here.

Sergio:        Would you recommend that people migrate to the US?
