Uriel

Interviewee

Anne Preston

Interviewer

June 5, 2019

Mexico City, Mexico

Anne:        Okay, so this is Anne and I am interviewing Uriel. And do you go by Uri or Uriel?

Uriel:        Uriel.

Anne:        Uriel, okay, great. So first, I want to thank you for coming to talk to me.

Uriel:        You're welcome.

Anne:        So, it's tough. You're in a tough situation. And you're going to be recounting a story that's probably painful. And I just want to thank you for coming to talk me.

Uriel:        You're welcome.

Anne:        And I wasn't at your survey, so I don't know much about you. So maybe we could start by, you can tell me the circumstances behind you coming to the United States, how old you were, why you came, your first impressions.

Uriel:        Yeah. Well, I was two years old when my parents brought me to the States and well, I really was a baby, I didn't really know much about it. But the reason we went over there… money. You know, we needed money, so we went over there to the States.

Anne:        And was there any violence here in Mexico that might've...

Uriel:        I mean, like I said, I was two years old, so...

Anne:        You don't know.

Uriel:        Yeah, I don't know, ma'am.

Anne:        Yeah. So it was your mom and your dad and you.

Uriel:        Yeah, and my siblings too. My brothers. We're four in total.

Anne:        Okay so, there are four of you?

Uriel:        Yeah, we are four siblings.

Anne:        Boys?

Uriel:        Three boys, one female.

Anne:        I see. What were you the?

Uriel:        I'm the second oldest.

Anne:        Second oldest. So presumably some of them were born in the States?

Uriel:        No, but they're actually working on their permit and so they're...

Anne:        So even though you were two years old, there were two younger than you?

Uriel:        Yeah, one was months, and the other one was one... We were only a couple months off.

Anne:        I see. Interesting. And so where did your parents take you?

Uriel:        To Los Angeles.

Anne:        To Los Angeles. So presumably you didn't know any English?

Uriel:        No, I didn't.

Anne:        And your parents located in Los Angeles? Did they work?

Uriel:        Yeah. My mom actually had two jobs and had to work hard to provide us with food and the things we needed.

Anne:        Yeah. And what kind of jobs? Was it… ?

Uriel:        Cleaning houses and stuff.

Anne:        And how about your dad?

Uriel:        My dad, construction.

Anne:        Construction. So they worked hard.

Uriel:        Well, yeah.

Anne:        So then you started school in the States?

Uriel:        Mm-hmm (affirmative). I started from kindergarten all the way to high school. I got my high school diploma.

Anne:        Great. So you learned English there, or did you know English before you went to kindergarten?

Uriel:        No, I learned it in school and like in the streets.

Anne:        Uh-huh (affirmative). So your family, what was family life like?

Uriel:        It was hard, but your family is your family at the end of the day. So you got to deal with it.

Anne:        Yeah. Yeah. So it was hard because they were working...

Uriel:        Yeah. I didn't really get to see my parents during the day. You know? It was hard.

Anne:        Did you have the older siblings that took care of you, or?

Uriel:        Well… it was something like that.

Anne:        Babysitters?

Uriel:        Yeah. Yeah, yeah. And you learned English at school?

Uriel:        At school.

Anne:         Did you like school?

Uriel:        I didn't, but I had to go, you know, because I thought about my future and how it was going to help me out.

Anne:        How long did it take you to learn English?

Uriel:        I think a while.

Anne:        Did you make lots of friends?

Uriel:        Yeah, I actually did. I still got some friends calling me, like checking up on me from the States. Like, "How are you doing?" you know?

Anne:        What did you like best about growing up in the States?

Uriel:        Everything, everything, right. Everything. The life, everything. Everything is just way better over there.

Anne:        Did you grow up in a neighborhood with mostly Mexicans, or was it more diverse?

Uriel:        It was half blacks, half Mexicans.

Anne:        So friends from everywhere?

Uriel:        Mm-hmm (affirmative).

Anne:        And your school? Again, lots of different kinds of…

Uriel:        Yeah, different races, different kids.

Anne:        In school, what did you do best in?

Uriel:        Science and social studies.

Anne:        Yeah, did you ever think about going beyond high school?

Uriel:        No. Well, right now I'm in a situation where I need to work to provide for myself. So I don't got time for college or nothing like that.

Anne:        How old are you?

Uriel:        I'm 28 years old, ma'am.

Anne:        28.

Uriel:        Yes.

Anne:        So your siblings are still there?

Uriel:        Yeah. All my family are still there.

Anne:        Okay. So as you got older and graduated from high school, you went and you got a job. Is that what you did?

Uriel:        Yeah I had a job for a little while.

Anne:        And did you get in trouble?

Uriel:        Yeah, I actually did. I did seven years in prison, ma'am.

Anne:        Oh no. So how old were you?

Uriel:        I was 21 when I got arrested.

Anne:        Had you been getting in trouble earlier? Like when you were in your teens?

Uriel:        Yeah I was.

Anne:        You were… Gangs?

Uriel:        Yeah. I'm an ex-gang member. You know?

Anne:        So why do you think kids like you end up in gangs?

Uriel:        I think because of the way that they live, you know, and the things that happened to them. You know? It's hard, sometimes.

Anne:        Hard to avoid?

Uriel:        Yeah. Not only that, but just the experience you know that happens. Like for instance, me and my brothers, like our parents were never there, so we were running the streets.

Anne:        Do your parents know?

Uriel:        Yeah. My parents know.

Anne:        They knew that you were out in the streets?

Uriel:        No, they didn't at the time, but...

Anne:        Eventually.

Uriel:        Yeah.

Anne:        Do you have a girlfriend?

Uriel:        I actually have a daughter. She's in the States too. The mother of my daughter, she's over there too.

Anne:        So you graduated from high school, you said you got a job. What kind of job?

Uriel:        Well, I was working in an authorizing shipping center, UPS and FedEx.

Anne:        Oh, okay. Was that good?

Uriel:        Yeah, it was good.

Anne:        Good work?

Uriel:        Yeah. But it really didn't last long. I got fired.

Anne:        Why'd you get fired?

Uriel:        For stealing.

Anne:        Oh, okay. And you… you were guilty? You did it?

Uriel:        Yeah. Well, I'm not going to lie, you know.

Anne:        So, at that point, did you have your daughter?

Uriel:        My daughter's eight years old, so right before I went to prison I had her.

Anne:        Yeah. So did you feel like you just needed more money to live, to succeed? Were you on drugs?

Uriel:        Yeah I was. I was.

Anne:        Okay. And so a lot of it was to...

Uriel:        Yeah, it was just the way was… you know, the way I lived when I was young and then this escalated to a higher level and then I ended up in Mexico.

Anne:        Did your siblings do similar things?

Uriel:        No, I'm actually the black sheep of my family.

Anne:        I'm sorry.

Uriel:        I'm the only...

Anne:        Why do you think that is?

Uriel:        I have no idea. My brothers are doing good. They're married with kids. They're doing good.

Anne:        So what happened? What was the crime?

Uriel:        I got charged for two firearms and possession of narcotics.

Anne:        So you were just caught in it.

Uriel:        I was driving and they stopped me and they found the guns and the drugs.

Anne:        Were you going anywhere in particular, or you always had guns?

Uriel:        Well, I used to sell drugs over there.

Anne:        So once you were caught, did you go to court?

Uriel:        Yeah, I got, went to court. I got sentenced. They gave me seven years.

Anne:        So is that a lot for the crime, do you think?

Uriel:        I think it is a long time. It means seven years of my life… gone. In the trash.

Anne:        Yeah. And so what kind of prison did you go to?

Uriel:        Federal prison.

Anne:        It's tough.

Uriel:        Yeah. It's tough.

Anne:        Tell me about it. Was it hard to survive it?

Uriel:        Yeah, it is. It is. It's hard, but, you just got to mind your own business and stick to yourself and you'll be all right.

Anne:        So you did? You stuck to your own business and kept clean and?

Uriel:        Yeah. That's how you survive.

Anne:        So when you were in prison, what were you thinking, in terms of how your future would unfold?

Uriel:        I actually... Well, I was just wondering what they were going to do with me. And try to better myself, you know like thinking about the future and try to change my bad ways… to be a better person.

Anne:        Did you do any education in prison at all?

Uriel:        I actually do, I have like 20 certificates that I took. Classes that they had provided there.

Anne:        What kind of classes?

Uriel:        Like, exercise classes. What's it called? Computer classes, soldering classes. They had plenty of stuff. Automotive mechanics class.

Anne:        That's good.

Uriel:        Yeah.

Anne:        So you had... Did you know that they were going to take you and deport you as soon as you got out?

Uriel:        Well, I had a detainer. I had an ICE hold. So I knew something was up.[a]

Anne:        So you sort of wonder if they're going to deport you, why keep you in jail for seven years? Did you think about that? If you're going to deport me, why keep me in jail for seven years?

Uriel:        Yeah, I did. But they just did. They gave me seven years.

Anne:        So once the seven years were done, you...

Uriel:        They brought me straight to Mexico. Right after my time.

Anne:        You didn’t see a judge?

Uriel:        I never seen a federal judge or an immigration judge. Never.

Anne:        Was that the first time that ICE had detained you? When you were caught for...

Uriel:        Actually, they detained me twice, but like I said, I never seen a federal judge, like an ICE judge, immigration judge, no.

Anne:        And the first time they detained you, was it for another criminal stuff?

Uriel:        Yeah. But a minor one.

Anne:        Minor. And then they let you go.

Uriel:        Um, no.

Anne:        Then what happened?

Uriel:        They tried to deport me and I post bond and...

Anne:        Oh you posted bond?

Uriel:        Yeah. And then I got out and then the situation happened with me with the guns and drugs and...

Anne:        Oh, so you were in a bond when that happened?

Uriel:        Yeah. Mm-hmm (affirmative).

Anne:        Wow. Um, so how much did you have to pay for the bond?

Uriel:        Uh, $5,000.

Anne:        That's okay. So you got out and the idea was that you were going to try and fight the?

Uriel:        Yeah, fight it, yeah. Because like I said, I was basically born over there, since I was two years old. Yeah. So I tried to fight it, but then I got in trouble and this happened.

Anne:        Yeah. You were an American. That's what you thought about.

Uriel:        Yeah, like if my parents wouldn't have told me that I was born here, like I would have thought myself that I was born in the States.

Anne:        When did you figure it out? That you were not?

Uriel:        Well, when my mom, my mother told me one time to behave, because I wasn't born there. She said behave or you know, you can't be doing stuff that you're not supposed to. She told me that.

Anne:        How old were you?

Uriel:        It was like 16, 17.

Anne:        But you were already getting in trouble?

Uriel:        Yeah.

Anne:        It's tough once you get...

Uriel:        Yeah, it is.

Anne:        Did you feel like the gangs were sort of more your family than your family?

Uriel:        No. I never thought like that, or never will. You know, my family comes first all the time.

Anne:        That's great. So are you still in contact with your daughter?

Uriel:        Yeah. I talk to her actually every other day, when I have a chance.

Anne:        And she's eight.

Uriel:        She's eight.

Anne:        And how about her mother?

Uriel:        We’re actually… we’re not together no more, but yeah. I talk to her because I have to, in order to talk to my daughter.

Anne:        Did your daughter come visit you in jail?

Uriel:        Actually, she did. I got a couple pictures of her. Me and her.

Anne:        How's she doing?

Uriel:        She's doing good.

Anne:        Yeah. Third grade?

Uriel:        Third grade.

Anne:        She likes school?

Uriel:        She does. I tell her… tell her to keep doing good, you know?

Anne:        Does she see your mom at all? Your mom, her grandma. Her grandmother.

Uriel:        Yeah, she sees her.

Anne:        And the rest of the family is still there.

Uriel:        Yeah. Everybody's over there.

Anne:        That's great. So you immediately...

Uriel:        Yeah, right after I finished my time there, they brought me here to Mexico.

Anne:        And that was how long ago?

Uriel:        I've been here since January. I just got here.

Anne:        Oh you just got here?

Uriel:        Yeah. I'm barely getting to know Mexico and...

Anne:        What do you think?

Uriel:        Sucks. Excuse my language, ma'am.

Anne:        It’s okay. Tell me why it sucks?

Uriel:        Just everything. Pshh, [scoffing] The money, the way that people are here, the way the law is here, everything, everything. I don't like it.

Anne:        Who are you living with?

Uriel:        By myself.

Anne:        Do you have a place?

Uriel:        Yeah. I bought a property here.

Anne:        Wow. So, where?

Uriel:        Chalco.

Anne:        How far away is that?

Uriel:        Two hours from here.

Anne:                And you have a job there?

Uriel:        Actually, I just started a job right here in TeleTech.

Anne:        Right.

Uriel:        I've started working.

Anne:        So what are you going to do with the property in two hours away from here?

Uriel:        Well, I have to deal with it. I want to keep the job I had to may be here in time and all that.

Anne:        There are no jobs there where you bought the property?

Uriel:        Well they wouldn't take me. I got too many tattoos.

Anne:        Because it's more rural.

Uriel:        I don't have no idea, but they won't hire me.

Anne:        Discrimination.

Uriel:        Yeah. Yeah.

Anne:                So did you buy the place there because your relatives were from there?

Uriel:        Yeah.

Anne:        Are your relatives still there?

Uriel:        Well, one of my aunts lives there.

Anne:        A weekend place?

Uriel:        Huh?

Anne:        A weekend place? [chuckle]

Uriel:        No.

Anne:        So you're just trying to figure out how to live here.

Uriel:        Yeah, and I'm trying to see how things are and hopefully, I won't have to be here long. But if I do, just how to survive, basically. You know? How things work. Like I said, I got like four months here and everything's new to me right now.

Anne:        And you're now at TeleTech?

Uriel:        Yeah. I just started.

Anne:        What do you think about that?

Uriel:        I think it's perfect. They hired me with all my tattoos and I talk English, which is right. I think it's good.

Anne:        You made any friends?

Uriel:        Actually female friends, yeah.

Anne:        Are they at TeleTech too?

Uriel:        Yeah, they're right there too.

Anne:        So that's good.

Uriel:        Yeah.

Anne:        When you say, I hope I'm not going to be here too long, what are your plans?

Uriel:        So, I don't know, man. Like hopefully some laws come in or something that can help me out.

Anne:        Would you go back illegally?

Uriel:        No. They told me if I go back, they would give me 15 years. I don't want to do 15 years.

Anne:        Yeah. It's a lot. It’s a lot. So, you're American. If I said to you, are you American or Mexican, what would you say?

Uriel:        American, of course. I would tell you I lived in LA all my life.

Anne:        4th of July comes along, what will you do?

Uriel:        Drink.

Anne:        [Laughs] Will you celebrate?

Uriel:        Yeah. I'll give myself some couple beers and enjoy the day.

Anne:        What are your dreams now?

Uriel:        Actually I have no dreams. I'm just trying to work and survive, like I said. Survive.

Anne:        Maybe when you get some money, you'll have some dreams.

Uriel:        Yeah. Buy a nice car, buy a nice house, stuff like that, yeah.

Anne:        See your daughter again?

Uriel:        My daughter actually, she's going to come in a couple months. So I can't wait for that.

Anne:        Is the mom going to…

Uriel:        She's going to come too, yeah.

Anne:        Very good.

Uriel:        Unfortunately I don't want her to come, but she's going to come too.

Anne:        She's a US citizen, so she can come and go.

Uriel:        Yeah.

Anne:        And your daughter's a US citizen, which is great for her.

Uriel:        Exactly.

Anne:        She can come and go.

Uriel:        Mm-hmm (affirmative).

Anne:        But you've never really lived with her?

Uriel:        With my daughter?

Anne:        Yeah.

Uriel:        No, last time I seen her, she was months. She was a little girl. She was a little baby. She grew up on me.

Anne:        Is she a beautiful girl?

Uriel:        Yeah, she's beautiful.

Anne:        So tell me about being here. Is it hard to escape the criminal activity here?

Uriel:        Mmmmm… I mean, like I said, I'm going to have a different mentality. If it don't happen to me, I don't care. Like, just mind your own business and things. It's how it usually works in the streets.

Anne:        So, trying to be a new person?

Uriel:        Actually, I am, I'm trying to leave all them bad ways behind and try to better myself and work and, you know, stay busy.

Anne:        You said you're still in contact with your mother and your...

Uriel:        Yeah, everybody.

Anne:        And your siblings are trying to get their...

Uriel:        They're actually working on it right now. They're going to court and paying the payments.

Anne:        And your mom?

Uriel:        My mom too.

Anne:        So when they get that, they can come see you.

Uriel:        Yeah. Correct.

Anne:        It's just, you can't go there, right now.

Uriel:        No. I can't.

Anne:        It's tough.

Uriel:        Yeah, it is.

Anne:        Um, if you had something you wanted to share with people who are listening to your experiences, people like your experiences, something that you haven't said so far, what would you want people to hear?

Uriel:        I mean just, stay out of trouble and try to stay in the States, ‘cause Mexico is nothing nice.

Anne:        You think that would stop little kids from joining gangs?

Uriel:        Yeah, ‘cause obviously they have no idea, you know, what their future is when they do happen to join one. But it sucks over here. Sucks.

Anne:        If I see you in a year, and I talk to you again. I hope you don't say it still sucks quite this much.

Uriel:        I mean, I can't get used to a place like this. I just can't. I can’t. I mean I’m going to have to you know? I'm going to have to make the best of it, because I have no choice, obviously, you know, but yeah, I can't.

Anne:        So I encourage you to talk to the people at New Comienzos. They're trying to create a community of returning migrants so that you have social, you know…

Uriel:        Yeah. I'll look into it.

Anne:        Friends. If the job doesn't work out, they can find you other jobs, but just finding people like yourself might help.

Uriel:        Yeah.

Anne:        But you said you've met some nice women.

Uriel:        Yeah. They actually come from the States too. Everybody that works there comes from over there too.

Anne:        So that's sort of helpful?

Uriel:        Yeah, it makes me feel at home, you know? Like I'm back in the city.

Anne:        Yeah. So you lived in LA, so you lived in the city. You're still in a city.

Uriel:        Yeah, but it's not LA.

Anne:        They call this area Little LA.

Uriel:        Psh [scoffs], it’s nothing like it.

Anne:        A lot of traffic.

Uriel:        Yeah.

Anne:        [Laughs] But nothing else.

Uriel:        Nothing else.

Anne:        Okay. Well, thank you so much for talking to me.

Uriel:        You're welcome, ma'am.

[a]Spent a good portion of his life wondering what effect his status would have after prison.
