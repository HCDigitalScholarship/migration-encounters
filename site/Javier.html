Mexico City, Mexico

Anne:         This is Anne and I am interviewing Javier. I didn't sit through your survey, so I don't know much about you, but can you tell me what the circumstances were for you to migrate to the US?

Javier:         For sure, absolutely. Well, it was because I was really poor when I was a young kid, and my father starts to work as a teacher, and then he had to get another job as a police officer. It was kind of difficult for me to get concentration for my work, school and everything like that. When I was 17, I decided to move because there was not enough money. So I decided to move. I gave it a try at first. I moved to Baja, California and then one of my friends in there told me we can try to cross the border. There's some people you paid and they put you inside a truck and there you go.

Anne:         And you hope for the best.

Javier:         Yeah, that's correct.

Anne:         It was really because you felt that you didn't have enough money? You thought you couldn't concentrate on your studies?

Javier:        It was because we didn't have enough money. And by dad, mom, and my grandparents were very, very sick, I mean, in an ill way. One of them had cancer and my grandmother had some issues on her throat. She wasn't able to speak really good. My brothers began to work too. One of them became a police officer, too. One of them become a military man. I'm the youngest one from all of them, so I decided to move. It was a chance presented to me by some friends. Like I mentioned before, we moved first to Baja, California, and from there we crossed.

Anne:         How much school had you finished before you went?

Javier:         In that moment, I finished high school. And then the process that I was... and the time that I spent in the US was three years, but the first two of them, I was in Los Angeles. Then I came back and I began my university studies. But then something happened that my brother became sick. He got cancer, so I had to move again. I spent another year over there and one of those days my mom called me, "You know what? Your brother is really sick. We need you to come back." Two months after I came back, he died for the cancer. Since that moment I've been living here. I've been thinking to maybe move, but not right now.

Anne:         In total, how many years were you in the US?

Javier:         Three years.

Anne:         Three years. And were you working or going to school while you were there?

Javier:         No working, working, working. Yeah. The first time I arrived there, I work in a kitchen. Here on the high school, we got some... how to say it?... like side activities. And they show us how to cook, some procedures for cooking and things. And when I arrived there, I began working in a kitchen. Then the money was not enough, and one of my cousins that live in Delaware told me, "You know, I know a guy over there that has some work on construction. You want to give it a shot?" And I said, "Yes, let's take it."

Anne:         So you went from California to…?

Javier:         No. I stayed there in Los Angeles, but in construction.

Anne:         Oh, but your cousin knew someone in LA?

Javier:         Yeah, that's correct.

Anne:         I see. I see. You did construction and then you went back?

Javier:         That's correct. Yes.

Anne:         … to Mexico? And then you came back to the US?

Javier:         That's correct. Crossing the border again.

Anne:         How many times did you…?

Javier:         Two times.[a]

Anne:         And the second time did you do construction as well?

Javier:         Yes. Construction and then in kitchen again. The very last job that I had there was in kitchen. The last six months that I spent there, I hadn't a job in there. I was living with some of my family members in Pomona, and they were paying me to do all the home job, like washing the dishes, cleaning all the house. They didn't pay me a lot, but they allowed me to live in there and to have some money to send to my mom.

Anne:         So when you went to the US it was really to make money for the people back in Mexico? That's why you went?

Javier:         That's correct.

Anne:         And that was before your brother got sick?

Javier:         That is correct.

Anne:         But your grandparents were sick, so there were medical bills.

Javier:         That is correct. Medical bills and then, like I mentioned, the last time that I came back, it was because of my brother that died two months after I came here. The mainly reasons that I went there was to make money.

Anne:         And how long have you been back?

Javier:         Hm?

Anne:         How long have you been back?

Javier:         In here? It has been six years.

Anne:         Six years.

Javier:         Yeah, that's correct.

Anne:         Well, I'm very sorry about your brother.

Javier:         Do not worry. Also, some kind of bad stuff happened to me once that I came back here. The reason why I came back here, they never deported me. Fortunately, they never deported me, but I was catched by the police because I was dreaming too much to become a musician over there. I'm always been passionate for the music. So I went to a concert and somebody call and they say, "We got some wetbacks here." Yeah. So they catch me...

Anne:         There was a raid?

Javier:         Yeah.

Anne:         A raid at the music festival?

Javier:         Yeah, yeah. And somebody called that there was some wetbacks. They catch us up and they hit me once. They told me, "You know what? We will not deport you." They only throw me on to the streets. The police officers that catch me were Mexican, so it was kind of funny. They tell me, "We will not deport you, but you make your choice." And I was like, "What I'm going to do?" As soon as my mom called me and told me that my brother was sick, I said, "You know what? Maybe it's not the time for me to be here. I have to leave."[b] So I went to the border in California and I crossed. The only thing that the police officer told me to me, "Please do not come back unless you have your papers." That's the only thing they told me.

Javier:         Fortunately, when I arrived to Baja, California, because I crossed for el puente... that's what they call the bridge. There was a program called Welcome... how to say it... It is called Welcome Paisano. And they arranged some things for them to translate me from to here to Toluca, because I'm from Toluca. They moved me from Baja, California to Toluca. They paid everything.

Anne:         The government?

Javier:         Yeah. Yeah, the government. Yes. Yeah. Yeah. Yeah. They paid everything to bring me here. And then I began working again in a kitchen, and then I moved here to the Mexico City. And some of my friends that were deported, they told me, "You know what? We know a company which is TTEC here that is hiring people like us." Okay. Let's give it a shot.

Anne:         How long have you worked for them?

Javier:         A year. A year and three months. Unfortunately, the first month, was when my son born. After that month, he died because he was premature…

Anne:         I’m so sorry.

Javier:         … and he died, so I been in a really bad situation.

Anne:         You've had a hard life. I’m so sorry.

Javier:         Yes. We've been trying to give our best.

Anne:         Are you still with the baby's mother?

Javier:         Absolutely yes. She was my friend since I was 13 years old.

Anne:         Oh wow.

Javier:         I met her in a concert in Queretaro. I met her in there. I was playing in a band where I was a really, really, really young musician in that time. I met her and we keep chatting. Once that I came back three years ago, we start dating and then the last year we decided to have... Not the last year, two years ago, we decided to move together and we had a baby. Like I mentioned, unfortunately he died, but I'm still with her and she is the love of my life. And I been away from every single... I never touch a drug or alcohol, nothing like that.

Anne:         Are you still practicing your music?

Javier:         Yes, sometimes. Yes. I play the drums and also I play the bass guitar.

Anne:         Were you in a band?

Javier:         Yes. Absolutely, yeah. I work in... as you see my image is from a, how do you say it?... rocker or something like that, but I also play salsa with a group and I have my metal band, another rock and metal band.

Anne:         A metal band?

Javier:        Yes.

Anne:         And how often do you get gigs?

Javier:         Almost every 15 days. This Friday we're going to have one. Last Friday we didn't have it, but 15 days before, yes, we have it.

Anne:         Yeah. Do you get paid?

Javier:         Yes.

Anne:         That's great.

Javier:         It's not too much, but it is really good because I'm doing what I love.

Anne:         What you love. Yeah. You weren't in the US a whole lot, but you speak great English. Did you know English before you went for the first time?

Javier:         Just a little bit. Yeah, just a little bit, because since I was four, something like that, I was very interested on English music because of my father. When he was young, many, many years ago, he used to play in a band that actually, before he left them... sorry, after he left them, like three years he left them, they became famous.

Anne:         Oh. What’s their name?

Javier:         They are called, Peace and Love Super Banda. They were from California, from Baja, California. They played in our Mexican Woodstock called Avándaro, so they became famous in there, but my father didn't make it. He was on the road and the boss, he was coming just... How to say it? It burned, the motor burned and he didn't make it.

Anne:         He died?

Javier:         No, no, no, no, no, no, no, no. Fortunately, no, no, no, no. Just the bus. He didn't make it there. The band says, "You know what? We don't want you any longer in here." Three years after that they became really famous. Well, at least here in Mexico.

Anne:         So is that… listening to English music? Is that how you learned English?

Javier:         Yes. Actually, listening to English music. I was really passionate with Pink Floyd, Rush and many bands like used the English in a proper way.

Anne:         Yeah.

Javier:         Because there was some bands that I didn't like the way that they used the language, using bad words and something like that. And many of the United Kingdom bands, they were like more proper in the ways to use the language. So I was like, "Oh, I like it." So I guess I learned listening to music. By that moment my father bought me a Larousse, a dictionary, to check the words, so I was listening and I write as I understand it, and then check the book. Oh, it is written like this.

Anne:         That's great. That's great. Wow. And so, by the time you got to the US you were fair, at least, at speaking?

Javier:         Yes.

Anne:         Yeah.

Javier:         Yeah. Yeah. I was able to communicate with the other people. I tried to imitate the accent, because on the first moment, it was really hard for me to get understood by the people because I had my Mexican accent, and I still have it.

Anne:         You speak very well.

Javier:         Thank you so much.

Anne:         Hardly an accent.

Javier:         Thank you so much. I tried to imitate and do some play roles, because yeah, it was really hard. I remember the first time I arrived to LA, I went to a Dunkin' Donuts and it was kind of racist because the guy didn't understand me.[c] And he took up a little menu and told me, "Just pick the number." And I was like, "Okay, this one." Now, okay, okay. I been practicing. In that moment I was practicing with some friends, and that's how I learned it. Then when I came back, I studied with a program called SEPA Inglés, Learn English, approved by the SEP, which is... Well, it's a government organization that rules all over the education here in Mexico. It is called Public Education Secretariat. Yes. Yes. That's what SEP is. I studied there when I came back and I was only one year minor.

Anne:         Hmm. So did you like the US?

Javier:         Yes. I like it a lot.

Anne:         What did you like about it?

Javier:         What I liked the most, it was the... how to say it? It kind of sounds weird, but I liked the people in there. You know? There was some people that didn't like me, that hit me like I mentioned, that'd be racist with me, but there was a lot of people, very, very, very nice. I love the way that they treat me, the way that they told me, "There is my hand. If you want to, hold it and we're going to do this together." That's something that at least, at least here at Mexico for the last year has been lost because of the violence, because of the cartels, because of the drug sellers, for the kidnappers. That's something that has been lost. And in the US, still the people is in that way.

Javier:         Also, I love the fact that I was earning more money than in here, because in here, when I worked in the kitchen, I was paid... for the week, I was paid not much, like $80, something closer, when the dollar in that time was like 12 pesos. And we really, really, really needed the money. And then actually another thing that I like a lot is the culture in general. That has the most high to me. I tried to apply for a citizen, but I didn't make it because I have the record when they catch me on the concert.[d]

Anne:         Because of the concert?

Javier:         Yes, they told me that not that I was not... how they say it? They say like suitable to be a citizen.

Anne:         But all you had done is just you were here without papers?

Javier:         Yeah.

Anne:         That's it?

Javier:         Yeah. They make a record of it, unfortunately. Sorry. No, no, no. Fortunately, when I came back, that record was erased.

Anne:         Oh good.

Javier:         … because I don't know how they call it, but it's like a special program that is active for three months. And in that moment, if I wanted to apply, I didn't make it, and that's what happened. They told me, "You're not suitable for this, so just erase that idea from your mind." I was like, "Okay."

Anne:         But now that's been expunged, so you can apply for a visa to go back?

Javier:         Yes, yes I can apply. Yes.

Anne:         So will you go back?

Javier:         I want to, I want to, because on the first moment when my son was alive, I thought, "I'm going to bring the best for him." I talked to my wife and we decided to move to the US. Like I mentioned, then they told me about this and I say, "Okay, I'll just give it a shot in here." Yes, I really want to move or stay here and help people. Something that I like a lot is to show people how to do the things. My nephews, my cousins, also my aunts, whenever I come to my house, because my house is from here, like to one you have to travel like three hours. So whenever I go to visit them, I do some English lessons for them. I understand that maybe I'm not very good at this, but I try to give them an idea. So they know the numbers, the ABC, the colors. Whenever I have the chance, I buy movies for them, only in English, Mickey Mouse and everything like that.

Anne:         Great.

Javier:         I do my effort to improve the things. By now, fortunately my father is... how to say it... retired. By now they are living good. All the bad stuff that happened to us, like we say here, it happens for something. By now they are good. My two remaining brothers, they have a good life.

Anne:         Here?

Javier:         Yes, yes, yes, yes. And we have to do the best. We try to do the best in here as much as we can.

Anne:         So what are your dreams?

Javier:         My dreams for always is to be a musician, maybe not famous, but to be a musician. That's what I love the most. And maybe, because I was also involved in gangs, to show the kids to not involve in that. It's not good.

Anne:         When were you involved in gangs?

Javier:         When I was 13 years old.

Anne:         Here in Mexico?

Javier:         Yes. Because a lot of... Where I live, actually where I live it's called Tenancingo. In Delaware, there's a small place... well, not too small... that they call them Tenancingo Chiquito, small Tenancingo because there's a lot of people from Tenancingo living in there.

Anne:         In Delaware?

Javier:         Yeah.

Anne:        In the States?

Javier:         Yeah. A lot of neighbors that been deported that went and came back and everything like that, so they were in gangs. So I was a young kid with nothing else to do than study and do... how to say it... I don't know. Can we use just a small, bad word?

Javier:         Okay. Stupid things. I was a small kid just studying and doing stupid things, and I decided to get into the gang. There was a lot, a lot of Sureños. How you said it? Southside 13 gang members. And it was like, "If you want to be here, you got to join us." And I was like, "No. Don't want to do." I was like-

I only give some like sadness to my family if I keep here, so let's quit it."
